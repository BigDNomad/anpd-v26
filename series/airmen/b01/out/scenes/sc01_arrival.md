The ramp came down and the heat came up, a physical thing, like stepping into the exhaust of something enormous. Archer got his duffel off the deck and walked down into it. The air tasted of jet fuel and something underneath the jet fuel that was older and wetter and had nothing to do with the base. The jungle started at the perimeter wire and it didn't stop.

Nakhon Phanom sat on the Mekong's western bank and looked across the river into Laos, and even here, even on the tarmac, you could feel the weight of what was over there. Archer had been told this by no one. He felt it anyway.

A jeep was parked at the base of the ramp. The man leaning against the hood had his arms crossed and his boots crossed at the ankle and he was watching Archer come down the ramp the way a man watches something he's already made a judgment about. He was lean in the way that wasn't about being fit. It was about having had things burned off him. His face was the color of the teak furniture Archer had seen in the Bangkok transit lounge, and it had about as much give to it.

Archer reached the tarmac and the man didn't move.

"Are you my pup?" the man said.

Archer set the duffel down. "Archer. Danny Archer."

"I know your name." The man pushed off the hood. "Silas Vance. I asked if you were my pup."

Archer looked at him. "I guess that depends on what you mean by it."

"It means you're new and I'm not," Vance said. "It means you've got training and I've got experience and those are not the same thing. It means for the next however long it takes, you do what I tell you and you don't do what I don't tell you and at the end of it you'll either be worth something or you won't." He picked up Archer's duffel without being asked and dropped it in the back of the jeep. "That's what I mean by it."

"Then yes," Archer said. "I'm your pup."

Vance looked at him for a moment. Not assessing — he'd already done that. Something else. Confirming.

"Get in," he said.

The jeep moved off the flight line and onto a service road running parallel to the runway. A C-130 was turning at the far end, its props throwing heat shimmer across the concrete. Archer watched it and said nothing. Vance drove with one hand on the wheel and the other resting on his thigh, and he drove the way he stood — like a man who had decided some time ago that urgency was a thing you spent carefully.

"How long you been in?" Vance said.

"Four years."

"How long a PJ?"

"Eighteen months qualified."

"Stateside rescues?"

"Two. One training exercise that went real."

Vance nodded. He didn't say whether that was enough or not enough. The road curved away from the runway and toward a cluster of low buildings set back from the main base infrastructure. The buildings were the color of the ground they sat on. There were no signs.

"You know what we do here," Vance said. It wasn't a question.

"CSAR. Cross-border."

"You know what cross-border means."

"Laos," Archer said. "Officially we're not there."

"Officially we're not anywhere," Vance said. "You go down over there, there's no official rescue. There's us." He pulled the jeep to a stop in front of the nearest building and killed the engine. The heat settled back in immediately. "The men we go after — pilots, aircrew, the occasional other — they're alive when we get there or they're not. Our job is to make sure that when they're alive, they stay that way long enough for us to bring them home. Everything else is secondary."

"Everything else," Archer said.

"Everything." Vance got out of the jeep. He didn't elaborate.

Archer climbed out and stood in the heat and looked at the building. There was a screen door and behind it a darkness that the light hadn't gotten into yet. From somewhere beyond the perimeter wire came a sound that might have been birds or might have been something else, and then it stopped.

Vance was already at the door.

"There are rules here," he said, not turning around. "I'll tell you the ones that matter. The ones I don't tell you, you'll figure out. The ones you don't figure out—" He pulled the screen door open. "Well. That's what the pup part is for."

He went inside.

Archer picked up his duffel from the back of the jeep. The jungle smell was stronger here, away from the flight line. It came off the tree line in the heat like something breathing. He stood there one moment with the duffel in his hand and the screen door swinging shut ahead of him, and then he followed Vance inside.
