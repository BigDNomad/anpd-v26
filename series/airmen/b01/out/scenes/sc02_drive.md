The jeep was already moving when Archer got his duffel squared in the back. Vance drove the way he did everything else — without wasted motion, one hand on the wheel, eyes on the road ahead. The base spread out around them in the afternoon heat, low buildings and red laterite dust and the smell of aviation fuel hanging in the air the way it always did near a flight line. Archer watched the perimeter fence slide past and said nothing.

Vance spoke first.

"Two years," he said. "That's the program. You don't get on a live mission without supervision until I say you're ready, and I don't say you're ready until you've shown me you can do the work without me holding your hand. Could be two years. Could be longer."

Archer kept his eyes forward. "Understood."

"You've got eighteen months qualified. I know what that means and I know what it doesn't mean. What it doesn't mean is that you know how to work in this environment, with this crew, on this kind of mission. The pipeline here is different. The margin is different."

He turned onto a narrower track between two long buildings, the jeep's suspension working over the ruts.

"You'll start on the hoist. Every evolution, every drill, until the mechanics are automatic. You won't be thinking about the hoist when you're going down through a triple-canopy jungle with people shooting at you. You'll be thinking about the survivor. The hoist has to be nothing. It has to be like breathing."

Archer nodded.

"Medical protocols are the same. You know them. You'll know them better. Vance doesn't run a crew that improvises on a survivor because someone forgot their trauma sequence under stress. You'll run the protocols until they're reflex."

The jeep slowed for a Thai airman crossing the track, then accelerated again.

"The crew," Vance said. "Dalton is your crew chief. What Dalton tells you about the aircraft, you listen. Evans handles comms and navigation. Kowalski is your other PJ — he's been here fourteen months and he knows the work. You don't outrank him in this aircraft regardless of what your service record says. Miller handles the guns. Motherway is your copilot when I'm not flying. You'll learn what each of them needs from you and you'll provide it without being asked."

He said it the way a man might read off a parts list. No inflection, no invitation to comment.

"The mission is CSAR. Combat search and rescue. You know the definition. Out here the definition means something specific. We go into Laos. We go in without official acknowledgment, without official support, and without the option of calling for help if things go wrong. If we go down, there is no rescue package coming for us. You understand that before you understand anything else."

Archer understood it. He'd been told versions of it before, in briefings, in the pipeline, by men who'd rotated through. But hearing Vance say it in that flat, unhurried voice, with the Mekong somewhere behind them and Laos sitting right there across the water, it landed differently. Not fear. Something more specific than fear — the recognition that the distance between a briefing and the actual thing was exactly the distance they were currently crossing.

"The survivor is the mission," Vance said. "Not the aircraft. Not the crew. The survivor. You go down the hoist because someone is alive down there and your job is to keep them that way until we can get them out. Everything else is secondary to that. If you can't hold that in your head when it gets bad, you tell me now and we find you something else to do."

Archer said, "I can hold it."

Vance looked at him once, briefly, then back at the road.

"Rule one," he said.

He didn't say it like he was building to something. He said it the way a man states a fact about weather.

"Don't fuck up."

No elaboration. No list of what fucking up looked like or how to avoid it. The rule was the rule and it sat there in the heat between them and Archer didn't ask him to expand on it because there was nothing to expand. The margin Vance was describing wasn't a margin that accommodated explanation. A man on a hoist over the jungle either had the thing or he didn't, and the men who didn't were already dead or they were somewhere else, and either way they weren't here.

Vance pulled the jeep to a stop in front of a low building, indistinguishable from the others except for the number stenciled above the door. He shut the engine off and sat for a moment with his hands in his lap.

"Get your gear," he said.

Archer pulled the duffel from the back. The heat came off the ground in visible waves. Somewhere on the flight line a reciprocating engine turned over and caught.

Vance was already at the door.
