The Black Widow came off its firing pass and climbed back into the dark, the twin jets pushing hard, and Coyle trimmed her out and let the aircraft find its level. Below, the Trail was burning in two places. The sensor operator called it good and the gunners were already talking about something else.

Coyle reached into his left breast pocket and took out the block.

Basswood, half the size of his fist, maybe a third of the way toward something. He wasn't sure yet what it wanted to be. He'd learned not to decide too early. The folding knife came out of his right pocket and he opened it one-handed against his thigh, the blade locking with a sound he'd heard so many times it didn't register anymore.

He flew with his knees. It wasn't regulation. It wasn't something he'd taught himself so much as something that had accumulated over years of long sorties, the aircraft becoming an extension of a man's body the way a good tool does, until the distinction between operating it and simply being in it started to blur. The Black Widow was a stable platform. She wanted to fly straight. He let her.

The knife moved in small strokes. Curl of pale wood fell away into his lap.

On the intercom the co-pilot was telling one of the gunners about a bar in Udon Thani, a specific disagreement about the price of something, the kind of story that had no real ending and didn't need one. The gunner said something back. The sensor operator laughed. Coyle let it run. The chatter was the sound of men filling the space between the work, and the work out here was long and the space was longer, and there was nothing wrong with it. He'd been listening to versions of this conversation for years. The names changed. The bars changed. The disagreement was always about the price of something.

He checked his instruments. Altitude, heading, fuel state. Everything where it should be.

The Trail sortie had been clean. Three passes, the 20-millimeter doing what it was built to do, the sensor operator threading the truck park through the targeting system with the kind of quiet efficiency that didn't require comment. Coyle had flown the passes and the crew had done their work and now they were going home. That was the whole of it. Most nights were like this. The ones that weren't, you knew it early.

He took another stroke with the knife. The wood was soft and cooperative and he worked the grain without fighting it.

The co-pilot finished his story. One of the gunners said something about the sensor operator's mother that was anatomically creative and the sensor operator said he'd heard better from a Pathet Lao prisoner and the co-pilot said that was probably true. Coyle didn't say anything. He didn't need to. They weren't talking to him, exactly. They were talking around him the way men talk around a fixed point, something that doesn't move and doesn't need to be addressed directly. He'd been that fixed point long enough that he didn't think about it anymore.

The knife moved. The block turned in his fingers.

He'd started whittling on a long transit out of Vientiane, years back, a different aircraft and a different war in the sense that every year of it was a different war while remaining exactly the same war. Someone had left a block of wood on the jump seat and a folding knife beside it and Coyle had picked them up without thinking much about it and by the time they landed he'd roughed out something that might have been a bird. He'd kept the knife. He'd kept the habit.

It wasn't meditation. He didn't think of it that way. It was more that his hands needed something to do when his mind was working, or when his mind was deliberately not working, which amounted to the same thing at altitude over a country that didn't officially exist on any map he was officially flying over.

The co-pilot called out a waypoint. Coyle acknowledged it and adjusted heading two degrees and the Black Widow moved obediently under his hands, the knees coming off the yoke, the knife folding back into his pocket, the block going back into his breast pocket against his chest.

Below them the karst was invisible in the dark. He knew it was there. He always knew it was there. The limestone ridges running north to south, the jungle packed into every valley, the Trail threading through it all like something alive. He'd been looking at it long enough that he could read it the way you read a face, the geography of it familiar and indifferent in equal measure.

The sensor operator called the fuel state. Coyle ran the numbers and told him they were fine.

The chatter started up again. Something about Udon Thani again, a different bar this time, the same disagreement about the price of something.

Coyle reached back into his pocket and took out the block.

The knife opened against his thigh.

The Black Widow flew on toward the Mekong.
