The Black Widow flew at eight thousand feet with her running lights off and her engines quiet against the dark. Below, the karst rose in broken columns through the jungle canopy, and the Trail threaded between them the way water finds its level — not straight, never straight, but always moving.

Coyle had the knife folded and the wood block in his breast pocket. Both hands on the yoke now.

"Contact," said the sensor operator. His name was Briggs and he'd been on the Trail long enough that his voice didn't change when he found something. "Black Crow's got ignition signatures. Multiple sources. Moving south."

Coyle looked out the left side of the canopy. Nothing visible to the naked eye. The jungle was black on black, the karst shapes darker than the sky above them.

"NOD," he said.

The night observation device picked them up in the green-washed image that fed to Coyle's secondary display — a thread of pale light moving through the dark below, the headlights of the convoy spaced unevenly along a switchback section of road, the lead truck maybe a kilometer ahead of the last. He counted the lights. Seven, eight, maybe ten vehicles. The spacing told him they weren't worried. They'd been running this road long enough to feel comfortable on it.

That was their problem.

"How many you make it?" Coyle said.

"Black Crow's reading nine distinct signatures," Briggs said. "Could be more running dark. Lead element's about four klicks south of the last waypoint."

"Fire control."

The gunner — a staff sergeant named Paulson who'd been on the aircraft longer than anyone except the airframe itself — acknowledged and began working the targeting solution. The AC-119K carried three M134 miniguns in a side-firing configuration, and the fire-control system took the sensor data from Black Crow and the NOD and translated it into a firing solution that accounted for airspeed, altitude, and the geometry of the orbit. The guns didn't point at where the trucks were. They pointed at where the trucks would be.

The crew worked without conversation. Each man on his station, each man doing the thing he'd done enough times that it didn't require thought. That was the point of all of it — the training, the repetition, the hours in the seat until the procedures became reflexive. You didn't think your way through a fire-control sequence at night over karst at eight thousand feet. You'd already thought through it. Now you just did it.

Coyle put the aircraft into a left bank and began the orbit. The Trail was below and to his left, the convoy still threading south, still unaware. They had no radar. No early warning. The Black Widow was passive — Black Crow didn't emit, it only received, pulling in the electromagnetic signatures that leaked from every truck engine like heat from a stove. The convoy below was broadcasting its own location to anyone with the equipment to hear it, and they didn't know it, and there was nothing to be done about that now.

"Firing solution set," Paulson said.

"Stand by," Coyle said.

He held the orbit, watching the NOD display, the thread of lights moving at the pace of men who believed themselves safe. The lead truck was on a straight section now, the road running along a ridgeline with jungle dropping away on both sides. Good geometry. The trucks behind it were bunching slightly on the approach to the straight — the accordion effect you always got with convoys on mountain roads, the drivers in back not seeing the road open up ahead.

Coyle watched it for another few seconds.

"Cleared hot," he said.

The miniguns opened up and the aircraft shuddered with the sustained vibration of six thousand rounds per minute cycling through three barrels, the sound less like gunfire than like something tearing, a long continuous rip in the fabric of the night. The tracers didn't arc — they went nearly straight down in the geometry of the orbit, a solid line of light connecting the aircraft to the road below.

The lead truck went first. Then the second. On the NOD display the convoy came apart — vehicles stopping, some pulling off the road, one of them going over the edge of the ridgeline in a slow tumble that ended in a secondary explosion when the fuel caught. The Black Crow signatures were going erratic as engines died or drivers killed their ignitions, too late, the way men always did things too late when the thing they feared arrived without warning.

Coyle held the orbit through two more passes. Paulson worked the guns in controlled bursts, walking the fire down the length of the convoy.

When it was done the road below held seven fires and two vehicles that had made it into the tree line. The Black Crow showed four remaining signatures, stationary.

"Cease fire," Coyle said.

He rolled the aircraft out of the orbit and put the nose toward the Mekong. Behind him the fires on the Trail were already shrinking with distance, becoming indistinct from the stars.

He reached into his breast pocket and took out the wood block and the knife.
