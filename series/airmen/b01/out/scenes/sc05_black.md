Coyle brought the left wing down and let the Black Widow settle into the pylon turn, two thousand feet above the valley floor. The karst rose on both sides of the corridor, black against black, and the convoy below was still moving. He could see the headlights — some of them, the ones the drivers hadn't killed yet — threading south through the trees.

"Briggs," he said.

"Four vehicles still showing signatures. Moving slow. They don't know we're here."

That was the thing about the Black Crow. It didn't care what the trucks knew. It just listened to their engines and told you where they were.

Coyle held the bank angle and felt the aircraft settle into the geometry. The pylon turn was the whole trick of it — keep the target fixed in the orbit, let the fire-control system do the math, and the guns would walk right down the column. He'd done it enough times that his hands knew the corrections before his mind did.

"Paulson."

"Weapons live," Paulson said. "All three. Fire-control linked."

"NOD?"

"I've got them. Four vehicles, maybe a fifth in the trees. Troops on the road. Looks like they stopped to check the damage from the first pass."

That was their mistake. Stopping.

Coyle tilted the aircraft another degree, watching the targeting reticle in the side window drift across the road below. The reticle was a simple thing — a circle of light etched into the glass — and when the geometry was right it sat over the target like a finger pointing. He waited. The turn held. The reticle drifted and then stopped drifting and sat where it needed to sit.

He squeezed the trigger.

All three miniguns opened at once.

The sound was not a sound so much as a physical event — a tearing, a sustained mechanical shriek that came up through the airframe and into his hands and his seat and his back teeth. Six thousand rounds a minute, times three. The tracers went down in a solid red line, not individual rounds but a stream, a rope of fire from the aircraft to the road below, and where the rope touched the road the road came apart.

The first truck went immediately. The fuel tank caught and the whole vehicle lifted slightly before it settled back and began to burn, and the fire threw light across the road and the trees and the soldiers who had been standing in the road and were no longer standing.

Coyle held the bank. The pylon turn kept the stream on the column.

The second vehicle was a personnel carrier of some kind and it burned slower, the fire working from the cab back, and then something in the bed cooked off — ammunition, probably, or an RPG — and the secondary explosion threw debris across the road and into the trees on both sides. He could see figures running in the light from the fires. The miniguns found them.

"Two more," Paulson said. His voice was flat, professional. "Treeline, left side."

Coyle adjusted the bank by two degrees. The reticle moved. He held it and squeezed again and the guns walked into the trees, and one of the vehicles there caught immediately — a fuel truck, by the size of the fireball — and the fireball lit the whole valley for a moment, the karst walls going orange and then fading back to black as the initial flash died and the sustained burn took over.

The fourth vehicle tried to move. He could see it on the road, pulling forward, trying to get clear of the burning trucks ahead of it. Paulson was already tracking it.

"I've got him," Paulson said.

The guns shifted and the vehicle stopped moving.

Coyle held the turn for another thirty seconds, watching the road below. The fires were burning well now — three on the road, two in the trees, and the fuel truck still going in the treeline, throwing light and smoke up into the dark. Nothing was moving that he could see. The Black Crow was quiet.

He rolled the wings level and brought the nose around.

Below him the valley held its fires. The road was blocked in two places and the trees on the left side were burning where the fuel truck had gone up, and the smoke was rising in columns that he could see against the stars. Whatever had been moving south on that road was not moving anymore.

He reached into his breast pocket and found the wood block and the knife. Opened the blade with his thumb and took a thin curl off the corner of the block, just to have something to do with his hands.

"Briggs," he said. "Any signatures?"

"Negative. Nothing moving."

"Paulson. Damage assessment."

"Seven fires confirmed. Four vehicles destroyed on the road. Two in the treeline. Troops in the open, unknown number." A pause. "They're not moving."

Coyle closed the knife and put it back in his pocket.

He turned the Black Widow north and climbed.
