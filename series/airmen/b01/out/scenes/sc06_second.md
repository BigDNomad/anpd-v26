The survivors came off the road fast, breaking for the treeline in ones and twos, and then the AK fire started — scattered, undisciplined, muzzle flashes winking up from the dark below like something trying to signal him. An RPD joined in, its cyclic rate distinct from the Kalashnikovs, a lower, heavier stutter. Coyle heard none of it through the airframe. He knew it was there the way you know weather is coming — by the instruments, by the way Paulson's voice changed on the intercom.

"Taking small arms. Low and behind. They're not close."

"Noted," Coyle said.

He held the pylon turn, the Black Widow banking hard left, the valley floor rotating beneath them in the NOD's green wash. The fires from the first pass were still going — four on the road, two in the treeline, the fuel truck a column of light that made the NOD bloom and wash out if you looked at it directly. The remaining Black Crow signatures were still active. Four vehicles, stationary, engines running. Somebody down there hadn't gotten the word yet, or couldn't move, or was waiting to see what came next.

What came next was the second pass.

"Targets at your nine," Briggs said. "Stationary. Two trucks, looks like a personnel carrier. Fourth signature is weak — might be the engine cooling."

"Paulson."

"Ready."

Coyle tightened the bank and the miniguns opened up, three streams of fire walking down from altitude in a line that looked almost lazy from the cockpit, the way tracer rounds always looked lazy until you understood the geometry. The personnel carrier went first — it was the largest return and Paulson knew his business. Secondary explosion, small, probably ammunition in the cargo bed. The two trucks came apart under sustained fire, one of them rolling off the road embankment and catching in the brush below, burning sideways. The fourth signature went dark.

"Troops in the open," Paulson said. "Thirty meters east of the road. Moving."

"Take them."

The miniguns traversed. Coyle held the bank angle and watched the NOD and did not think about what was happening on the ground below him, because thinking about it was not the job. The job was the pylon turn, the altitude, the fuel state, the heading home. The job was keeping the aircraft in the geometry that let Paulson do his work. Everything else was someone else's accounting.

"Clear," Paulson said.

Coyle rolled out, climbed two hundred feet, and came back around for the third pass.

The valley below was a different place now than it had been twenty minutes ago. The road was blocked in two places — burning hulks that nothing was going to move before daylight, and maybe not then. The fires had spread to the brush on the eastern shoulder, a line of orange working its way uphill through dry scrub. Briggs called no active Black Crow signatures. Paulson's NOD showed no movement that wasn't fire.

"Anything?" Coyle said.

"Negative," Briggs said. "Road's cold."

Paulson said nothing for a moment. Then: "One figure. North end of the column. Not moving."

"Leave it," Coyle said.

He made the third pass anyway, low and slow, the miniguns silent, Paulson and Briggs working their instruments and calling what they saw. Two vehicles burning to the frame. One vehicle inverted in the ditch, no fire, no signatures. The personnel carrier was a gutted shell. The fuel truck was still going, a sustained burn that would be visible from altitude for hours, a marker on the Trail that the Trail's owners would have to explain to someone in the morning.

No movement. No return fire. The AKs had stopped.

Coyle rolled wings-level and held the heading for a moment, looking at what they'd done. Nine vehicles confirmed, possibly more. The road blocked. The cargo — whatever it was, rice or ammunition or fuel or men — not going south tonight, not going south in any of those trucks ever again. The Black Crow had done its work. Paulson had done his work. The Black Widow had done what it was built to do.

He checked the fuel state. The margin was tightening. Not critical, but not a number you ignored.

"Time to go," Coyle said.

He broke the orbit and turned the aircraft west, climbing, the engines pushing hard as the Black Widow came up through the dark air above the valley floor. The karst ridgelines rose ahead of them, black shapes against a slightly less black sky, the border country between Laos and Thailand that wasn't a border in any way that mattered to the men who flew it. He climbed for the ridgelines and held the heading and let the aircraft find its altitude.

Behind them the fires were still burning. They would burn for a long time. The valley would smell of it for longer than that, but Coyle was already past the valley, already past the accounting, the Black Widow climbing west toward the Mekong and the base and whatever came next.
