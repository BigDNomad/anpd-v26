The radar-warning receiver lit up at 0214.

Not a search tone. Not the slow pulse of something sweeping the sky looking for targets. This was the fast, hard chirp of a missile guidance radar that had already found what it was looking for.

Coyle had the wood block in his left hand and the knife in his right when it hit. He dropped both into his breast pocket in the same motion he used to take the yoke.

"Missile launch," the co-pilot said. His name was Harwick and he was twenty-three years old and he said it the way you'd say *check engine light* — flat, informational, already moving to his own panel.

The Black Widow was at four thousand feet, climbing westward, the Mekong still forty miles out. Below them the valley was dark except for the fires they'd left, seven orange points shrinking behind the aircraft like embers cooling in a grate.

Coyle rolled left hard.

The SA-2 was a telephone pole with a rocket motor and a radar seeker and it did not care about the fires or the valley or the forty miles to the Mekong. It cared about the electromagnetic return from the Black Widow's airframe and it was already in the air.

"Chaff," Coyle said.

Harwick hit the dispenser. The aircraft shuddered slightly as the bundles went out, aluminum strips tumbling into the slipstream to bloom into false returns on the missile's seeker head.

In the back, Briggs had already killed his active emitters. The Black Crow sensors went dark. Whatever signatures they'd been painting the valley with, they were gone now, the aircraft going as quiet electronically as it could manage while still flying. He didn't need to be told. None of them did.

Paulson swung the NOD away from the gun port and pressed himself against the fuselage skin, looking aft and down, trying to find the missile's exhaust plume in the dark. The miniguns were useless here. He knew that. He looked anyway.

The warning tone changed pitch.

Coyle pushed the nose down and rolled right, reversing the turn, trading altitude for airspeed. The Black Widow was not a nimble aircraft. It was a cargo plane with guns bolted to its side and two turbofan engines that gave it enough speed to be somewhere else but not enough to be somewhere else *quickly*. What it had was a pilot who'd been flying it long enough to know exactly how much it would give him and exactly where that ended.

He held the dive.

The karst ridgeline that had hidden the SAM site was behind them now, the missile climbing from the eastern slope of a limestone formation that didn't appear on any chart he was officially using. Someone had positioned that launcher well. Set it back from the ridgeline crest so the radar had a clean acquisition window on the valley's western approach. They'd been waiting. Not for the Black Widow specifically, maybe. But for something like it.

The tone cut out.

Harwick said, "Lost lock."

Coyle held the dive for another three seconds, then pulled back on the yoke and leveled off at two thousand feet. The trees below were close enough that Paulson, looking down through the gun port, could see individual canopy crowns in the moonlight.

Nobody said anything for a moment.

The warning receiver was quiet. The chaff cloud was somewhere behind them, drifting, a ghost of the aircraft that had passed through. Whether the missile had gone for it or simply lost acquisition in the terrain clutter, Coyle didn't know and it didn't matter. What mattered was the tone was gone.

"Second launch possible," Harwick said. "They had time to reload if they're a full battery."

"I know."

Coyle kept the aircraft low and pushed the throttles forward. At two thousand feet the karst worked against the radar, the limestone formations breaking up the ground return and making it harder to hold a track. It also meant that if something went wrong — an engine, a control surface, anything — there was no altitude to work with. You were in the trees before you finished the thought.

He flew west.

The fires in the valley were gone from sight now, the ridgeline between them and the burning road. Somewhere back there, in the dark, whatever was left of the convoy was stopped and burning and the men who'd survived were doing what men do after something like that — checking themselves for wounds, looking for their officers, trying to understand what had just happened to them.

And somewhere on that eastern ridgeline, a SAM crew was watching their radar screen and deciding whether to light it up again.

Coyle kept the aircraft low and fast and said nothing. His hands were steady on the yoke. The wood block was in his breast pocket and the knife was folded and the Mekong was thirty-eight miles out and the warning receiver was quiet.

He flew west.
