The second missile came from the south.

Paulson saw it first and got one word out — *break* — before it detonated forty feet off the starboard wing.

The sound was not a sound. It was a pressure event, a wall of compressed air that hit the fuselage like a fist closing around it, and then the shrapnel arrived in the same instant, a horizontal rain of steel moving faster than thought. It punched through the skin of the aircraft in a dozen places. It found Briggs at the sensor station and it found Paulson at the gun mount and it found the co-pilot's side of the cockpit where Harwick sat with his hands on the yoke.

Coyle felt the fragment enter his right thigh before he heard anything. A hot iron driven deep into the muscle, no pain yet, just the fact of it. He looked down and saw the tear in his flight suit and the dark spreading through the fabric and he put his hand on it and pressed and kept flying.

The aircraft yawed hard right. He corrected with left rudder and felt the pedal go soft under his boot, something in the linkage damaged or gone, and he worked with what he had.

"Harwick."

Nothing from the right seat.

He looked. Harwick was slumped against the side window, his helmet touching the glass, both hands still on the yoke by reflex or by death. Coyle could not tell which. He reached across and checked the man's neck with two fingers and felt a pulse, faint and fast, and that was all he had time for.

The fire warning light for the starboard wing came on. Then the port wing light. Then the master caution panel lit up like a carnival and he stopped reading it because there was nothing on it that changed what he had to do.

He keyed the interphone. "Briggs. Paulson. Anyone back there."

Static. Then a voice he didn't recognize, one of the gunners, saying something that wasn't words, a sound a man makes when he is trying to report and cannot.

The aircraft was descending. He had not put it in a descent. He checked the throttles — both engines still running, both turbofans putting out power — and he realized the trim had gone and the nose was hunting and the controls were sluggish in a way that meant something structural had been compromised. He pulled back on the yoke and got partial response and arrested the descent at eighteen hundred feet.

Thirty-six miles to the Mekong.

The starboard wing was burning. Not a small fire. He could see the orange light of it reflected in the windscreen and he could see the glow intensifying as the fuel in the wing tank fed it. The AC-119K carried its fuel in the wings and in the center section and there was nothing between what was burning now and what would burn next except a few seconds and the laws of physics, which were not going to make an exception for him.

He thought about the crew. He thought about Harwick beside him breathing in shallow pulls. He thought about whoever was still alive in the back of the aircraft, in the dark with the smoke and the torn metal and the guns that would never fire again tonight.

He reached up and hit the bailout alarm. The bell was loud and continuous and it meant what it meant.

He did not know if anyone in the back was in a condition to hear it or act on it. He hit it anyway. That was the protocol and the protocol was the only thing left.

The yoke shuddered in his hands. The aircraft was telling him something and it was telling him now. He looked at the altimeter — sixteen hundred feet — and looked at the terrain below, which was karst and jungle and darkness, no flat ground, no roads, nothing that a man could call a landing strip even in daylight with a functioning aircraft.

He reached across and unlatched Harwick's harness and then his own. He got his hand under Harwick's arm and pulled the man toward him and Harwick made a sound, which meant he was alive, which was the only thing that mattered in that moment.

The starboard wing tank let go.

The explosion was not loud from inside the cockpit. It was a concussive thump and then a roar and then the aircraft rolled hard right and the nose came up and Coyle was fighting the yoke with everything he had and losing. The altimeter was unwinding. The fire was everywhere on the right side now, the whole wing a torch, and the center section fuel was next.

He got Harwick's parachute handle into the man's right hand and closed the fingers around it.

"Pull that," Coyle said. "You hear me. Pull that."

He did not know if Harwick heard him.

He found the door handle and hit it and the slipstream took the door and the night came in cold and loud and full of fire-light, and Coyle went out into it with his hand pressed hard against his thigh.
