He didn't bail when the wing went. That was the rule, and it wasn't written anywhere because it didn't need to be. You stayed at the controls until you couldn't. The aircraft was the shell. The crew was the cargo.

Harwick was already gone, the ejection seat having taken him up and out through the overhead hatch in a single violent second, and Coyle had watched it happen from the corner of his eye the way you watch something you've already decided to let go of. The Black Widow was coming apart around him. The starboard wing was burning back to the root. The trim was gone and the nose wanted to drop and he was letting it, managing the drop, riding the angle down through sixteen hundred feet of dark air over terrain that would kill a man on a good day.

He had maybe forty seconds.

He used them.

The valley floor was invisible below him, jungle canopy and karst and whatever the dark held, and he held the yoke with both hands now and kept the wings as level as he could keep them, which wasn't level at all, and he thought about Briggs and Paulson back in the fuselage and didn't think about them again because there was nothing left to think. The fires from the convoy were still burning somewhere behind him to the east, orange smears in the dark, and the Black Widow was descending at an angle that was going to put her into the slope of the karst ridge rather than the valley floor, and he made the calculation without making it, the way you make calculations when there's no time for calculation, and he held the nose up just enough to bleed the speed, just enough to keep the impact from being purely vertical.

The canopy hit first.

The sound was not like anything he had a word for. Not an explosion. Not a crash in the way the word meant anything. It was the sound of a large machine being unmade by the world, continuous and total, the jungle taking the Black Widow apart the way the jungle took everything apart, just faster. The nose punched through the upper canopy and the fuselage flexed and the starboard wing sheared off against a karst outcrop and the whole aircraft yawed hard right and the cockpit filled with green and black and the smell of fuel and burning and then the slope caught the belly and the Black Widow began to shed itself, structure peeling away in sections, the tail separating somewhere behind him, the fuselage splitting along a seam that ran from the cargo door forward, and Coyle was inside all of it, strapped to his seat, and the seat was moving with the fuselage and the fuselage was moving with the slope and the slope was winning.

He had his arms crossed over his face.

The aircraft stopped.

Not all at once. It stopped the way a wave stops, energy dissipating forward through the wreckage until the last piece of it came to rest against something that wouldn't move. A limestone formation, probably. The karst was everywhere up here. It stopped things.

Coyle sat in the dark for a moment. The cockpit was open to the sky on the right side where the fuselage had split. He could see stars through the gap in the canopy above him. The instrument panel was dark except for one gauge that was still lit, the fuel flow indicator, which read zero, which was correct.

He took inventory. His hands were there. He moved them. His legs were there. The right one had the shrapnel in it and the shrapnel was still in it and the leg still worked, which was what mattered. His head had hit something during the impact and there was blood on his face from somewhere above his left eye, but the eye worked and the other eye worked and he could hear, which meant his eardrums were intact, which meant the impact hadn't been as bad as it could have been.

He unstrapped.

The harness release took two tries because his hands weren't working quite right yet, some residual shock in the fingers, and then he was free of the seat and he stood up in the wreckage of the cockpit and looked at what was left of the Black Widow.

She had come down the slope for maybe two hundred yards. Pieces of her were distributed through the jungle behind him, visible as dark shapes against the darker jungle, some of them still smoking. The tail section was gone. The cargo bay was open to the sky, split along its length, and he could see into it from where he stood and there was nothing moving in there.

He stood there a moment longer than he needed to.

Then he climbed out of the wreckage and stood on the slope in the dark jungle of a country that didn't exist and listened to the fires burning in the canopy above him and the distant sound of the valley below, and he reached into his breast pocket and the wood block was still there, and the knife.

He was alive.

He needed to stay that way.
