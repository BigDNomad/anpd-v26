The night air at NKP was still and warm and smelled of aviation fuel and cut grass from the runway margins. Archer sat on the concrete step outside the field hospital with the wooden bird in his right hand, turning it in the light that came through the window above him. The carving was four inches long and unfinished, the tail feathers only roughed in, the beak not yet separated from the breast. Coyle had worked it through the whole of it — through the holding area and the corridor and the Claymore detonations and the hoist and the Mekong — and now it was here, and Coyle was inside under sedation with his leg in a half-cast and his arm wrapped shoulder to wrist, and the bird was in Archer's hands instead.

He did not think about sleep. That was the thing that was different, though he would not have said so. Before the first scramble he had lain on his bunk and stared at the ceiling and counted the hours. Now he sat on the step and held the bird and watched the tarmac and there was nothing in him that needed to be managed.

Ninety hours. That was the number. Two years of pipeline — the indoctrination, the water work, the jump school, the paramedic rotations, the jungle adaptation course — and the ninety hours had been the end of it, the thing that finished the making. Not a metaphor. The forge itself. You did not come out the other side the same man who went in, and the difference was not something you could explain to anyone who had not been in the corridor with the Claymores going and the napalm rolling through the tree line and Coyle's leg bent wrong and Vance bleeding through his hand.

Tran was alive. Archer knew that the way you know a thing you cannot prove. The NVA captain had taken a round and gone into the darkness and the darkness had kept him. He was wounded and moving and he had his detail with him, and somewhere north of the Mekong he was putting distance between himself and what had happened at the camp. He would surface again. That was the nature of men like Tran — they did not finish, they withdrew and reconstituted and came back at a different angle. The round Archer had put in him was a debt, not a settlement.

Bounchanh was still in Khammouane. That was his province and his karst and his war, and two Claymores and a napalm strike had not changed that. He had seven hundred steel balls in a sixty-degree arc and he had walked away from it. He had three men left and he was forcing them south at gunpoint and he knew the terrain the way a man knows his own house in the dark. The camp was gone. The command tent was ash. The supply stack was a crater. But Bounchanh was not the camp. He was the ground itself, and the ground remained.

Vance came out through the door behind him and sat down on the step without saying anything. His thigh was bandaged under his flight suit and he moved carefully but he sat straight. They stayed like that for a while.

"Coyle's in surgery," Vance said.

"Dalton told me."

"He'll make it."

"I know."

Vance looked at the bird in Archer's hand. He did not ask about it.

What had been spent was considerable. Two Claymores. A napalm strike. Sandy's ordnance load over the camp. Vance's blood on the floor of the Jolly Green. The holes in the fuselage skin. Meat sitting in the cabin staring at nothing since the Mekong, somewhere inside himself that no one could reach yet. The second American in the holding area, the one with the broken leg, still in the tarp structure when the air strikes came — Archer did not let himself think about that one too long or too directly, but it was there, the way a missing tooth is there, the absence you keep finding with your tongue.

What had been taken was Coyle, alive, which was the mission. That others may live. The creed was not a sentiment. It was a description of the arithmetic.

What was left behind was the man who could not sleep before a scramble. He was back in the corridor somewhere, in the dark between the banyan roots and the first Claymore, and he was not coming out.

Archer turned the bird over in his hand. The grain of the basswood ran clean along the body. Coyle had known what he was making even when it was still a block. You could see it in the way the wings were started — not hacked out but drawn out, the wood removed to reveal what was already there.

He set it on his knee and looked at the tarmac and the dark line of the runway and the lights of the base strung out to the west.

Tran was north of the river.

Bounchanh was in his karst.

The war was where it had always been.
