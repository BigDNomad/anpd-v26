He came back to himself by degrees.

Sound first. A low crackling that he knew before he knew anything else, the way a man knows fire before he opens his eyes. Then the heat, pressing against the right side of his face like a hand. Then pain, arriving all at once and from several directions, and he understood that he had been unconscious because the pain was the kind that would have kept him from it.

He was on his side in the wreckage. Something hard and angular pressed into his ribs. The instrument panel was above him and to the left, which meant the cockpit had rolled or the fuselage had broken and settled wrong. He could not immediately determine which. It did not matter.

He tried to move his right arm and the pain that came back was white and total. He lay still for a moment and breathed through it. The arm was burned from the elbow down, the skin tight and wrong-feeling where he could feel it at all. He moved his left arm instead and it answered, and he used it to push himself up enough to see.

The fire was in the starboard section, climbing the interior framing toward the wing root. The wing itself, or what remained of it, was outside and above him, and the fuel tanks were in the wing. He did not know how much fuel was left in them. He knew it was enough.

Briggs was in the cargo bay. Paulson was in the cargo bay. He could see them both from where he lay and he did not look at them longer than he had to. They were beyond anything he could do for them.

He tried his right leg and it moved. He tried his left leg and it moved but the movement produced a grinding sensation high in the thigh that told him the femur was broken. He filed that information and set it aside.

The comms gear was aft of the cockpit, mounted in the port-side rack. It was maybe twelve feet from where he lay. The fire was on the starboard side and moving aft and upward. He had time. He did not know how much.

He started moving.

He used his left arm and his right leg and dragged himself across the deck, which was canted at an angle that made everything harder. Debris everywhere. A section of conduit. Something that had been a seat. He pushed through it and the broken leg dragged and each time it caught on something the grinding came back and he breathed and kept moving.

The laceration above his eye had opened again. He could feel the blood running into his left eye and he blinked it clear and kept moving.

He reached the port rack and pulled himself up against it with his left arm. The comms gear was intact. The panel was dark but that meant nothing — the aircraft had lost primary power in the descent and the gear ran on backup battery. He found the switch by feel and hit it and the panel came alive, amber and green in the smoke-thickened dark.

The fire was louder now. He could hear it working through the airframe, the sound of metal expanding and giving way.

He keyed the emergency frequency and held the transmit button. His voice came out rougher than he expected.

"Any station, any station, this is Black Widow. We are down. I say again, Black Widow is down." He gave the valley coordinates from memory, the numbers he had been holding in his head since the second missile hit, since he understood what was going to happen. "Crew casualties. One survivor. Request immediate CSAR. Over."

He released the button and listened. Static. The karst was doing what karst did to radio signals, breaking them up, bouncing them wrong. He did not know if anyone had heard him.

He transmitted again. Same message. Same coordinates. He kept his voice level because a panicked transmission was a useless transmission and he had learned that a long time ago, in a different war, in a different country that also didn't officially exist.

The fire reached something in the starboard rack and the light in the cabin changed, brightened, and he felt the heat increase against his right side. The burned arm was past feeling now. That was not a good sign.

He transmitted a third time.

Then he lay against the port rack with the handset in his left hand and looked at the fire working toward the wing root and thought about the fuel in those tanks. He thought about Harwick, who had gone out the hatch somewhere over the karst and might be alive or might not. He thought about Briggs and Paulson and did not think about them long.

He keyed the transmit button one more time and gave the coordinates again, slowly and clearly, and then he released the button and held the handset and listened to the static and watched the fire climb.
