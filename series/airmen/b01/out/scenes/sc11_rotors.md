The KL-7 was bolted to a rack behind the co-pilot's station, or had been. The rack had sheared from its mounts and the machine lay canted against the buckled fuselage wall, its housing cracked along one seam but intact. Coyle got his left hand on it and pulled himself close enough to see.

Too heavy. Forty pounds of chassis, maybe more, and the lower edge had driven itself into a fold of aluminum skin that had closed around it like a fist. He could not move it and he could not carry it and the fire was working its way forward along the starboard side with a sound like paper tearing, steady and patient.

He did not think about it long.

The rotor housing was on the face of the machine, eight individual cipher rotors seated in their numbered slots. He got his fingers under the lip of the first one and pulled. It resisted, then came free with a click that he felt more than heard over the fire. He pocketed it. Pulled the second. The third. His burned right arm was useless below the elbow and he was working with his left hand only, reaching across his own body at an angle that put weight on his fractured leg every time he shifted. The bone moved. He kept pulling rotors.

Four. Five. The sixth one stuck and he worked it side to side until it released. Seven. Eight.

He patted the breast pocket of his flight suit. Eight rotors, each one the size of a large coin, each one worth more to the people looking for this wreck than the chassis they were seated in. The chassis was theirs now. A hunk of aluminum and wiring that would tell them nothing without what he was carrying. He registered this as the best available outcome and did not spend more time on it.

Getting clear of the wreck took longer than it should have. He went out through the gap he'd made coming in, dragging the fractured leg, keeping the burned arm tucked against his chest. The fire was louder outside, the heat more direct, and the smoke had thickened enough that it lay in layers between the trees. He moved away from the wreckage on his hands and one knee, the other leg trailing, pulling himself through undergrowth that caught at his flight suit and tore at the burned skin of his forearm when he misjudged the clearance.

He put thirty feet between himself and the machine. Then he heard the voices.

Lao. Two of them, maybe three, coming from the northeast where the tree line was thickest. Moving toward the fire. He could not see them yet but the voices were not cautious — they were men following smoke, following the obvious, and they were close enough that he could hear the cadence of what they were saying without understanding any of it.

He went flat.

The undergrowth here was low fern and something broader-leafed that he didn't know the name of, and it was not good cover but it was what there was. He pressed himself into it, face down, and did not move. The fractured leg was straight behind him. The burned arm was under his chest and the pressure on it was considerable and he breathed through his nose and did not make a sound.

He got the .38 out of his shoulder holster with his left hand. Cocked it. Held it.

The voices moved closer and then stopped. He heard them talking, shorter exchanges now, and he understood from the tone that they had reached the edge of the wreck site and were looking at it. One of them said something that might have been a question. Another answered. There was a pause.

The fire popped and something in the fuselage gave way with a low metallic groan, and the voices went quiet for a moment and then started again, louder.

Coyle did not move.

He thought about the rotors in his pocket. Eight of them. The chassis was thirty feet away and burning and they were welcome to it. Whatever they pulled from that wreck, they would not pull the thing that mattered. He had denied them that much and it was enough. It was the job, or the part of the job that was left to him now.

The voices moved. He tracked them by sound — they were working around the perimeter of the wreck, not pushing into the jungle. Looking for survivors, maybe. Looking for anything that had been thrown clear. He stayed flat and let them look.

The .38 was in his left hand and he was not a good shot left-handed and there were at least two of them and probably more behind them. He knew the math. He held the gun anyway.

A branch broke somewhere to his left, close enough that he stopped breathing.

Then nothing.

The voices drifted back toward the wreck. He heard them calling to each other, the tone changing — they had found something, or decided something, and whatever it was it was pulling their attention away from the tree line.

He did not move. He waited.
