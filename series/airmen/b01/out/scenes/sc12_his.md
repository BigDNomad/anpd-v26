The radio operator read the coordinates twice and Bounchanh wrote them in his notebook without looking up. He had a system. Province, grid reference, aircraft type where known, time of contact. He drew a small circle on the map with a grease pencil and set the pencil down.

"Confirmed kill?" he said.

"Confirmed. The crew saw it go down on the karst slope. No chutes observed."

He wrote *no chutes* and then crossed it out. The crew had seen what they'd seen in the dark over broken terrain. That meant nothing about chutes.

"Send a patrol to the grid. I want the site before morning."

The radio operator relayed the order and Bounchanh closed his notebook. He stood at the map table in the low building his men had requisitioned from a village that no longer had anyone in it to object. The building had a dirt floor and a corrugated roof and a single kerosene lamp that threw yellow light across the map. The map was French, 1954 vintage, and wrong in several places he had learned by walking the ground himself. He had corrected it in pencil. The corrections were more reliable than the original.

He went to the door and looked out. The valley was dark. Somewhere to the east, past the karst ridgeline, there were fires still burning from the convoy attack. He had heard the guns from here, the distant mechanical tearing sound of the American miniguns working, and then the silence, and then the secondary explosions that meant fuel or ammunition. He had lost nine vehicles and the men with them. He had written that in the notebook too.

But the battery had been waiting on that ridgeline for three weeks. He had positioned them himself, walked the sight lines himself, argued with the battery commander about the acquisition window until the man understood what Bounchanh already knew from the terrain. The valley's western approach was the only clean corridor through the karst at that altitude. Whatever flew that corridor at night was hunting the Trail. And whatever hunted the Trail would eventually come back the same way it had gone in.

He reached into his breast pocket and touched the blue ribbon without taking it out. A habit. He left it where it was.

The notebook was on the table behind him. He did not need to open it to know the count. Eleven aircraft since he had taken command of this province. Two fighters, one reconnaissance plane, three helicopters, four transport aircraft of various types, and now this gunship. The gunship was the most significant. He had seen the AC-119 work before, had watched from a hillside while one turned a column of trucks into burning scrap in under four minutes. They were not fast and they were not nimble but they were patient and they were accurate and they came back. You could not simply hide from them. You had to kill them.

He had killed this one.

He went back inside and looked at the map. The grid the radio operator had given him put the crash site on the karst slope above the valley floor, in the secondary jungle below the limestone outcroppings. Difficult terrain. His patrol would have trouble moving through it at night, but they would move through it anyway because that was what his men did. They knew this ground the way the Americans never would. The Americans flew over it and looked down at it through their instruments and believed that seeing a thing from above was the same as knowing it. It was not the same.

He traced the contour lines with one finger. The slope ran northeast to southwest. If the aircraft had come down on the upper slope the wreckage would be scattered. If it had come down lower, near the valley floor, there might be something intact worth recovering. Either way there would be equipment. There were always cipher machines, navigation equipment, weapons. He had a collection of American survival radios in a crate under this table. He had a .38 revolver taken from a pilot two years ago that he still carried on occasion, not for sentiment but because it was a good weapon and well made.

He thought about the crew. The radio operator had said no chutes, but he had crossed that out. If there were survivors on that slope they would be injured and they would be transmitting. The Americans always transmitted. They had been trained to believe that transmission meant rescue, that if they could only reach their radio someone would come for them. And someone would come. That was the other thing Bounchanh had learned about the Americans: they came back for their people. It was a weakness and a strength at the same time, and he had learned to use it.

He picked up the grease pencil and drew a second circle on the map, larger than the first, centered on the crash grid. The rescue aircraft would come from the west, from Thailand. They would approach through the same corridor.

He wrote the time in his notebook and closed it.

Outside, his men were already moving.
