The smoke column had been visible for twenty minutes before they reached it, rising straight in the still air above the canopy, white at the top where the fuel had burned off and black underneath where the airframe was still going. Bounchanh followed it in.

The wreck had come down on the slope, not in the valley floor, and the jungle had taken most of the impact — trees sheared at chest height, a furrow of torn earth and aluminum running sixty meters before the fuselage had stopped against a limestone outcrop. The tail section was thirty meters back up the slope, separated cleanly. The starboard wing was gone entirely. What remained of the aircraft was burning from the center outward, the fire low and steady now, feeding on insulation and wiring and whatever remained in the fuel lines.

Bounchanh moved through his men without speaking. They had formed a loose perimeter at the tree line, watching the wreck the way men watch something they don't understand. He walked past them.

The heat reached him at fifteen meters. He stopped and looked at what was left.

The fuselage had split along the belly seam and the interior was visible from where he stood, the way a split log shows its grain. Cargo had scattered across the slope — crates, equipment cases, things he couldn't identify at this distance. The fire was concentrated at the wing root and working forward. The cockpit section had separated and lay on its side against the limestone, and that was already burned through, nothing left there worth looking at.

He moved left, circling the wreck at a distance, reading it. The cargo bay was the section that interested him. He had seen American aircraft before, had walked through the wreckage of three of them, and he knew what they carried. Radios. Navigation equipment. Weapons. And sometimes, on the aircraft that flew at night over the Trail, the machines that handled their coded communications.

He saw it through the gap in the fuselage.

The chassis was mounted to what had been the forward bulkhead of the cargo section, still bolted to a rack that had partially separated from the wall. The fire hadn't reached it yet. The smoke was thick around it but the flames were still two meters back, working through a tangle of wiring and insulation. The machine was recognizable by its shape — the rotor housing, the keyboard assembly, the size of it. He had seen photographs. He had been told what to look for.

He stood and looked at it for a moment.

The value of it was not in what it could do. He understood that. The machine itself was useless to him. But the Americans would want it back, or would want it destroyed, and that distinction was useful to know. More useful was what it represented — proof of what this aircraft had been doing, proof of the communications it had handled, and the leverage that came with having it before the Americans could recover it or burn it themselves.

He turned and found Khamla standing three meters behind him.

Khamla was one of the younger men in the unit, had been with him since the dry season, and had not yet done anything to distinguish himself in either direction. He was obedient. That was sufficient for what Bounchanh needed now.

Bounchanh pointed at the gap in the fuselage. He spoke in Lao, his voice flat and without ceremony.

"The machine on the bulkhead. Bring it out."

Khamla looked at the wreck. The smoke was rolling out of the gap in a slow, heavy curtain. The fire behind it was audible — a low crackling that rose occasionally when it found something new to take.

"The whole machine?" Khamla said.

"The chassis. Whatever is bolted to the rack. Bring it out."

Khamla looked at it again. He was not refusing. He was measuring the distance, the smoke, the heat. Bounchanh recognized the calculation and waited for it to finish.

"Yes, Major," Khamla said.

He handed his rifle to the man beside him and moved toward the wreck.

Bounchanh watched him go. The fire had maybe ten minutes before it reached the bulkhead, possibly less if the wiring bundle caught cleanly. The smoke was the worse problem — thick and chemical-smelling, the kind that came from burning insulation and hydraulic fluid, the kind that dropped a man before the heat did. Khamla was moving at a walk, not a run, which was correct. Running into smoke was how men panicked.

The gap in the fuselage was low, a meter off the ground where the belly seam had split. Khamla crouched at the edge of it and looked in. The smoke rolled past him and he turned his face away for a moment, then turned back.

He went in.

Bounchanh stood in the heat and the light of the burning aircraft and watched the gap where Khamla had disappeared, and behind him his men watched from the tree line, and the fire worked steadily through the wiring toward the bulkhead where the cipher machine waited.
