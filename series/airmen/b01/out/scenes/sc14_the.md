Khamla came out of the fuselage at a low crawl, the KL-7 chassis dragging behind him on a length of cargo strap he'd looped through the mounting bracket. The smoke was thick enough that Bounchanh lost him for a moment in the gray-black pour coming from the forward section, then saw him again — a shape resolving out of the haze, moving on hands and knees through the torn aluminum and scattered debris, pulling the machine.

He was moving fast. That was something.

Bounchanh watched from fifteen meters back. He had positioned himself behind the root mass of a fallen tree, not from fear but from the practical understanding of what burning aircraft did when they finished burning. The starboard wing root was still feeding the fire. The fuel remaining in the tanks was an unknown quantity. He had seen aircraft burn for an hour and he had seen them go all at once.

Khamla cleared the fuselage opening and got to his feet. The chassis was heavy — Bounchanh could see it in the way the private moved, the strap cutting into his palm, his body canted sideways against the weight. He was pulling it across the slope, away from the wreck, angling downhill toward the tree line where the ground leveled.

Twenty meters. Twenty-five.

Bounchanh stepped out from behind the root mass and raised his hand. Far enough. That was far enough.

Khamla looked up and saw him. He set the chassis down on a flat section of ground and straightened, pressing one hand to the small of his back. His face was black with smoke. He was breathing hard through his mouth. He looked at Bounchanh and Bounchanh nodded once.

The fuel tanks went.

It was not a single sound but a sequence — a deep concussive thump that Bounchanh felt in his chest before he heard it, then the secondary detonation of the remaining wing tank, and then the pressure wave moving through the trees. He had dropped behind the root mass by instinct. When he came up the fuselage was gone. What remained was burning in pieces across a thirty-meter radius, and the jungle on the eastern edge of the wreck site was catching, the dry undergrowth taking the fire quickly.

Khamla was down.

Bounchanh walked to him. There was no urgency in the walk. He had seen enough to know. The private was on his back, one arm thrown wide, the other folded across his chest as if he had been placed that way. The blast had taken him from behind. He had been twenty-five meters from the fuselage when the tanks went. It had not been enough.

Bounchanh stood over him for a moment. Khamla had been with the unit since the dry season. He had been a good soldier in the way that young soldiers are good — obedient, willing, not yet worn down by the work. He had gone into the burning aircraft because he was ordered to go, and he had come out with the machine because he was the kind of man who completed what he was sent to do.

The order had been given. The order had been obeyed. The cost was what it was.

Bounchanh crouched and checked the chassis. The blast had knocked it onto its side but the casing was intact. He righted it and examined the mounting bracket and the connection points. The rotors were gone — he had expected that, had been told the Americans would strip the rotors before abandoning the machine. What remained was the chassis, the wiring harness, the internal mechanism. Intelligence would want it. They would get what they could from what was left.

He called to the two soldiers at the tree line and they came forward. He told them to carry the chassis and to be careful with it. He told them to leave Khamla where he was for now and to come back for him when the fire settled.

The soldiers looked at Khamla and then looked away and picked up the chassis between them.

Bounchanh walked back toward the wreck site perimeter, or what remained of it. The fuselage was gone. The wing sections were burning down to the spars. The smoke column was higher now, fed by the jungle fire spreading east, and it would be visible for kilometers in every direction.

He thought about the American pilot. The man had been here — the footprints in the soft ground near the forward section, the disturbed debris, the missing rotors. He was somewhere in the jungle to the west, moving or hiding, carrying whatever he had taken from the machine.

The smoke would tell the rescue aircraft where to look. That was fine. Bounchanh had already drawn the circle.

The sound reached Coyle as a double concussion through the trees — the first hit like a fist against his sternum, the second rolling after it like thunder that had no weather behind it. He stopped moving and put his back against a tree and listened to the fire spreading through the canopy to the east.

He did not need to go back to know what it meant. The machine was gone.
