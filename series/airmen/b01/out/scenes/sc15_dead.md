The sound reached him through the soles of his boots before it reached his ears.

A single concussion, deep and flat, the kind that doesn't echo so much as absorb into the canopy and the limestone and the wet air between the trees. Coyle felt it in his jaw. He stopped moving and stood with his weight on his good leg and listened to the silence that followed.

Nothing followed. The jungle came back — insects, a bird somewhere high and unseen, the distant drip of moisture off broad leaves. Whatever had made that sound was finished making it.

He didn't need to work it out. The fuel tanks had gone. He'd seen the starboard wing burning when he cleared the wreckage, had known it was a matter of time. The machine was gone now. All of it — the fuselage, the cargo section, the forward bulkhead where the KL-7 had been mounted. Whatever the fire hadn't taken, that concussion had.

He stood there a moment with that knowledge and let it settle.

Then he looked down at his left hand, at the canvas pouch he'd wrapped around the rotors and tied off at the wrist. Eight of them. He'd counted twice before he moved. Eight rotors from the KL-7, stripped clean before he'd left the wreck, and now the only thing in this jungle that mattered.

The machine was gone. The rotors were here. He was here.

He started moving again.

The left femur was the problem. Not the burns — the burns down his right side were a condition, something to be managed, something that would be worse tomorrow and worse again the day after, but they didn't stop him from walking. The femur was different. Every time his left foot came down wrong, every time the terrain shifted under him and his weight transferred wrong, there was a white moment where his vision narrowed to a point and he had to breathe through it. He'd splinted it with a length of branch and his belt and the lacing from his right boot, and it held, more or less, and he moved.

West. Away from the crash site, away from the smoke column that was probably visible from the Thai border. Away from whoever had been moving through the jungle from the northeast — he'd heard them before he'd cleared the wreck, heard the way they moved through undergrowth without particular caution, following the smoke the way anyone would follow smoke.

West was the right direction. West was Thailand. West was the corridor any rescue package would come through.

He thought about the radio. He'd transmitted the coordinates before he'd lost consciousness, or thought he had — the sequence was not entirely clear to him, the period between the second missile and waking up in the wreck had gaps in it he couldn't account for. But he'd transmitted. He was reasonably sure he'd transmitted. And if the signal had gotten out through the karst, someone at NKP had the valley coordinates and a downed aircraft and a pilot who had not checked in since.

They would come. That was the logic of it. A downed pilot was a rescue package. A downed pilot carrying what he was carrying was something more than that.

He moved through a stand of bamboo, ducking under a fallen section, and the movement torqued his left leg and he stopped again and breathed. The pain was information. He filed it and kept moving.

The jungle here was dense in a way that was almost useful — the canopy was high enough that he could move upright, and the undergrowth was thick enough that it would close behind him. He wasn't leaving much of a trail. He was leaving some trail. There was nothing to be done about that.

What he knew: the rotors were in his hand. The machine was destroyed. No one else had what he had.

What he did not know, and had no way of knowing, standing in that jungle with the concussion still fading in his back teeth: that the chassis had been pulled clear before the tanks went. That Khamla had gone into the burning fuselage and come out with the casing intact, wiring harness and all, and that the blast had taken the empty shell of the aircraft and not the thing that mattered to the men who'd been waiting three weeks on a karst ridgeline for exactly this opportunity.

He didn't know about Khamla. He didn't know what Bounchanh had already drawn on his map.

He knew the rotors were in his hand and the machine was gone and that made him the only thing worth finding in this jungle. Valuable men got rescued. That was the logic. That was the whole of it.

He moved west through the trees, the canvas pouch against his wrist, his left leg managing the terrain one step at a time. The smoke column was behind him now, invisible through the canopy, but he could still feel the heat of it in the air.

The jungle ahead of him was quiet and green and gave nothing away.
