Bounchanh turned the chassis over in his hands. The casing was intact, the wiring harness still attached at three points, the mounting bracket bent but not broken. Khamla had done well getting it out before the tanks went. But the rotor mounts were empty. All eight of them. Stripped clean, not torn out — the threads were undamaged, the locking rings backed off properly. Someone had done this deliberately, and done it fast, and done it before the fire reached the forward bulkhead.

He set the chassis down on the flat rock his men had been using as a table and looked at it for a moment. The pilot. Had to be the pilot. The co-pilot had ejected over the karst and was either dead on the limestone or running east, which was the wrong direction for a man who wanted to live. The pilot had stayed with the aircraft long enough to pull the rotors and then walked west into the jungle with them.

Bounchanh picked up the radio handset.

The connection to his headquarters took three attempts. The karst broke up the signal the way it broke up everything else — sound, light, the will of men who did not know the terrain. When the duty officer's voice came through it was thin and distant.

"Report," Bounchanh said.

He gave it in order. The shoot-down, confirmed kill, no parachutes observed from the aircraft itself. The wreck site, the fire, the secondary detonations. The cipher machine chassis recovered with wiring harness intact. The rotor mounts stripped. The pilot's tracks moving west.

The duty officer said he would relay it up.

Bounchanh said, "Tell them the chassis is incomplete. Tell them the pilot has the rotors. Tell them the pilot is mobile."

He released the handset and looked west into the trees. The jungle there was dark even in the middle of the day, the canopy closing off the sky in layers. A man moving through it with a broken leg — and the tracks said broken leg, the drag pattern was unmistakable — would not be moving fast. But he would be moving. And he would be moving toward the radio contact he needed, which meant west, which meant Thailand, which meant the same corridor Bounchanh had already mapped.

The duty officer called back. Headquarters had relayed to Hanoi. Hanoi had logged the shoot-down as a confirmed kill and flagged the partial recovery. There would be further instructions.

Bounchanh acknowledged and set the handset down.

He looked at the chassis again. Without the rotors it was a housing. It was metal and wire. The Americans would not be coming for the housing.

The instructions came down through channels that moved faster than Bounchanh expected. Hanoi had not treated this as a routine report. The cipher machine was known to them — known by description, known by the intelligence value attached to it — and the gap between what had been recovered and what was still in the jungle was a gap that Hanoi intended to close.

The assignment went to Captain Tran Van Khoa.

Tran was already in Laos. He had been in Laos for eleven days, working out of a forward position in the eastern panhandle, running a network of informants along the Trail corridor. He was not a combat officer in the conventional sense. He was General Reconnaissance Bureau, which meant he understood the difference between what a thing was worth and what it cost to get it, and he made those calculations without sentiment.

He read the message twice. Chassis recovered, rotors missing, pilot mobile and moving west. He looked at the map. He looked at the terrain between the crash site and the Thai border. He looked at the distance.

The pilot had a broken leg and burns. He had the rotors. He had a radio, or had transmitted on one before losing consciousness — the message had included that detail. He was moving toward extraction, which meant he was moving toward the western corridor, which meant he was moving toward the same ground Tran could reach by truck in less time than a man with a broken leg could cover on foot.

Tran folded the map.

He told his sergeant to load the men. Two trucks, full kit, move in twenty minutes. The sergeant did not ask questions. That was one of the things Tran valued about him.

They loaded in the dark under the tree canopy, weapons and packs and the equipment Tran required for the kind of work this would become. The trucks started and moved out onto the track heading west, the engines loud in the jungle quiet, the headlights off.

Tran sat in the cab of the lead truck and watched the track ahead in the gray light that filtered through the canopy. The chassis was already in Bounchanh's hands. The rotors were in the jungle, moving west on a broken leg, attached by a cord to the wrist of a man who did not yet know that the apparatus now moving toward him was not a search party.

It was a retrieval.

The trucks moved west through the trees.
