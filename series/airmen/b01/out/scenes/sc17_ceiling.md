The ceiling fan made three sounds. A tick on the downstroke of the long blade, a faint wobble through the middle of the rotation, and then a soft rush of air that reached the bunk every two seconds or so. Archer had been counting it long enough to know the interval exactly.

The barracks building held maybe a dozen bunks and he was the only one in it. Vance had his own quarters somewhere down the row. The rest of the crew were wherever they were. The building smelled of mildew and old canvas and the particular staleness of rooms that get closed up in the heat and opened again without ever quite airing out, but that wasn't what was keeping him awake.

He wasn't afraid. He'd been through the qualification pipeline, the water work, the altitude chamber, the jump school, the combat diver course, the eighteen months of CSAR training that had left him with a separated shoulder and a scar along his left forearm from a hoist cable that had come apart at the wrong moment. He knew what fear felt like and this wasn't it.

What it was, he couldn't name exactly. Something closer to standing at the edge of a platform before a match, that particular stillness before the referee's whistle, when you've done everything you can do in the gym and now there's nothing left but to find out what it costs. He'd been good at that stillness. His coach at Carbondale had called it patience and said it was the rarest thing in a wrestler, rarer than strength or speed, and Archer had carried it through the service and into the pipeline and it had served him well.

It wasn't serving him tonight.

The fan ticked. The wobble. The rush of air.

He'd been on the hoist before. Training insertions, a stateside SAR exercise in the Cascades, one real pickup in the Philippines that had been straightforward enough — a downed Navy pilot sitting on a beach with a broken wrist, waving his signal mirror at the sky. That one had cost him nothing. He'd ridden the penetrator down, helped the man onto the seat, and they'd been back aboard in four minutes. The pilot had shaken his hand and said something about buying him a beer and Archer had thought, this is the job, this is what it is.

But Vance had told him something in the briefing room that afternoon, not as a warning, just as information delivered in the flat way Vance delivered everything. He'd said that the ones who came through the pipeline thinking they understood the job were the ones who needed the most watching. Not because they were wrong about their skills. Because they were wrong about the weight of it. You don't know what it costs, Vance had said, until you've paid it once.

Archer stared at the ceiling fan.

The thing was, Vance was right. He could run the procedures in his sleep. Hoist deployment, penetrator rigging, survivor assessment, field stabilization, the whole sequence from insertion to extraction. He'd done it enough times that his hands knew the steps before his mind caught up. That part he trusted.

What he didn't know was the other thing. The part that came before the procedures. The part where a man was down in the jungle with people trying to kill him, and the helicopter was taking fire, and the penetrator was swinging on the cable, and whatever was going to happen was going to happen regardless of how many times he'd run the sequence in a training environment. He didn't know what that felt like from the inside. He knew what it looked like from the outside, from the debrief reports, from the things Kowalski had said in passing without quite meaning to say them. But knowing a thing from the outside and knowing it from the inside were different categories of knowledge entirely.

That was what was keeping him awake. Not fear of dying. Something more specific than that. The weight of not yet knowing what the job actually costs, and knowing that he was going to find out, and not being able to do anything about it until he did.

The fan ticked. The wobble. The rush of air.

He put his arm over his eyes and tried to slow his breathing the way his coach had taught him, the long exhale, the deliberate release of tension from the shoulders and the jaw. It helped some. Not enough.

He was still working on it when the klaxon fired.

It was loud enough to feel in the chest, a hard repeating pulse that cut through the building and the night air outside and probably carried to the flight line and back. Archer was on his feet before the second pulse, his hand already finding his boots in the dark beside the bunk, the procedures beginning their automatic sequence in his hands and his body while the rest of him was still catching up.

The klaxon kept going.
