The klaxon was still going when Archer hit the briefing hut door.

Inside, six men already moving. Harnesses coming off pegs, survival vests pulled on over flight suits, the snap and click of buckles working fast in the low light. Nobody talking much. The hut smelled of canvas and gun oil and the particular sweat of men who'd done this before.

Dalton had his vest on and was running a thumb down the zipper of his left chest pocket, checking the seal. Evans was at the comms rack pulling his helmet down, the cord already looped and tucked. Meat Kowalski stood at the weapons board with his back to the door, checking the action on his survival weapon with the flat mechanical attention of a man checking his watch. Sparky Miller was already done, sitting on the bench lacing his second boot, and Animal Motherway was pulling a strap through a buckle on his harness with one hand while he held a map board under his other arm.

Archer moved to his peg. His gear was where he'd hung it, the way Vance had told him to hang it. He got the harness on and started the buckles in order, top to bottom, the way he'd been trained. His hands were steady. He noted that.

The hut was loud in a way the pipeline had never been. Not loud with voices — loud with the fact of it. The klaxon outside, the flight line noise starting up, the specific weight of men moving with purpose toward something that was actually happening. Training had its own sound. This was different. He'd known it would be different. Knowing it and standing inside it were not the same thing.

He worked the buckles. He checked the vest pockets in sequence. He did not look around to see if anyone was watching him.

Vance came through the door behind him. No rush in the way he moved, but no wasted motion either. He went straight to his peg, got the harness off, and started putting it on with the same economy he brought to everything. He didn't look at Archer. He didn't need to. Archer could feel the awareness anyway, the way you can feel a man's attention even when his eyes are somewhere else.

Dalton called out the aircraft status without turning around. "Two-Two's on the line. Fuel's topped. Sandy's already up."

"Copy," Vance said.

Evans said something into the comms handset, listened, said it again. He pulled the handset away from his ear and looked at Dalton. "Bird Dog's got a fix. Valley coordinates, karst terrain, single survivor."

"Single," Dalton said.

"Single."

Nobody said anything to that. Meat finished his weapons check and holstered the survival weapon and picked up his helmet. Sparky stood up from the bench. Animal folded the map board and tucked it under his arm again.

Archer got his helmet on and clipped the chin strap. He checked the Ka-Bar on his thigh rig, the snap on the sheath, the angle of the grip. He'd done the check a hundred times. He did it again. The wrestler's patience, his old coach used to say. You don't rush the setup. You wait for the position and then you move. He waited for the position.

The thing about the pipeline was that the instructors told you what was coming. They told you the scenario, they told you the terrain, they told you the parameters of the exercise. You could build a picture ahead of time and walk into it. Out there, past the Mekong, there was no picture. There was a valley with karst and a man on the ground and whatever else was in the dark between here and him. Archer had been trained for that. He understood, standing in the hut with the klaxon going and the flight line noise building outside, that understanding it and being ready for it were also not the same thing.

He was ready anyway. That was the job.

Vance finished his gear check and turned from the peg. He looked at Archer once, a flat look that took in the harness, the vest, the helmet, the hands. He didn't say anything. He moved toward the door.

Dalton said, "Let's go."

They went. Sparky first, then Dalton, then Evans with the comms bag over his shoulder. Meat went through the door without ducking even though he had to. Animal was last before Archer, and then Archer, and then Vance pulling the door shut behind them.

The dark outside was complete. The flight line lights were a yellow smear to the south and the rotor noise was already there, the Jolly Green's blades turning, the sound of it coming up through the ground and through the chest the way it always did. The air was wet and hot and tasted of the river.

Archer walked toward the lights. The hut was behind him. The dark ahead was the next thing, and the next thing after that, and whatever came after that.

He walked.
