The briefing hut smelled of cigarette smoke and the particular staleness of a room used only at bad hours. A single overhead bulb. A man Archer didn't know stood at the map board with a grease pencil and the flat affect of someone who had delivered this kind of information before and expected to deliver it again.

"AC-119K," the man said. "Call sign Black Widow. Down in the eastern panhandle, Khammouane province. Pilot is a contract aviator named Coyle. CIA." He tapped the map. "Here. Karst valley, jungle slope. Bird Dog has a single survivor located. No beacon lock confirmed. No voice confirmation."

He let that sit.

"Pathet Lao activity in the area is significant. We have reports of a provincial unit operating in that corridor — estimate company strength, possibly reinforced. They were in position before the aircraft went down." He paused. "That's not coincidental."

Vance was looking at the map. Not at the man, at the map. Archer watched him look at it.

"Enemy force density at the crash site is unknown," the briefer said. "What we know is they're there and they're moving. What we don't know is how many and how organized. You're going into a hot zone with incomplete intelligence. That's the situation."

Nobody said anything.

"The deniable-war context means you go now or you don't go. There is no delay option. There is no official rescue support if you go down. You know this." He capped the grease pencil. "Questions."

It wasn't a question itself. It was an invitation that expected no takers, and there were none.

Vance turned from the map. His face gave nothing away. He looked at Archer once, briefly, the way a man checks a door to confirm it's closed, and then he was moving toward the exit.

The order came from somewhere behind them, clipped and without ceremony: "Scramble."

They went out into the dark.

The flight line at NKP at this hour was a different country from the base behind them. The base had buildings and lights and the low hum of generators and the sense, however false, of institutional permanence. The flight line was just tarmac and aircraft and the smell of JP-4 and the Mekong somewhere to the east, invisible, the border it marked equally invisible, equally real.

Jolly Green Two-Two sat on the pad with her rotors turning, Dalton already moving around the fuselage doing his last check with a flashlight, the beam cutting low across the landing gear and the belly. Evans was at the crew door with his comms bag over one shoulder, headset around his neck. Motherway was climbing into the left seat. Miller was checking the gun mount on the port side, running his hand along the feed tray without looking at it, the way you check something you've checked a hundred times and trust your hands to find what your eyes might miss.

Kowalski fell in beside Archer as they crossed the tarmac. He didn't say anything. He had his survival weapon slung and his helmet in his hand and he walked with the particular economy of someone who had done this enough times that the movement itself was a kind of preparation.

Archer had the Ka-Bar on his thigh and the flight harness buckled and the survival vest checked and rechecked, and he was aware of all of it and aware also that being aware of it was the wrong thing to be focused on. He shifted his attention to the aircraft. To the rotors. To the sound of them, which was the sound of the mission already beginning.

Vance reached the crew door first. He stopped there for a moment — not hesitating, just stopped — and looked east. Across the tarmac, across the perimeter wire, across the river that wasn't a border and the country that wasn't a war. The jungle was out there in the dark and the karst was out there and whatever Pathet Lao company strength meant in that particular valley was out there, and somewhere in it a man named Coyle was moving or not moving, alive or not alive, with no beacon and no voice confirmation and a Bird Dog pilot's word that he'd seen something that looked like a survivor.

Archer saw Vance look east. He registered what it meant — not fear, nothing like fear, but the specific calculation of a man who has looked at bad odds long enough to know them on sight. The Pathet Lao activity level. The no-beacon. The hot zone with incomplete intelligence. Vance had taken all of it in during the brief and now he was looking at the direction it came from, and then he was done looking and he stepped up into the aircraft.

Archer followed him in.

Dalton pulled the crew door and dogged it. The rotors changed pitch. The tarmac fell away beneath them and NKP's lights spread out below and then contracted and then the Mekong was a dark line catching no light at all and then it was behind them.

Laos was ahead. The dark was complete.
