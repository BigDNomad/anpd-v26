The Mekong slid beneath them and was gone.

Archer felt the crossing in his chest more than he saw it — the river's pale ribbon there and then not there, the darkness below shifting from the flat black of water to the deeper, more complicated black of jungle and karst. He was seated on the starboard side with his back against the fuselage, harness cinched, and he kept his hands loose in his lap because that was the thing to do with hands when there was nothing yet to do with them.

Vance sat across from him. Not sleeping. Not talking. Just present in the way Vance was present, which was the way a load-bearing wall was present — you didn't notice it until you thought about what would happen without it.

The rotors were loud enough that conversation required the intercom, and nobody was using it.

Then Dalton's voice came through the headset, flat and unhurried, the New Jersey still in it after however many years out here.

"For the benefit of our newest crew member," Dalton said, "I want to point out that we are currently flying over a country that does not exist, on a mission that is not happening, to recover a man who was never there. If we go down, nobody's coming. If we get shot, the paperwork will reflect a training accident. And if anyone asks, I was in Omaha this whole time visiting my mother." A pause. "My mother lives in Trenton. I hate Trenton. So let's not get shot."

Kowalski made a sound that was almost a laugh. Miller said nothing but Archer could see him shift his weight at the gun mount, settling in.

Archer didn't laugh. He registered it — the mechanism of it, the way Dalton had built the thing and delivered it and let it land — and he understood what it was for. He just didn't have the equipment for it yet. That was the honest answer. The humor required a certain distance from the fear, and he hadn't put enough miles between himself and it.

He looked at his hands and kept them loose.

Up front, Evans worked the radio. His voice came through clipped and professional, the comms bag open across his knees.

"Sandy Lead, Jolly Green Two-Two, radio check, over."

Static. Then through it, broken but readable: *Two-Two, Sandy Lead, five by three, how copy.*

"Sandy Lead, same. Package is complete. Two-Two is with you."

The A-1 was out there somewhere in the dark, a sound more than a shape, the big radial engine a different note than the helicopter's rotors. Archer had been told the Skyraider could carry more ordnance than a World War Two bomber and fly all day doing it. He believed it. He'd seen them on the ramp at NKP, loaded to the wing roots, looking like they were trying to remember how to get airborne.

The Bird Dog checked in next, the O-1's pilot thin and reedy through the static: *Two-Two, Nail Four-One, I have your position. Proceeding to holding. Advise when ready for handoff.*

Evans acknowledged. Sandy Lead acknowledged. The package was assembled in the dark over a country that didn't exist, three aircraft talking to each other in broken transmissions over terrain that was already working against them.

The karst was doing it. Archer had been told about this — the limestone formations rising out of the jungle in columns and ridges, the radar return they threw, the way they broke up radio signals the way a hand breaks up water. He could see the shapes of them now through the port window, darker masses against the dark sky, no definition, just presence. The mountains were old and they didn't care about frequencies.

Evans tried a position confirmation with Sandy Lead and got back half a sentence and then nothing and then a fragment that might have been an acknowledgment. He logged it and moved on.

"Beacon?" Vance said, on intercom now.

"Negative," Evans said. "No lock. We're on the coordinate."

Which meant they were flying toward a point on a map where a man might be, or might not be, alive or not alive, with no electronic confirmation of either. The Bird Dog had eyes on something. That was what they had.

Archer looked out the window at the karst. The jungle below was absolute dark, no light anywhere, no road, no village, nothing that suggested the presence of human beings even though he knew there were human beings down there, some of them looking for the same man the helicopter was looking for, and not for the same reasons.

He checked his harness. He checked the Ka-Bar. He checked his harness again.

Vance was watching him. Not saying anything. Just watching, the way you watch a gauge that hasn't moved yet but that you're keeping your eye on.

Archer put his hands back in his lap.

The helicopter flew on into the dark, and the karst rose around them, and the radio gave back static where the confirmations should have been.
