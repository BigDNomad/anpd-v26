The karst came up gray in the early light, limestone towers running north to south like broken teeth, the jungle packed between them so dense it swallowed shadow. Dalton kept the Jolly Green low, threading the formations at a hundred and twenty knots, the rotor wash cutting white off the canopy tops below. The valley floor was maybe two hundred feet under them. It felt like less.

Evans had the headset pressed to one ear, his free hand flat on the comms bag. He'd been that way for ten minutes.

"Nail Four-One still has it," he said. "Intermittent. Signal's breaking up on the karst."

"Same grid?" Dalton said.

"Close. He can't pin it."

Nobody said anything to that. The helicopter banked left around a limestone tower and the sun came through the starboard windows at a low angle, throwing hard shadows across the deck.

Sandy Lead was somewhere above them, holding a racetrack pattern at altitude. He'd come on the radio twice since they crossed the Mekong. The second time he'd said: *Confirmed multiple positions around the crash perimeter. This is not a patrol. They are set.*

That was the word he'd used. Set.

Archer sat on the starboard side with his harness clipped and his hands loose in his lap. He was watching the jungle below the way you watch something you can't read yet. The canopy was unbroken from this angle. Whatever was under it stayed under it.

Vance sat across from him, back against the port bulkhead, eyes forward. He hadn't spoken since they crossed the river.

Kowalski was at the gun mount, settled in, not moving. Miller on the other side. Motherway was up front with Dalton.

The radio crackled. Evans held up one finger.

Static. Then something under it — a carrier wave, maybe a voice, the karst eating most of it before it arrived.

Evans shook his head. "Can't confirm. Nail says he's got a bearing but it's soft."

"How soft," Dalton said.

"Two hundred meters soft."

Two hundred meters in that terrain was the difference between a clearing and a cliff face. Dalton didn't say anything. He kept the helicopter in the valley, threading north, and the limestone towers moved past the windows like they were standing still and the rock was moving.

Sandy Lead came back on. His voice was flat and professional, the way a man sounds when he's done this enough times that the fear has been converted into something useful.

"Two-Two, Sandy. Hold your approach. Bird Dog is working the contact. Do not advance on the grid until I call it."

"Copy," Evans said.

Dalton eased back on the cyclic and the Jolly Green slowed, settling into a loose orbit between two karst formations. The rotor noise bounced off the limestone and came back at them from two directions at once.

Archer looked down through the open door. The jungle was forty shades of green and none of them told you anything. He thought about the briefing — *company strength, possibly reinforced, confirmed present and moving* — and then he stopped thinking about it because thinking about it wasn't useful. What was useful was the harness check he'd already done twice and the Ka-Bar on his hip and the fact that Vance was three feet away and hadn't moved.

He'd trained for the waiting. They'd told him about it in the pipeline, the instructors who'd been in-country and come back with the particular flatness in their eyes that meant they'd seen the real version of the thing they were teaching. *The waiting is the job,* one of them had said. *You wait wrong, you're already behind.*

He hadn't understood it then. He understood it now in a way that had nothing to do with comprehension. It was in his hands, which were still. It was in his breathing, which was slow. It was in the way he kept his eyes on the canopy below and didn't let them drift to Vance's face to check whether Vance looked worried.

The ground below was already occupied. That was the fact. Whatever was down there had been down there before they arrived and would be waiting when they came in. The survivor was somewhere in it, transmitting on a signal that the karst was eating, and the Bird Dog was up there trying to sharpen a soft bearing into something they could use, and until he did, the Jolly Green held its orbit and the rotor noise bounced off the limestone and came back wrong.

Evans pressed the headset tighter. His hand moved on the comms bag.

"Nail Four-One has a voice," he said. "Broken. He's asking for authentication."

Dalton said, "Tell Sandy."

Evans was already transmitting.

The helicopter held its position between the towers. Below, the jungle gave nothing back. Somewhere in it a man was transmitting on a survival radio with Pathet Lao positions drawn in a circle around him, and the signal was faint, and the window was not going to stay open.

Sandy Lead came on.

"Two-Two, stand by."
