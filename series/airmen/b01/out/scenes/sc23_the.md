The cable snapped taut and Archer came back to it hard — the penetrator seat biting into his thighs, the jungle floor dropping away below him as the hoist took his weight. He didn't think about the fall. He didn't think about the shoulder or the blood running into his left eye from somewhere above his brow. He unslung the GAU-5/A and found the tree line and started shooting.

The muzzle flash was visible in the green shadow below the canopy. He put rounds into it. Walked them left, walked them back. The carbine was short and light and he could manage it one-handed if he had to, and he did have to, because his left hand was gripping the cable above the penetrator seat and he was not going to let go of that. He fired until the magazine ran dry and then he reloaded by feel, the spare mag coming out of his vest without him looking for it, and he put the next twenty rounds into the southern tree line where the RPD had been working.

Sandy rolled in from the north.

The Skyraider came down the valley at low altitude, wings level, and the rocket pods fired in sequence — four, five, six white smoke trails arcing into the eastern tree line — and then the 20mm opened up and the sound of it was a long tearing sound, like canvas ripping, and the tree line came apart in a line of dirt and smoke and splintered wood. The RPD went quiet. Two figures broke from cover and Sandy caught them in the open and they went down and did not get up.

It was not enough.

The fire was coming from too many places. The ambush had depth and width and the Pathet Lao had set it well, positions staggered back into the jungle so that suppressing one only opened the angle on another. Sandy could work the edges but the center held, and the center was where Jolly Green Two-Two was hanging in the air above the canopy with Archer on the cable beneath it.

The second RPG came from the south.

Archer saw the backblast — a white puff low in the brush — and then the round was already gone, already climbing, and there was nothing to do about it. It hit the tail rotor pylon with a sound that was more concussion than noise, a flat hard crack that he felt in his chest and in the cable above him, and then the helicopter yawed.

Dalton caught it. He was already on the pedals, already fighting the torque that the damaged rotor was no longer fully countering, and the Jolly Green lurched right and then steadied, but it was not the same steady as before. The tail was wrong. Anyone who had ever flown one knew it from the sound alone — a vibration in the airframe that had not been there, a looseness in the yaw that the pedals could compensate for but could not fix.

Dalton made the call without getting on the radio about it.

He yanked the HH-3E hard right.

The maneuver was violent and it was correct. The helicopter broke out of the kill zone corridor in a banking turn that put the nose west and the tail east and the rotor wash flattened the canopy below in a circle that expanded and then collapsed as the bird clawed for distance. The door guns were still working. Meat and Sparky were still on them, firing back into the valley as the bird turned, the tracers arcing down and back in long orange lines.

Archer went with it.

The cable swung wide as the helicopter broke right, and he went with it the way a pendulum goes — out and up and then through the canopy edge where the trees were thinner, branches catching his legs and his left arm and one of them hitting him across the chest hard enough to knock the breath out of him. He held the carbine. He held the cable. The penetrator seat was still under him and the harness was still on him and those were the two facts that mattered and he held onto both of them while the jungle moved through him and around him and the helicopter dragged him west through the upper canopy at the end of sixty feet of steel cable.

There was no way to reel him in. Not at that speed, not with the tail compromised and Dalton needing every foot of altitude he could find. The hoist operator would know it and Archer knew it and neither of them could do anything about it.

A branch took him across the face and he tucked his chin and closed his eyes and held on.

The Jolly Green Two-Two clawed west out of the valley, damaged and low, with Archer swinging beneath it on the cable like something the jungle had tried to keep and hadn't quite managed to hold.
