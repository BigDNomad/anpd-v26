The harness went.

Not a clean break — the left shoulder buckle had been working loose since the first hard swing through the canopy, metal fatigue doing its quiet work, and when the helicopter yawed hard right on the damaged tail rotor the buckle let go all at once and Archer dropped.

The first branch caught him across the chest and spun him. The second took his left arm and turned him again. He was still gripping the GAU-5/A when a limb the diameter of a man's thigh came up fast out of the green dark and the carbine caught it full on, the weapon wrenched sideways and out of his hands with a force that nearly took the arm with it. He heard it crash away below him through smaller branches, gone, and then he was falling again through the layered dark of the canopy, each tier of vegetation slowing him a little, branch to branch, the jungle breaking his fall in stages the way a staircase breaks a man who falls down it — not kindly, not enough, but enough that when he hit the floor he hit it at something less than the speed that kills.

He hit on his left side and did not move.

The jungle floor was soft with decades of rot and leaf fall and it took him and held him and the sound of the helicopter was already moving west, the damaged tail rotor's vibration a sound you felt in the chest more than heard, and then that too faded into the canopy noise and the birds that had gone quiet came back one at a time and the jungle resumed itself around the shape of the man on the ground.

He did not move.

---

Dalton had both feet on the pedals and it wasn't enough. The tail rotor was giving him maybe sixty percent authority and the bird wanted to yaw right and he was fighting it with everything the pedals had and with throttle adjustments that should not have been necessary and were. Animal Motherway was on the collective, not touching it, hands ready, watching Dalton's face.

"Talk to me," Motherway said.

"She's flying," Dalton said. "She's flying."

The karst fell away beneath them as they cleared the western tree line and the valley opened into lower terrain and Dalton felt the bird settle fractionally, the yaw loosening to something he could manage rather than something he was losing to. He did not say anything about that. He kept his hands where they were and his eyes on the instruments and the horizon and he flew west.

---

Vance was at the door.

He had been at the door since the buckle went, since he watched Archer swing hard on the cable and then the cable come up empty and the penetrator seat swinging free in the rotor wash with nothing attached to it. He had not moved from the door. Evans said something to him and he did not hear it.

Below the helicopter the canopy was unbroken. Green in every direction, the karst ridgelines falling back to the east, the valley narrowing behind them. No movement in the trees. No signal mirror, no smoke, no color that was not jungle color. The spot where Archer had gone in was already indistinguishable from the rest of it. The jungle did not mark the place. It did not hold the shape of what had happened there.

Vance watched anyway.

Meat Kowalski had come off the gun and was standing behind him. Neither of them spoke. There was nothing to say that the situation had not already said, and Vance had been in this work long enough to know that the things you said in moments like this were not for the man who was down. They were for yourself. He did not need them.

The tree line moved under them and was gone and the helicopter kept flying west.

He had brought the kid out here. That was the fact he was standing with. He had looked at Archer in the briefing room and made a judgment and the judgment had been that the kid was ready, or ready enough, and now the kid was down in the jungle with no weapon and no radio that Vance knew of and a company of Pathet Lao between him and anything that could help him. If he was alive. The scene below had not resolved that question and the jungle was not going to answer it.

Vance put one hand on the door frame. The wind came through hard and steady and smelled of nothing but altitude and distance.

Behind him Dalton was holding the bird together with his hands and his feet and whatever he had left, and the bird was flying, barely, and that was what there was. You flew west with what you had. You did not get to go back. Not today, not into that, not with the tail rotor what it was and the fuel what it was and the valley full of what it was full of.

The green closed over the spot and stayed closed.

Jolly Green Two-Two flew on.
