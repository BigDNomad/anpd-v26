He came back to himself in pieces.

First the sound — insects, the drip of water somewhere off to his left, wind moving through the upper canopy in long slow passes. Then the pain, which arrived all at once and without apology. His back, his left shoulder, both forearms where the branches had caught him on the way down. He was on his side in the leaf litter with one arm folded under him and he did not move yet. He breathed. Four counts in, hold, four counts out. His instructor at Lackland had made them do it in the pool with their hands zip-tied, and Archer had hated the man for it, and now the breathing worked the way it was supposed to work and he was grateful.

The panic was there. He knew what it was and he let it sit.

He opened his eyes. Green above him, layered and dense, the canopy closing over the hole his body had punched through it on the way down. Shafts of gray light. He could not see the sky.

He moved his right hand first, then his left. Both responded. He flexed his feet inside his boots, worked his ankles, bent each knee in turn. Nothing ground against nothing. No compound fractures. His left shoulder had taken something on the way through — a branch or a fork in a branch — and the muscle there was wrong in a way that would get worse before it got better, but the arm moved and the joint held. He rolled onto his back and lay still for a moment, listening.

No voices. No movement in the brush. No rotor noise.

Jolly Green Two-Two was gone.

He sat up slowly and ran his hands over his gear. The GAU-5/A was not there. He had known it wouldn't be — he'd felt the sling part somewhere in the upper canopy, felt the weight leave him — but he checked anyway because checking was what you did. Gone. He found the Ka-Bar on his right hip, still seated in the sheath. He found the survival kit in its vest pocket, the zipper intact. He went through it by touch without opening it: signal mirror, fire-starting kit, water purification tabs, the small compass, the emergency rations. The .38 was Coyle's, not his. He had nothing with a trigger except whatever he could improvise, and he set that fact aside the way he'd set the panic aside, filed it under things that were true and could not be changed.

He got to his feet. The world tilted once and steadied.

He looked up through the canopy break and tried to fix his position from the angle of the light, the direction of the slope under his feet, the way the drainage ran. He had been on the hoist when the helicopter took fire and climbed hard to the east, and he had fallen — he thought — somewhere in the transition, the cable swinging wide before it parted or before he lost his grip, he wasn't certain which. The fall had taken him through maybe sixty, seventy feet of canopy. He had no way to know how far east the helicopter had carried him from the original hover point.

Coyle's coordinates he had. He had heard them twice over the radio before the mission went wrong, had repeated them to himself the way you repeat a phone number when you have no paper, and they were still there. He fixed them in his head now and worked the bearing. Coyle was northwest of the original hover point, which put him northwest of here, roughly. Distance was the problem. He could be a kilometer out or he could be four. The karst would make it worse. There was no straight line through this country.

He did not key his radio. Transmitting in this terrain, with Pathet Lao units working the area, was the same as standing up and shouting. He would move first, transmit only when he had to.

The panic was still there. He acknowledged it again, the same way you acknowledge a dog that wants your attention — you see it, you don't feed it, you move on. He had a bearing. He had a man somewhere in the jungle who was alive, or had been alive, and who needed him to get there. He had a Ka-Bar and a survival kit and whatever the jungle would give him. PJs had worked with less.

He checked the compass, oriented himself to the bearing, and looked at the ground ahead of him. The slope ran down to the northwest through dense secondary growth, the kind of brush that grabbed at your legs and slowed you and made noise if you weren't careful. He would have to be careful.

He started moving.

Behind him the canopy break was already closing in the way the jungle closed over everything, patient and indifferent, as if nothing had fallen through it at all.
