The Jolly Green came in low over the perimeter fence with the port engine coughing and the hydraulic warning light still burning red on the panel. Dalton held her steady through the last hundred feet and set her down hard on the tarmac, not pretty, the gear taking the weight with a shudder that ran through the whole airframe. The rotors kept turning, slowing, and then they stopped.

For a moment nobody moved.

Then Vance had the door open and was down on the tarmac before the ground crew reached them, and he turned back to look at the bird the way a man looks at something that has failed him. The port side was punched through in three places, the skin buckled and torn, hydraulic fluid dried brown along the belly. She'd made it. That was the only thing you could say for her.

Evans climbed out behind Vance. Kowalski came last, moving slow, his hand pressed against his side where a piece of something had caught him on the way out of Laos. Not bad enough to ground him. Bad enough.

A ground crew sergeant jogged up with a clipboard. Dalton was still in the cockpit going through shutdown. The sergeant looked at the damage and stopped writing.

Vance didn't wait for anyone to come to him.

"I need another bird," he said. "Fueled and ready. Now."

The sergeant looked at him. "Sir, I'm going to need to get—"

"Get whoever you need to get," Vance said. "Two men are on the ground in Laos. I need a helicopter turned around and sent back in."

Dalton dropped down from the cockpit. He'd heard it. He walked over and put himself between Vance and the sergeant, not to stop anything, just to be there.

"Silas," he said.

"Don't," Vance said.

Dalton looked at him for a moment. His face was tired in the way that had nothing to do with sleep. "I know," he said. "I'm going to push. Hard as I can push, I'll push it."

"Then push it right now. Archer's been on the ground—"

"I know where Archer is."

"—and Coyle's out there and we don't know what shape he's in and every minute we're standing here—"

"Silas." Dalton said it quiet. "The call doesn't belong to me."

Vance went still.

That was the thing about Dalton. He didn't soften it, didn't dress it up. He just said the true thing and let it sit there.

"It goes up the chain," Dalton said. "I can get on the radio right now and I will, but the authority to launch another package isn't mine. It's not the base CO's either, not for a cross-border mission. You know that."

Vance knew it. He'd known it before Dalton said it. That didn't make it anything other than what it was, which was the worst kind of answer — the kind that was correct and useless at the same time.

He looked out past the flight line to where the jungle started, or where you imagined it started, the green wall of it somewhere beyond the wire and the flat brown Thai plain. Laos was over there. Archer was over there. Moving through karst in whatever condition the fall had left him in, working toward a man whose status nobody knew, with no rifle and no radio contact and Pathet Lao units somewhere in the same jungle.

The Jolly Green sat on the tarmac behind him. The bird that could go get them. Parked.

"Get on the radio," Vance said.

"Already going," Dalton said, and turned back toward the aircraft.

Kowalski came up beside Vance. He didn't say anything. He stood there with his hand still pressed to his side and watched Dalton climb back into the cockpit, and the two of them stood on the tarmac in the heat while the ground crew moved around the damaged helicopter with their clipboards and their cameras, documenting the holes.

Evans sat down on the tarmac with his back against the landing gear. He took his helmet off and set it beside him and looked at the sky, which was white and flat and told you nothing.

The thing about a rescue mission was that it ended when the bird came home. The paperwork said so. The mission clock stopped when the wheels touched down. Whatever was still out there — whoever was still out there — that was the next mission, a different entry in a different log, requiring a different authorization from men sitting in rooms that were not this tarmac, in buildings that were not this airbase, making calculations that had nothing to do with Archer moving through the jungle on a bad shoulder with a dead man's revolver.

Vance stood in the heat and waited for Dalton to come back out of the cockpit with news.

The rotors had stopped turning. The hydraulic fluid had stopped dripping. The ground crew sergeant was writing something on his clipboard.

Two men on the ground.

The machine that could get them out was parked.
