He heard them before he saw them.

Voices first, low and conversational, the way men talk when they believe they own the ground they're walking. Then boots on karst — that particular sound, stone under rubber, irregular and close. Archer went flat against the slope and did not move.

The ridge ran above him maybe thirty feet, the limestone breaking through the soil in shelves and outcroppings that caught the last of the light. The security element was up there, moving along the spine of it, and from the sound he counted three, maybe four men. Moving east to west. The same direction he needed to go.

He stayed flat until he had their rhythm.

That was the thing about patience — it wasn't passive. You were working the whole time, reading what the sound told you, building the picture. The element was moving at a steady pace, not searching hard, not stopping to glass the slope. A sweep, not a hunt. They hadn't found sign of him yet.

He got to one knee and looked up the slope. The karst shelves gave him cover to the ridgeline but not past it. He didn't need past it. He needed parallel.

He started moving.

Each step he took, he keyed to them. When a boot came down above him he moved. When they paused he stopped, weight distributed, one hand against the limestone, the bad shoulder carrying nothing. The shoulder had stiffened since the fall and the range of motion was gone from it, but it would hold his weight if he kept it below his chest. He'd learned that in the first hour on the ground. You work with what you have.

The slope was loose in places, decomposed limestone and root matter that wanted to shift under his boots, and he had to pick each placement before he committed to it. No sound. No rolling stone, no snapped branch. The element above him was generating enough noise to cover small mistakes but he didn't plan on making any.

They stopped.

He stopped.

One of them said something in Lao, short and flat, and another answered. Archer held position against the karst shelf, one boot on a root, the other on bare stone, and waited. The revolver was in his right hand. He hadn't drawn it consciously — it was just there, the way the body makes decisions before the mind catches up. Six rounds. He thought about that and then he stopped thinking about it because it wasn't useful.

The voices continued for a moment. Then boots again, moving.

He moved.

This was the part that couldn't be taught directly, only absorbed through repetition until it became reflex. The timing. You had to feel the other man's footfall in your own body, had to let his noise become your permission. Too early and you were moving in silence against silence. Too late and you were behind the rhythm, rushing to catch up, and rushing made sound. The interval was everything.

He'd done this in training on ridgelines in New Mexico, instructors above him with noise-makers, and he'd done it badly at first and then less badly and then one day he'd done it without thinking about it at all, and that was when they told him he had it. You'll know when you have it, they said. He knew.

The element moved northwest along the ridge and he moved northwest below it, thirty feet of limestone and jungle between them, and the light was going now, the canopy above him darkening from green to gray. He didn't look up. He watched the slope ahead of him, the next placement, the next shelf, the next root.

They were slowing.

He slowed.

The voices came again, more of them talking now, and he heard what might have been a laugh. Relaxed. They were past the area they'd been tasked to sweep and they knew it. The discipline was coming off them the way it does when men think the work is done.

He held position and listened to them go.

The sound signature changed as they moved up the ridge, the voices dropping in register as distance took them, the footfalls becoming intermittent and then absent. He waited longer than he needed to. That was the other thing — the impatience that killed you was the impatience at the end, when the threat was receding and the body wanted to move and you let it move too soon. He'd seen it in training. He'd seen what it cost.

He counted to sixty. Then sixty again.

Then he moved.

The ridge above him was empty and the jungle below him was dark and he was still northwest of Coyle's coordinates, still moving, the bad shoulder aching in a way that had become background noise, the revolver back in his waistband. He hadn't fired it. He hadn't needed to.

He kept moving.
