The stream was maybe twelve feet across, shallow enough that he could see the pale stones on the bottom even in the failing light. The current moved quick over the rocks, making the kind of continuous noise that could cover a man if he used it right.

The sentry was on the far bank, elevated maybe eight feet on a shelf of limestone that jutted out over the water. He was sitting, not standing, which meant he'd been there long enough to get comfortable. AK across his knees. He was looking downstream, northeast, where the stream bent around a stand of bamboo. That was his attention. That was where he expected something to come from.

Archer had been watching him for four minutes. The sentry hadn't moved his head more than twenty degrees in either direction. No pattern to it — he wasn't scanning, he was just looking at the one place. A man who'd been told to watch a crossing and had decided the crossing was downstream.

The gap was upstream, to Archer's left. The shelf of limestone blocked the sentry's sightline in that direction — the rock itself cut his arc. Forty feet of stream bank that the man simply could not see from where he was sitting. Archer needed to cross in that window and reach the tree line on the far side before the sentry shifted.

He crouched and gathered a handful of gravel from the bank. Small stuff, quarter-inch, the kind that would scatter when it hit. He held it loose in his right hand and looked at the bamboo stand downstream. The bend in the stream. That was where the sentry was looking.

He threw the gravel sidearm, low, angling it well downstream of his position and well downstream of the crossing point he intended. It hit the bamboo and the near bank both, a brief clatter and then the sound of something moving through the cane.

The sentry's head came up and turned downstream. Both hands went to the AK.

Archer was already in the water.

He went in low, keeping his profile below the level of the bank, moving on a diagonal that used the current rather than fighting it. The water was cold and came up to his thighs. He kept his feet wide and his steps deliberate, feeling each stone before he committed weight to it. His bad shoulder he held close to his body, below chest level, the way he'd learned to manage it since the fall. The stream noise covered what little sound he made. He moved through it the way you move through a crowd — not fast, not slow, just steady, taking up no more space than necessary.

Eight feet. Ten. The limestone shelf was directly above him now and slightly behind. He didn't look up. Looking up would tilt his face and change his silhouette and he didn't need to see the sentry to know where the sentry was.

He reached the far bank and went up it in two steps, staying low, and into the tree line. He pressed himself against the first substantial trunk he reached and went still.

The sentry said something. A single word, low, not alarmed. Then nothing.

Archer waited. His breathing was controlled. The water ran cold down his legs and into his boots and he let it. The light was going gray-green in the canopy above him, the last of the day bleeding out of the sky.

The sentry shifted on his rock. Archer heard the scrape of boot leather on limestone. Then the man settled again and went quiet.

Archer gave it another minute. Then he turned and looked at what was ahead of him.

The ground rose sharply from the stream bank, karst limestone pushing up through the soil in broken shelves and columns, the jungle growing thick between them. Difficult ground. The kind of ground that slowed a man and made noise if he wasn't careful. He'd need to move along the base of the formation rather than over it, find the natural corridors between the rock faces, and that would add distance to what was already a long approach.

He didn't think about the crossing. It was done. The sentry was behind him and the sentry was not a problem he needed to carry forward.

What he needed to carry forward was the ground ahead, and Coyle somewhere in it, and the fact that he had six rounds in a dead man's revolver and a shoulder that worked if he kept it low and a set of coordinates that were only as good as the radio transmission that had carried them, which had been broken and partial and received under fire.

He'd gotten the numbers. He was reasonably sure he'd gotten the numbers.

He started moving along the base of the karst, keeping the rock to his right, reading the ground in the gray light. The stream noise faded behind him. The jungle closed in and went quiet except for the insects and the distant call of something he didn't try to identify.

The match wasn't over. It was just in a different period now.

He kept moving.
