The Bird Dog came in low over the ridge, maybe eight hundred feet, the little Cessna working the contours of the terrain the way a man reads a page — slow, methodical, back and forth. The FAC had been up there long enough to know what he was looking at. The movement below the canopy edge, where the karst broke into a shallow saddle between two limestone faces, was not animal. The spacing was wrong for animals. The intervals were tactical.

He keyed his radio and gave Sandy the grid.

Sandy One-Five acknowledged and began his run.

The A-1 Skyraider came in from the south, low and deliberate, the big radial engine loud enough to rattle teeth at altitude. It was not a combat pass. There was no ordnance coming off the wings. Sandy knew where the Americans were — or where they were supposed to be — and the rules said you did not put iron on a grid square that might contain your own people. So Sandy flew the pass clean, gear up, throttle back just enough to slow the aircraft and give the pilot time to read what was below him.

What was below him opened up.

Two positions, maybe three. The muzzle flash came from the saddle and from a point further west along the ridge where the limestone jutted out and gave a man a natural parapet. AK fire, mostly, and something heavier — an RPD by the sound of it, the cyclic rate slower and more deliberate than the Kalashnikovs. The tracers arced up and Sandy broke right, climbing hard, the Skyraider's engine bellowing as the pilot pushed the throttle forward and put the nose up and got altitude between himself and the guns.

He did not return fire.

He marked the positions on his kneeboard and got back on the radio to the Bird Dog.

Archer heard it before he saw anything.

The Skyraider's engine was unmistakable — that deep, irregular throb that no jet could replicate, the sound of a piston engine working hard at low altitude. He was pressed against the base of a limestone formation, the karst rising above him in a gray wall, the canopy overhead thinning where the rock broke through. His shoulder was tight. He had been moving carefully, favoring the right side, and the wet from the stream crossing had not helped. The fabric of his flight suit had stiffened as it dried and the stiffness pulled at the joint when he reached.

He stopped moving when he heard the engine.

The Skyraider came over fast and low, south to north, and then the guns opened up and the sound changed — the flat crack of the AKs and the slower hammer of the RPD, and then Sandy's engine going to full power as the pilot climbed out. The whole exchange lasted maybe four seconds.

Then it was quiet again.

Archer did not move for a long moment. He was listening to the jungle settle back into itself, the birds that had gone silent beginning to stir again somewhere above him. The light was going. The canopy had shifted from green to gray-green and the shadows between the limestone formations were deepening into something that would be dark inside an hour.

He thought about what the flyby meant.

Sandy had not come in to shoot. Sandy had come in to look, and the guns had told Sandy what he needed to know. The positions were marked now. The rescue package knew where the enemy was, which meant the rescue package was still working the problem. They had not pulled off station. They had not gone home.

He was still in the game.

That was the thing about the Skyraider — it was not a subtle aircraft. When Sandy was on station you knew it. When Sandy left you knew that too. The silence after a Sandy departure had a different quality than the silence of a jungle that had never heard an engine. Archer had learned to read that difference. Right now the silence said Sandy had broken off but not broken away. The Bird Dog was still up there somewhere, too high to hear, circling.

He shifted his weight off the limestone and checked his bearing. Coyle's coordinates were northwest. The terrain between here and there was karst all the way, which meant slow going and no straight lines. The light was going to be a problem.

He pushed off the rock face and started moving.

The shoulder complained when he used his right arm to steady himself against the limestone, and he let it complain and kept moving. The revolver was in his left cargo pocket, six rounds, and he had not fired it and did not intend to. Noise was the one thing he could not afford. The security element was somewhere behind him on the ridge and the gun positions Sandy had just marked were somewhere to his north and west, which put him between them.

He moved parallel to the ridge, staying low, using the karst formations for cover the way the terrain intended.

Above him, somewhere in the gray sky, the Bird Dog turned and began another pass.
