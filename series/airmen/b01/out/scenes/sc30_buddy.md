He found Coyle in a hollow between two limestone faces, the man propped against the rock with his legs out straight and his hands pressed to his side. The flight suit was dark from the collar down on the left. His eyes were open.

Archer came in low and said nothing until he was close enough to touch him.

"Archer," Coyle said. His voice had the quality of something running out.

"Don't talk." Archer got his knee down and put two fingers to Coyle's neck. The pulse was there but it had no conviction to it. He pulled Coyle's hands away from the wound and looked. The bleeding had slowed the way bleeding slows when there isn't much left to lose.

"Blood type," Archer said.

Coyle's eyes moved to him. "O-negative."

Archer looked at him for a moment. "This is your lucky day."

He unslung the aid bag from his left shoulder and set it open on the ground. His right shoulder pulled when he reached across, the way it had been pulling since the fall, and he worked around it. He got the IV kit out and the citrate solution and the Y-junction connector and laid them in order on the flat of a rock.

"I'm going to run a line," Archer said. "You're going to feel the needle and then you're going to feel better. Not right away. But you will."

Coyle said, "How long."

"Twenty minutes."

Coyle closed his eyes and opened them again. "Okay."

Archer tied off Coyle's left arm above the elbow with a length of surgical tubing, found the vein, and ran the catheter in. Coyle didn't move. Archer taped the line down and connected the Y-junction, then drew the citrate into the line to keep it from clotting. He worked the other end of the junction and found his own vein in the crook of his left arm, the arm he could still use without the shoulder fighting him, and ran the second catheter home.

He taped his own line down and sat back against the limestone.

The line ran between them, a thin clear tube with the dark of Coyle's blood already moving through it.

Neither of them could go anywhere now.

Archer looked at the hollow they were in. The limestone walls rose on two sides, maybe eight feet, and the canopy closed most of the gap above. The light that reached them was the color of old brass and it was going. He could hear the Bird Dog somewhere above the canopy, its engine a steady drone that told him nothing had changed up there. Sandy would be burning fuel in a wide circle, waiting.

He thought about the security element he'd moved around on the ridge. They'd been working east to west and then northwest. The direction of travel put them somewhere behind him now, or it should have. He didn't know how long they'd been moving since he crossed the stream. He didn't know if they'd found the sentry.

He looked at the line. The flow was slow and steady.

Coyle's color was the color of the limestone. His breathing had evened out some, which could mean the blood was helping or could mean something else. Archer watched his chest and counted.

"You came back," Coyle said.

"Don't talk."

"I'm just saying."

"I know what you're saying. Save it."

Coyle was quiet. The Bird Dog made another pass, lower this time, and Archer tracked it by sound until it faded north.

The hollow gave them concealment but not cover. If someone came down from the ridge and looked into the gap between the limestone faces they would see two men sitting against the rock with a line running between them and nowhere to go. Archer had the revolver in his left cargo pocket. Six rounds. He thought about what six rounds meant against a four-man patrol with AKs and decided it meant very little and put the thought away.

The work required what it required. You didn't stop a transfusion because the situation was uncomfortable.

He checked the line again. Checked Coyle's pulse. Still thready but present, and maybe — maybe — with a little more behind it than before. It was hard to say. He wanted it to be true so he was careful about believing it.

The light in the hollow dropped another degree. Above the canopy the sky would still be pale but down here the dark came early and it came fast.

Coyle's eyes were closed. His chest moved.

Archer sat with his back against the limestone and his left arm extended and the line running out of him and into the man beside him, and he listened to the jungle. A bird somewhere. The distant engine of the Bird Dog. The sound of water moving over rock, farther off than the stream he'd crossed.

And under all of it, or maybe just in the place where his attention went when it had nowhere else to go, the sound of nothing. The particular silence that meant men moving carefully through undergrowth.

He wasn't sure. He listened harder.

The line ran between them. Coyle breathed.
