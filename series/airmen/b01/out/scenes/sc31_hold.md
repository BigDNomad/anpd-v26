The first one came through at maybe ten meters, moving slow, the way men move when they're looking for something they half expect to find. Archer heard the brush before he saw the shape — a dark figure working through the undergrowth, rifle carried low, head turning.

He got the revolver up with his left hand. His right arm lay across his chest, the catheter taped at the crook of his elbow, the surgical tubing running down to where it connected at the Y-junction and then to Coyle's arm. The line was taut. Not much slack in it. Maybe four inches of play if he moved carefully, and carefully was not a word that went with what he might need to do.

He couldn't fire. Couldn't shift his body. Couldn't do anything that would pull the catheter free of either arm, because if the line came out of Coyle it was over, and if it came out of his own arm it was over a different way, and both of those ways ended the same.

So he held.

The figure resolved into a man — Pathet Lao, web gear, AK slung now across his back, moving with the careful step of someone who'd been told to be quiet and was trying. Behind him a second shape, then a third. They were moving east to west along the base of the limestone face, maybe following the natural line of the terrain, maybe following orders, maybe following nothing at all except the path of least resistance through the jungle.

Archer tracked the lead man across his front. The revolver had six rounds and he had one functional arm and the line between him and Coyle was four inches of slack and the nearest man was ten meters away in failing light and the math on all of that was not good math.

Coyle hadn't moved. His breathing had steadied over the last stretch of the transfusion — shallow still, but regular, the kind of breathing that meant something was working. His color had come up slightly from the gray-white it had been when Archer found him. Not good color. Better color. There was a difference.

The second man passed through a gap in the vegetation and Archer could see his face for a moment, young and watchful, scanning the hollow without seeing what was in it. The limestone overhang threw shadow across the ground where they lay. The light was going fast now, the canopy above already dark, and whatever the hollow looked like from ten meters it apparently looked like nothing worth stopping for.

Archer's finger stayed indexed along the frame. Not on the trigger. He'd made that decision when the first shape appeared — indexed, not on, because a man with one working arm and a line running out of his elbow and a target at ten meters in bad light was not a man who should be making trigger decisions until he had to. When he had to, he'd know.

The third man came through. Then a fourth, moving faster than the others, catching up. One of them said something low in Lao and another one answered and then they were past the gap in the vegetation and moving west along the base of the karst face, the sounds of their movement fading into the general noise of the jungle.

Archer held the revolver up for another thirty seconds. Counted it out. Then he lowered it to his chest and lay still and listened to the jungle come back to itself — the insects first, then somewhere above them a bird starting up again, and then just the sound of his own breathing and Coyle's.

The line between them was still intact. He could feel the faint pull of it at his elbow, the tape holding, the catheter seated. He looked at Coyle's arm where the other end ran in and the tape there was holding too.

He watched the drip in the Y-junction slow. Then stop.

The citrate solution was through. The blood transfer was done.

He lay there another minute without moving, listening to the direction the patrol had gone, listening for anything coming back. Nothing came back. The jungle held its ordinary sounds and nothing else.

Coyle's chest rose and fell. His lips had color in them now that hadn't been there before — not much, but enough to see even in the dimming light. His pulse, when Archer pressed two fingers to his wrist, was still weak but it was there and it was steady, and steady was what mattered.

Archer began working the tape free from his own arm, slow and careful, keeping the catheter in place until he had the tubing clamped off. His right shoulder ached through the whole operation, the stiffness deep in the joint, the range of motion still wrong. He ignored it the way he'd been ignoring it.

The transfusion had held. Coyle was stabilized. The patrol was gone.

None of that meant they were out of it. The gun positions were still up on the ridge. The security element was still working the area. The light was almost gone and they were still on the ground in Laos and the helicopter was not here.

But Coyle was breathing. That was the thing that was true right now.
