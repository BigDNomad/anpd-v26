Coyle came back slow. Not all the way, but enough. His eyes tracked the overhang above them, then found Archer's face, and something in them sharpened.

"The rotors," he said.

Archer waited.

Coyle moved his right hand to his chest, fingers working at the inside of his flight suit. He had them in a canvas pouch sewn flat against the lining, eight of them, each one a machined aluminum disc no bigger than a silver dollar. He laid them on the ground between them one at a time, and Archer watched him do it.

"Cipher rotors," Coyle said. "KL-7. I pulled them from the nav station before I got out."

Archer looked at the rotors. Small things. Unremarkable.

"MACV-SOG uses that cipher family for network traffic," Coyle said. "The whole northern Laos net. Air America cover identities, the 404 personnel, the road watch teams." He paused to breathe. "Anyone who can read these can roll up every American asset between the Mekong and the DMZ."

Archer picked one up. The machining was precise, the tolerances tight. He set it back down.

"How many people know you have them."

"The crew chief who helped me out of the aircraft. He's dead. The Black Widow went in hard." Coyle's voice was flat about it. "Nobody else."

The light in the hollow was going. Through the gap in the limestone Archer could see the sky had turned the color of old brass, and the jungle below the karst was already dark. He thought about the patrol that had passed through, the careful way they'd moved, the spacing between them. He thought about what he was carrying in his own blood right now, running back through the catheter line.

He had been sitting next to this man for the better part of an hour. He had not known.

"You need to bury them," Archer said.

"That's what I'm saying."

"Not near here. Not where the patrol came through."

Coyle nodded. He was working to stay present, Archer could see that, the effort it cost him to hold the thread of the conversation. "There's a riverbend I passed. Coming in. Limestone formation on the outside of the bend, big boulders, stacked. Distinctive enough you could find it again without a map."

"How far."

"Quarter mile. Maybe less."

Archer looked at the rotors. Eight of them. He thought about what it would mean if he was taken. What it would mean if Coyle died before extraction and someone found the body. The rotors could not be on either of them. That was the only logic that held.

"Can you walk," Archer said.

"I can walk."

He couldn't, not well, but he could move with Archer's shoulder under his arm, which was the left shoulder, the good one, and they went slow and careful down the slope from the hollow, Archer watching the treeline and the ridge above them. The gun positions on the ridge were quiet. The patrol had moved northwest. The jungle held its sounds — insects resuming, a bird somewhere in the canopy working through its last business before dark.

The riverbend was where Coyle said it would be. The water was low, running clear over flat rock, and the boulders on the outside of the bend were limestone, pale gray, stacked in a formation that looked almost deliberate, the way karst sometimes did. The largest one had a face on it, a vertical plane turned toward the river, and above it the treeline broke at an angle that Archer fixed in his mind: forty-five degrees off the bend, the gap between two taller trees making a notch against the sky.

He would be able to find this again.

Coyle sat against the boulder while Archer dug with the Ka-Bar, working one-handed, the right arm doing what it could. The ground at the base of the formation was soft, river silt carried up in the wet season and deposited here, and the digging went faster than he expected. He went down eighteen inches and stopped.

Coyle put the rotors in the pouch and handed it across. Archer set it in the hole and covered it and tamped the earth flat with his boot. He moved a flat rock over the spot, not to mark it but because the disturbed earth would show otherwise.

He stood and looked at the formation. The bend angle. The boulder face. The notch in the treeline above. He walked ten feet back toward the jungle and turned and looked again, fixing the approach line. He would know this place.

"Okay," he said.

Coyle was watching him from the ground. His color was better than it had been in the hollow, or maybe that was the failing light making it hard to tell. He said, "If something happens to me before you get out."

"I know," Archer said.

"Someone needs to know where—"

"I know," Archer said again. "Let's move."

He got Coyle up and they started back toward the hollow, moving carefully in the last of the light, the jungle closing around them.
