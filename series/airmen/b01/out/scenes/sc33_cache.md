Archer keyed the handset twice, listened to the static, then brought it close to his mouth.

"Bird Dog, Two-Two Bravo. Authenticate Kilo."

He kept his voice flat and low. The limestone overhang caught it, held it. Coyle was propped against the rock beside him, eyes open now, watching.

The handset crackled. A voice came through broken and thin, the karst eating half of it. "Two-Two Bravo, Bird Dog. Authenticate—" Static. Then: "—Romeo. Go ahead."

Archer had the landmarks fixed in his mind the way you fix a knot — not memorized so much as tied there, the sequence of it physical. The riverbend. The boulder formation, three stones in a rough triangle with the largest facing upstream. The overhang above the waterline. He gave it in the brevity code they used for grid references, four groups of numbers, then the descriptors in plain language because the code didn't have words for limestone and river-bend and the way the largest boulder leaned.

He kept the whole transmission under thirty seconds.

When he released the key the jungle came back — insects, the distant water, the sound of his own breathing. He waited.

"Two-Two Bravo, Bird Dog. Copy all. Confirm—" The signal broke apart and reassembled. "—grid and landmarks. Good copy. Stand by."

Archer set the handset against his thigh and looked at the tree line. Nothing moved. The light was going amber through the canopy, the shadows starting to pool and run together the way they did in the last hour before dark. He had maybe forty minutes of usable light left, less in the karst where the limestone walls cut the sky down to a strip.

He thought about the transmission. Thirty seconds, maybe thirty-five. A direction-finding team with the right equipment could get a bearing in less than that. Whether there was a DF team within range was a question he couldn't answer. The Pathet Lao units he'd been tracking didn't carry that kind of gear, not the patrol that had come through. The NVA advisors might. He didn't know.

What he knew was that the coordinates were out now. Command had them. The rotors' location was no longer something that lived only in his head and in whatever was left of Coyle's.

That was the point of it. That was why you transmitted even when you didn't want to.

Coyle said, "They get it?"

"They got it."

Coyle let his head rest back against the limestone. His color was better than it had been — not good, but better, the gray gone from around his mouth. He was breathing without the shallow catch that had been there before. The catheter line lay coiled beside him, the work of it done.

"Good," Coyle said.

Archer didn't answer. He was watching the tree line still, the handset in his good hand, his right arm held close to his body the way it wanted to be held. The shoulder had stiffened through the afternoon and the transmission had cost him — reaching up to key the handset, holding the angle. He hadn't let it show.

The handset crackled again. "Two-Two Bravo, Bird Dog. Command confirms receipt. Package is working the problem. Advise you hold position. How copy?"

"Good copy. Holding."

"Bird Dog out."

Static, then nothing.

Archer set the handset down and looked at what they had. Two men in a limestone hollow with one knife between them and a revolver with four rounds in it and the light going. The patrol that had come through was northwest now, or had been. The gun positions on the ridge hadn't moved. The package was working the problem, which meant Vance and Dalton were somewhere over the border or waiting to come over it, and command was talking to command, and the machinery of it was turning.

He had done what he could do from the ground. The rest of it was out of his hands.

That was the part that sat in him without settling. Not the transmission itself — the transmission was clean, it was done, the information was where it needed to be. It was the other side of it. Every signal went two ways. You put something into the air and the air didn't care who was listening.

Coyle was watching him.

"You think someone else heard it," Coyle said. It wasn't a question.

"Maybe."

"How long before they could move on it?"

Archer looked at the light in the canopy. "Depends on where they are."

Coyle was quiet for a moment. Then: "We need to be somewhere else when they get here."

"Yeah."

"Can you move me?"

Archer looked at him. Coyle's face was set, the pain in it controlled and deliberate, a man deciding something rather than feeling it.

"Not far," Archer said. "And not fast."

"Far enough," Coyle said. "Fast enough."

Archer picked up the handset and the Ka-Bar and got his feet under him. His shoulder sent its message up through the joint and he acknowledged it and moved anyway. The hollow was good cover but Coyle was right. Good cover that someone was walking toward was just a place to die.

He reached down and got Coyle under the arm.
