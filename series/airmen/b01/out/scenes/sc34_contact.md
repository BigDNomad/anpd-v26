Archer keyed the handset once, listened to the static, then transmitted low and fast. Alternate LZ, grid two-seven-four, route along the drainage east of the limestone face. He released the key before the sentence was fully out of his mouth. Twelve seconds, maybe less. He pulled the handset down and held still.

Bird Dog acknowledged with two clicks. That was enough.

He tucked the handset against his body and looked at Coyle, who had his back against the limestone and his eyes open. Coyle gave a small nod. They both understood what came next.

Archer moved first, angling south along the base of the formation to put distance between them and the transmission point. The shoulder had locked up worse in the last hour, the whole right side of his back pulling tight when he reached or turned, and he kept the arm close to his body and used his left hand on the rock face for balance. The light was going. Not gone yet, but going, the jungle floor already in shadow while the canopy above still held color.

He heard it before he saw anything. A single displacement of undergrowth, not the random settling of branches but the specific sound of weight shifting onto a foot. Deliberate. Careful. Moving toward the transmission point.

Archer stopped.

The sound came again, closer. Whoever it was had a bearing on where the voice had been.

He set the handset down against the base of the rock, drew the Ka-Bar, and moved toward the sound. Not away from it. The shoulder made the grip awkward and he switched the knife to his left hand and held it there. He had four rounds in the revolver and no way to use them without bringing everything in the valley down on top of them.

The scout came through a gap in the undergrowth moving slowly, head turning, listening. Young. Carrying an AK at the low ready. He had not yet seen Archer.

Archer closed the last three feet fast and took him from behind, left arm across the throat, the Ka-Bar in under the ribs and angled up. The scout's body went rigid and then went slack and Archer held him until there was nothing left to hold, then lowered him to the ground without a sound.

He stayed crouched over the body for a moment. His left hand was shaking. Not from effort. The knife was still in his hand and he looked at it and then looked away and wiped the blade on the scout's shirt and sheathed it. He listened to the jungle. Nothing moved. Whatever the scout had been, he had been alone, or far enough ahead of the others that no one had seen.

Archer picked up the handset and moved back to Coyle.

Coyle looked at him. Looked at his hand. Did not say anything for a moment.

"How far to the LZ," Coyle said.

"Four hundred meters. Maybe five."

Coyle nodded and started to push himself upright. Archer helped him with his left arm and Coyle got his feet under him and stood, breathing through his teeth.

They were almost moving when Coyle said, "First time with a blade."

It was not a question.

Archer did not answer right away. The shaking had moved up into his forearm and he could not make it stop. He had killed men before, from distance, from the door of the helicopter with the GAU, and that was a different thing entirely. This had been close enough to feel.

"Yeah," he said.

Coyle looked out at the jungle. His color was better than it had been but his face was still drawn tight around the eyes, the way a man looks when pain has become the background condition of everything.

"It doesn't leave you," Coyle said. "The first one. None of them do, but the first one especially. You think it will and it won't." He said it the way a man states a fact about weather or terrain. No softness in it, no attempt to make it easier. "You carry it. That's all there is to it."

Archer looked at him.

"I'm not telling you that to help," Coyle said. "I'm telling you because it's true and you should know it."

"I know it," Archer said.

"No. Right now you think you know it. Give it time."

They moved out along the base of the formation, Coyle's hand on Archer's left shoulder for balance, Archer watching the ground ahead and the shadows between the trees. The light was failing steadily now, the karst going gray above them, the drainage they were following dropping into deeper dark. Four hundred meters. Maybe five. The alternate LZ was a clearing the Bird Dog pilot had spotted on his last pass, a break in the canopy along the drainage where the limestone had pushed the trees apart.

Archer kept moving. The shaking in his arm did not stop. He did not expect it to.
