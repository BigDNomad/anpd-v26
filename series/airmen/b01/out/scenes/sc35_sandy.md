The round came up through the floor of the sky and found the Skyraider somewhere over the karst, and the Colonel felt it before he heard it — a hard shudder through the airframe, like the aircraft had swallowed something wrong. The oil pressure gauge dropped. Not all the way, not yet, but enough.

He keyed the radio. "Sandy One-Five is hit. Taking smoke. Breaking off."

The Bird Dog pilot came back with something but the Colonel was already in a bank, pulling north, watching the temperature climb on the engine instruments. The smoke wasn't heavy. Not yet. He had time. Maybe.

The Skyraider was a forgiving aircraft. That was the thing about the old Spad — it would carry you a long way on not much. He'd seen them come back with half a wing. He'd seen them come back with holes you could put your fist through. He leveled out and pointed the nose toward the border and let the aircraft tell him what it had left.

What it had left was not a great deal.

The smoke thickened somewhere over the ridge line. He could smell it now, that particular smell of hot metal and burning oil that meant the engine was eating itself from the inside. The temperature gauge was pinned. He pulled the throttle back and the aircraft settled into a long descending glide, the jungle sliding past below him, green and indifferent.

The border was a line on a map. It didn't look like anything from the air. But he knew where it was, and he watched the landmarks come and go beneath him — the river bend, the karst formation that looked like a fist — and when he crossed into Thai airspace he let out a breath he hadn't known he was holding.

The engine quit over the paddy fields.

Not dramatically. It just stopped, the way a conversation stops when there's nothing left to say. The propeller windmilled and then slowed and the aircraft became a very expensive glider with a very short glide path. He had maybe thirty seconds and a paddy field directly ahead and he used the thirty seconds to set up the best approach he could manage.

The Skyraider hit the mud at about ninety knots and kept going.

It went through the near berm and into the next paddy and threw up a wall of brown water on both sides and the Colonel held the stick forward and kept the wings level and let the muck do the work of stopping him. The aircraft slewed left and he corrected and it slewed right and he let it go and it came to rest nose-down in the far berm with the tail in the air and the cockpit full of the smell of hot metal and wet earth.

He sat there for a moment. The ticking of the cooling engine. Water draining somewhere. His hands were still on the controls.

He climbed out. His legs worked. That was the first thing to establish. He stood in the paddy field up to his knees in water and looked back at the aircraft and the long dark scar it had cut through the mud, and then he looked toward the airbase, which was close enough that he could see the control tower.

A jeep was already coming.

---

The debrief was short. The Colonel had been in enough of them to know what they needed and what they didn't, and he gave them what they needed in the order they needed it. Ground fire, single round, oil system, engine failure, border crossing, forced landing. No injuries. Aircraft a total loss.

The operations officer nodded through all of it and then said, "We've got a replacement on the ramp."

"How long."

"Thirty minutes. Maybe less."

The Colonel looked at the clock on the wall. He thought about the Bird Dog still circling over the karst. He thought about the two men on the ground in the limestone country, the PJ and the pilot, moving south along the drainage in failing light.

"Make it less," he said.

He walked out to the flight line and the replacement Skyraider was already being armed. The ordnance crew worked without being told, hanging the ordnance in the sequence he preferred, and he stood and watched them and drank the coffee someone had put in his hand and did not think about the paddy field or the ticking of the cooling engine or the way his hands had stayed on the controls after the aircraft stopped moving.

There were things you thought about later. There were things you thought about never. The trick was knowing which was which.

The crew chief gave him the thumbs up. He put the coffee down and climbed up the wing and settled into the cockpit and ran through the checklist and the engine caught on the first try, that big radial turning over with the sound of something that meant business.

He taxied to the runway.

Sandy One-Five was back in the rotation.
