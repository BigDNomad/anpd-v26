They came through the undergrowth without warning, the way things always come when you've stopped expecting them.

Archer heard the brush move to his left and he was already turning, the .45 coming up, and the man was right there — close enough that Archer could see the sweat on his face and the way his eyes went wide when he registered what he was looking at. The .45 fired once. The man sat down hard against a root mass and did not move again.

The second man was a half-step behind the first, off Archer's right shoulder, and he was bringing his AK around when Coyle shot him. The .38 cracked twice in the close air under the canopy. The man went down sideways into the undergrowth and the jungle took him.

Neither of them moved for a moment.

Coyle was propped against the limestone with the revolver still extended, both hands on it, the muzzle tracking the space where the second man had been. His breathing was audible. His color was better than it had been — the transfusion had done something — but he was still not a man who should be shooting at anything.

Archer crossed to the second man and confirmed what he already knew. He came back and crouched beside Coyle.

"You good," he said.

It wasn't a question. Coyle lowered the revolver.

"Yeah," Coyle said.

Archer looked at the first man. Pathet Lao, not NVA. Local militia by the look of the kit — mismatched web gear, sandals cut from tire rubber, the AK worn and taped at the stock. A flank pair, which meant they were part of something larger working the area. The patrol that had passed through earlier, maybe, or a second element he hadn't accounted for.

He took the AK and checked it. Magazine seated, round chambered, selector on semi. He slung it over his good shoulder.

"They'll have heard that," Coyle said.

"Yes."

"How far do you think—"

"Far enough that they won't know exactly where. Not close enough that it doesn't matter." Archer was already moving, checking the second man's kit. Another AK, this one with a cracked receiver he didn't trust. He left it. Took the spare magazines from the man's chest rig. "We need to move."

Coyle got himself upright against the limestone. It cost him something. He stood there a moment with his jaw set, getting his legs under him.

The jungle had gone quiet the way it does after a shot — everything holding still, waiting to see what happens next. Then, slowly, the insects started again. A bird somewhere above the canopy. The ordinary sounds coming back in, which meant nothing large was moving toward them yet.

Archer helped Coyle get the pack situated. The man was steadier than he had any right to be. Whatever the blood had done for him, it had done enough for now.

"Drainage is east," Archer said. "We stay off the ridge."

"The LZ the Bird Dog spotted."

"Grid two-seven-four. We move slow and we move quiet and we don't stop unless we have to."

Coyle looked at the two men on the ground. He didn't say anything for a moment. There was something in his face that Archer recognized — not regret exactly, not guilt, but the specific weight a man carries when he's added to a count he's been keeping for a long time.

"First time in a while," Coyle said. "Up close like that."

"Don't think about it now."

"I'm not." He looked away from the bodies. "I'm really not."

Archer believed him. That was almost worse.

They moved out through the undergrowth, Archer on point with the AK, Coyle a step behind and to his right. The limestone formations rose around them in the failing light, pale and indifferent, the karst doing what karst does — offering nothing, demanding everything. The drainage was maybe four hundred meters east if the Bird Dog pilot had his landmarks right. Four hundred meters in this terrain could be an hour's work.

Archer kept his pace slow and deliberate. His shoulder pulled with every step, the joint grinding where it had no business grinding, but the arm was functional and the AK was light enough that he could manage it. He kept his eyes moving — the middle distance, the near ground, the canopy breaks where light came through and silhouetted anything moving above.

Behind him, Coyle moved without complaint. The man had been a pilot for a long time and pilots learn to manage fear the way you manage fuel — you account for it, you don't pretend it isn't there, and you don't let it make your decisions for you.

The jungle closed in around them and the light continued to go.

Somewhere to the northwest, in the direction the patrol had moved, there was nothing. No voices, no movement, no indication that anyone had heard the shots or was responding to them.

That would change. It always changed.

They kept moving south toward the drainage.
