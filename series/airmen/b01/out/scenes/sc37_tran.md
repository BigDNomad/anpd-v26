The NVA trucks came in from the north track, two of them, canvas-sided, and the Pathet Lao sentries at the perimeter wire watched them come without moving to stop them. The lead truck stopped short of the command post and Captain Tran Van Khoa stepped down from the cab before the engine quit. He did not look at the sentries. He walked to the center of the compound as if he had been here before and knew where everything was.

His detail came out of the second truck. Six men, NVA regulars, carrying their weapons at the low ready. They spread without being told.

The Pathet Lao sergeant who ran the base camp in Bounchanh's absence came out of the command post and stood in the doorway. He was a careful man and he took his time reading what he was looking at.

Tran stopped in front of him and produced a document folded in thirds. He held it out and waited while the sergeant read it. The sergeant read it twice.

"Major Bounchanh is in the field," the sergeant said.

"I know where the major is," Tran said. "The mandate is from Hanoi. It does not require the major's presence to be valid."

The sergeant looked at the document again. He looked at the six NVA soldiers standing in his compound. He handed the document back.

"What do you need," he said.

"The command post. Your radio operator stays. Everyone else outside."

The sergeant gave the order and his men filed out. They did not go far. They stood in groups of two and three near the perimeter and near the cook fire and they watched the NVA soldiers without speaking. The compliance was real enough. The resentment underneath it was also real, and Tran noted both without concern. He had worked with Pathet Lao before. They were useful in their territory and unreliable outside it and they did not like being reminded of the difference.

He set his map case on the table and unrolled the topographic sheet, weighting the corners with a compass and a canteen. The radio operator, a young man with a careful face, sat at his set and did not look at Tran directly.

Tran spread the intercept reports beside the map. Three separate cache reports, two of them partial, one nearly complete. He had read them on the truck coming in and he read them again now with the map in front of him.

The reported movement corridor ran northeast from the crash site, following the drainage, then cutting up through the karst toward the ridge. He traced it with one finger. The distances were right for two men moving carefully. The timing was consistent with the ejection window. But the weight estimates in the second report — a patrol leader's field assessment, imprecise but not useless — described two men moving at a pace that did not fit men carrying anything substantial. Not rotor hardware. Not eight of them.

He stood back from the map.

The rotors were not with the men. Or they had not been with the men at that point in the movement. Which meant they had been cached somewhere along the route, or left at the crash site, or — and this was the version that concerned him most — separated from the pilot before the patrol ever picked up the trail.

He did not write this down. He held it.

Behind him, he heard the radio operator key his handset. A short transmission, low, in Lao. Tran did not turn around. He listened to the transmission end and the static resume and he continued studying the map.

He knew what the man had done. He had transmitted to Bounchanh in the field. Reported the NVA arrival, the document, the takeover of the command post. It was what Tran would have done in the same position. A soldier's loyalty ran to his own commander first, and the Pathet Lao had not asked for Hanoi's help and did not particularly want it.

Tran turned from the map.

The radio operator was sitting with his hands in his lap, looking at the set.

"What frequency does the major monitor in the field," Tran said.

The operator told him.

"Good," Tran said, and turned back to the map.

He found the drainage marked at grid two-seven-four. The limestone pushed the tree line apart there and left a gap that would show from the air. If the Americans were moving east from the karst, that was where they were going. Not because it was the best ground. Because it was the only ground that gave a helicopter room to work.

He put his finger on the grid and left it there.

The rotors were somewhere between the crash site and that clearing. Or they were already in American hands and moving toward the border. Either way, the men on the ground were the thread. Find the men, find the rotors, or confirm they were gone and report accordingly to Hanoi.

He called his sergeant in from outside.

"I need a guide who knows the drainage east of the limestone face," he said. "And I need him in ten minutes."
