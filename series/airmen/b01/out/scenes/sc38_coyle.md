The drainage was maybe two hundred meters ahead. Archer could hear it — water moving over rock, steady and low — and he had Coyle's arm across his good shoulder and they were making time, not good time but time, picking through the limestone rubble where the karst had shed itself in plates and chunks across the jungle floor.

Coyle was moving under his own power now, mostly. The transfusion had done something. His color was wrong still, the gray not fully gone from his face, but his legs were working and he wasn't dead weight the way he'd been. He kept his right hand pressed to his side where the shrapnel had gone in. He didn't complain about it.

They'd left the hollow twenty minutes back. The militia pair was still there, in the undergrowth where Archer had dragged them. Someone would find them. Maybe already had. The shots had been close and the jungle carried sound in ways that were hard to predict, bouncing it off the limestone faces, swallowing it in the canopy, delivering it somewhere unexpected. Archer didn't know what the shots had told anyone. He knew they needed to move and they were moving.

The ground dropped slightly toward the drainage and the vegetation thickened, which was cover and also noise, branches catching at them, roots crossing the path. Archer was watching the ridgeline to his left where the gun positions were. Nothing moving up there that he could see. The Bird Dog was still circling somewhere above the overcast, engine note faint and intermittent.

Coyle stumbled on a root and Archer caught him and they stopped for a moment, Coyle getting his feet back under him, breathing through his teeth.

"I've got it," Coyle said.

"I know you do."

They started moving again.

The sound came from the right. Not the drainage. Something else. A branch, the specific sound of a branch moved by intention rather than wind, and then the mechanical sound underneath it, small and distinct, a bolt being drawn back and seated.

Archer heard it a half-beat late.

Coyle heard it first.

He didn't think about it. His hand came off his side and he grabbed Archer's collar and pulled him down and sideways and the burst came through the space where Archer's head and chest had been, cutting leaves, snapping a branch, the rounds going high and left and into the limestone face beyond them with a sound like hammers on stone.

They were both down in the undergrowth. Archer had the revolver up. He couldn't see the shooter. The muzzle flash had been in the tree line to the right, maybe thirty meters, and he put two rounds into the base of the trees where a man would be standing and heard something fall.

Silence.

He waited. Coyle was flat beside him, not moving, his breathing controlled and shallow. Archer scanned the tree line. Nothing else moved. One man, maybe. A point element, separated from whatever was behind him.

He waited another thirty seconds. The jungle gave him nothing.

He looked at Coyle. Coyle was looking back at him. There was blood on Coyle's hand from where he'd grabbed the collar, his wound reopened or just the old blood on his palm, hard to tell. His face was tight with pain he wasn't going to say anything about.

Archer had been carrying him since the hollow. Through the karst, through the undergrowth, through the patrol that had passed too close. He'd put the catheter line in and given him blood out of his own arm and he'd killed two men in the dark with a knife and he'd kept them moving east toward the drainage and the LZ and the possibility of getting out of this.

And Coyle had just pulled him out of a burst that would have taken him through the throat.

Neither of them said anything about it. There was nothing to say. The accounting between them was its own thing, and it didn't need words, and words would have made it smaller than it was.

Archer got up first, checked the tree line one more time, then helped Coyle to his feet. Coyle got his arm back across Archer's shoulder. His grip was stronger than it had been. Not strong, but stronger.

"Drainage is close," Archer said.

"I can hear it."

They moved. The undergrowth thinned as the ground dropped toward the water and the limestone pushed the trees apart the way the Bird Dog pilot had described, a gap in the canopy that from above would look like a clearing, which was what they needed it to look like.

Archer kept his eyes on the tree line to the right. Whatever had been there was down. He didn't go to check. They didn't have time to check.

The drainage opened up ahead of them, water running clear over flat rock, and beyond it the gap in the trees where the sky came through gray and low and wide enough.

Wide enough.
