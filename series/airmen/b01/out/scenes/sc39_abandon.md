Coyle said it quietly, the way a man states a fact he's already made peace with.

"Take the rotors and go."

Archer looked at him.

"I mean it." Coyle's voice was barely above breath. "You know what's in that bag. You know what happens if the wrong people get their hands on it. One pilot against every road watch team between here and the DMZ — that's not a hard calculation."

"It's not my calculation to make."

"I'm making it for you."

"No," Archer said. "You're not."

They were pressed into the drainage cut, the limestone face at their backs, the jungle close on three sides. Coyle's color was better than it had been but better wasn't good. He had one hand flat against the rock to keep himself upright. The bag with the rotors was between them.

"Listen to me," Coyle said. "I've been in this business long enough to know how the math works. The mission doesn't end when the man gets home. Sometimes the mission is the thing, not the man."

"That's not my creed."

"Your creed is a nice idea."

"It's the only idea I've got," Archer said. "That others may live. That's not a suggestion."

Coyle looked at him. The kid's face was close enough that Coyle could see the dried blood at his hairline, the set of his jaw. He'd seen that expression before, on men who'd decided something and weren't going to be talked back from it. He'd worn it himself, once, a long time ago in a different kind of war.

"You're going to get us both killed holding onto me."

"Maybe," Archer said. "But I'm not leaving you."

Coyle opened his mouth to press it further and Archer's hand came up — not a gesture, just a flat stop — and both men went still at the same moment.

Boots on stone. Above them, on the shelf of rock that jutted out over the drainage, maybe eight feet up. The scrape of a sole finding purchase, then another. Two men, moving without urgency, which was worse than if they'd been moving fast. Fast meant they were reacting. Slow meant they were looking.

Low voices in Lao. A question, an answer. The tone of men doing a job they'd done before, not alarmed, not relaxed either. The boots moved along the shelf, east to west, and a small cascade of grit came down from the lip above them and landed on Coyle's shoulder and neither man moved.

The voices again. Closer now. One of the men stopped directly above them — Archer could track it by the sound, the particular silence of a man standing still — and then the other man said something short and the first man answered and the boots resumed their movement.

West. Then northwest.

The sound of them faded into the jungle and did not come back.

Archer waited. Coyle waited. The Bird Dog was somewhere above the overcast, invisible, its engine a faint and distant thing that might have been wind.

When Archer was certain, he let out a slow breath through his nose.

Coyle didn't restart the argument. He looked at Archer for a moment and then looked away, at the drainage, at the gap in the canopy where the limestone had pushed the trees apart and left a ragged slot of grey sky. The alternate LZ was four hundred meters east along this same cut. He knew it. Archer knew it.

The rotors were in the bag between them. Eight cipher wheels that could unravel every American asset in northern Laos if they fell to the wrong hands. Coyle had pulled them from the nav station himself, had carried them through the ejection and the crash and the jungle and the blood loss, and now a twenty-something PJ with a bad shoulder and a dead man's revolver was telling him that none of that changed the math.

He thought about arguing it again. He thought about the crew chief, who was dead, and the Black Widow, which was burning wreckage in a Laotian valley, and the road watch teams whose names he knew and whose faces he'd never see. He thought about what it cost to be the thing that others may live was written for.

He didn't argue it again.

What he registered, finally, was that the kid wasn't being stubborn. Stubborn was what you called it when a man was wrong and wouldn't move. This was something else. This was a man who had arrived at the only answer available to him and was standing in it the way you stand in ground you've decided to hold.

Coyle picked up the bag.

Archer checked the drainage east, checked the canopy gap, checked the revolver. His shoulder moved with the stiffness of something that had been damaged and was working anyway.

"Four hundred meters," Archer said.

"I know," Coyle said.

They moved.
