The village came through the trees in pieces — a roof line, a gap in the canopy where smoke had once risen, the pale vertical of a bamboo post. Archer stopped and Coyle stopped behind him, and they stood at the tree line and watched.

Four structures. Maybe five. The kind of village that didn't appear on any map Archer had ever seen. Raised platforms, thatch, the whole thing built to be left behind when the season changed or the war moved closer. No movement at the entrances. No cooking fire. The afternoon light came flat through the canopy and lay across the packed earth between the huts without illuminating anything useful.

Coyle's breathing had gone shallow in the last hour. Not labored, just reduced, like a man conserving something he wasn't sure he had enough of. His responses when Archer checked him were a beat slow. He'd stopped asking questions, which was its own kind of answer.

Archer watched the village for two minutes. Then he watched it for two more.

The hut on the near side sat slightly apart from the others. No light inside. The woven mat at the entrance hung straight and undisturbed. No sound from it, no smell of food or fire.

He turned to Coyle and pointed to a position behind a root mass at the jungle edge. Coyle understood. He moved to it without being told twice and settled against the roots with the revolver in his lap, muzzle toward the village, and his back against the wood.

Archer went in low and fast across the open ground.

The mat at the entrance was dry. He pushed through it and stopped just inside, letting his eyes adjust. The interior was small — a sleeping mat rolled against one wall, a clay pot, a wooden shelf with a bowl on it. The bowl held rice, cooked and cooling, enough for two men if neither of them was particular about portions. He didn't touch anything else. He took the bowl in both hands, careful with his shoulder, and came back out through the mat and across the open ground and into the trees.

No alarm. No sound from the village at all.

Coyle looked at the bowl and then looked at Archer and didn't say anything. They sat at the jungle edge with the village in front of them and ate with their fingers, fast and without conversation, eyes moving across the huts and the spaces between them. The rice was plain and had gone cold but it was food and Coyle ate his share and then some, and Archer let him.

When the bowl was empty Archer set it in the dirt beside him. His shoulder had stiffened during the crossing and he worked it carefully, rotating the joint through what range it would allow, which wasn't much.

The village stayed quiet.

Coyle wiped his hand on his flight suit. Some color had come back into his face during the eating, or maybe it was the light changing. Either way he looked less like a man who was deciding whether to lie down.

They stayed at the tree line a little longer than they needed to. Not long. Just long enough.

What Archer didn't know, and couldn't have known from where he stood, was that the hut had not been empty. The woman had been in the back corner, behind the partition of woven grass that divided the sleeping space from the rest, and she had heard the mat move and heard the particular silence of a man trying not to make sound, which is a different silence than the absence of sound. She had not moved. She had not called out. She had stayed where she was with her knees drawn up and her back against the wall and listened to the bowl being lifted from the shelf, and then she had listened to the footsteps cross the packed earth and go back through the mat, and then she had listened to nothing.

She did not know what the men were. She knew what they were not. She had heard the Pathet Lao move through the village twice in the past day, and she knew the sound of that, the particular weight of it, the way it pressed the air down. This had been something else.

She stayed where she was for a long time after the footsteps were gone.

Outside, Archer and Coyle moved back into the trees and the village fell behind them, and the jungle closed over the gap where the village had been, and the afternoon light continued its slow work on the canopy above. The Bird Dog was still up there somewhere, circling, patient as a hawk that has learned to wait. The drainage cut ran east ahead of them, and the alternate LZ was four hundred meters along it, and Coyle was moving under his own power, which was what mattered.

The bowl sat in the dirt at the jungle edge where Archer had left it.

The woman in the hut did not come out to retrieve it.

Not yet.
