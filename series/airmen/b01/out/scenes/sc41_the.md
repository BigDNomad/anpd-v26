He heard her before he saw her. Footsteps on packed earth, unhurried, coming along the path from the tree line where the village garden plots had gone to weed. He went still against the trunk of a dipterocarp at the jungle's edge, the revolver in his right hand, shoulder screaming at the angle.

She came out of the undergrowth carrying a length of green bamboo with a fish laid across it, roasted, the skin split and dark. She was maybe forty, maybe older, wearing an indigo sinh and a work blouse faded to the color of river clay. She walked the way people walk when they have walked that path ten thousand times and expect nothing different at the end of it.

Then she saw him.

She stopped. Not the startled stop of someone surprised — the deliberate stop of someone who has already decided what to do and is waiting to see if the situation requires a different decision. Her eyes went to the revolver. Then to his face. Then back to the revolver.

Archer did not move. He did not raise the weapon and he did not lower it.

She looked at him for a long moment. The jungle behind her was quiet. No voices, no movement, nothing following her down the path. She looked at the fish on the bamboo. Then she looked at him again and nodded once, a small precise movement, and held the bamboo out toward him.

He took it with his left hand. The fish was still warm.

She turned and walked back the way she had come, through the garden plots and into the undergrowth, and did not look back.

Archer stood at the jungle edge until she was gone. Then he moved.

---

Coyle was where he'd left him, sitting with his back against a limestone face in the shadow of the canopy, the bag between his knees. He looked up when Archer came through the brush.

"What's that."

"Fish."

Coyle looked at it. "Where."

"Village woman. She handed it to me."

Coyle didn't say anything to that. He took the bamboo when Archer offered it and broke the fish apart with his fingers, working carefully, and they ate in the green half-dark without speaking. The fish was river fish, small-boned, cooked over coals with something in the skin that had caramelized and gone bitter at the edges. It was the best thing Archer had eaten in two days.

He watched Coyle eat. The man's hands had steadied. He was pulling the flesh from the bones with some deliberateness now, not the fumbling of before. When he finished his half he wiped his fingers on his flight suit and sat up straighter against the limestone.

"How far to the drainage," Coyle said.

"Three hundred meters. Maybe less."

"Then we should move."

His voice was clearer. Not strong, but present in a way it hadn't been. The blood had done some of it. The food had done the rest. Archer filed this away the way he filed everything — not with relief, because relief was a thing you felt when you were safe, and they were not safe — but as information. Coyle could move. Coyle could think. That changed the math on what came next.

He thought about the woman. She had known what he was. Not American specifically, maybe, but not Pathet Lao, not NVA, not anyone she was supposed to help. She had a fish and she had given it to him and she had walked away. No calculation he could make explained that fully. People in a war zone learned to read the wind, and most of them read it toward whoever had the most guns in the immediate vicinity. She had read something else.

He didn't know what she had seen in him that made her hand over the fish. He didn't know what she stood to lose if someone had been watching. He knew that she had made a choice and that the choice had cost her something, even if the cost was only the risk of it, and that she had made it without asking for anything back.

He let the thought go. There was no use in it. The drainage was three hundred meters east and the Bird Dog was still up there somewhere above the canopy and the gun positions on the ridge were still manned and the patrol that had gone northwest was still out there working its way back. The woman had given them time. That was what it amounted to. Not safety. Time.

"Up," Archer said.

Coyle got his feet under him and stood. He swayed once and then steadied, one hand on the limestone. He picked up the bag.

They moved east along the drainage, staying low, keeping the limestone between them and the ridge. The canopy closed over them. Behind them the village was quiet, the huts standing empty in the afternoon light, the cook fire gone cold.
