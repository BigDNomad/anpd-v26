The jeep came off the perimeter road and onto the flight line without slowing, the driver threading between a fuel bowser and a stack of ordnance crates before the Colonel told him to stop. He was out before the vehicle fully stopped moving.

The replacement Skyraider sat on the ramp where Sandy had left it, armed and ready. The Colonel walked past it without looking at it. He was looking at the second one, parked two hardstands down, a crew chief doing a pre-flight walk-around with a clipboard.

"Who owns this aircraft," the Colonel said.

The crew chief looked up. "Sir, she's assigned to—"

"Not anymore." He turned to the ordnance sergeant standing near the weapons cart. "What's she carrying?"

"Standard RESCAP load, sir. Guns, rockets, two external tanks."

"Pull the tanks. Give me napalm. Two canisters minimum."

The sergeant looked at the crew chief. The crew chief looked at his clipboard.

"Sir," the sergeant said, "that's going to take—"

"How long."

"Forty minutes. Maybe thirty-five."

"You've got twenty-five." The Colonel was already walking toward the operations building. "And don't touch the guns or the rockets."

The sergeant didn't say anything else. Neither did the crew chief. They started moving.

The base commander met the Colonel at the operations building door. Colonel Whitfield was a careful man, the kind who kept his desk organized and his paperwork current, and he had the look now of a man who had been careful his whole career and was watching it matter less than he'd expected.

"Colonel," Whitfield said. "I wasn't notified you were coming."

"I'm here now."

They went inside. The duty officer and two clerks found reasons to be elsewhere in the room.

"I need a second helicopter assigned to the Jolly Green package," the Colonel said. "Whatever you've got on the line."

Whitfield set his hands flat on the map table. "The package is already authorized. One Jolly Green, two Sandys, the Bird Dog. That's the approved configuration for a single-survivor CSAR."

"There are two men on the ground."

"The authorization covers—"

"I know what the authorization covers." The Colonel picked up the grease pencil from the table and set it back down. "I'm not asking you to rewrite the authorization. I'm telling you to assign a second helicopter."

"That requires a request through Seventh Air Force. There's a process."

"The process," the Colonel said, "is that I'm standing in your operations building telling you what I need. That's the process."

Whitfield looked at him. He was not a stupid man. He understood what was in the room with him and what wasn't, and what wasn't in the room was anyone who was going to back his play if he pushed this further.

"The second Jolly Green is on a maintenance hold," Whitfield said. "Hydraulic inspection."

"Is she flyable."

A pause. "Technically."

"Then she's off the hold." The Colonel moved to the door. "I want her crewed and on the ramp in forty minutes. She flies as a backup. She doesn't go in unless the primary takes fire or goes down."

"And if something goes wrong with this," Whitfield said. "If we lose aircraft, if there's an incident—"

"Then it happened in Thailand," the Colonel said. "Same as always."

He went back out to the flight line. The ordnance crew was already pulling the external tanks off the second Skyraider, working fast, not talking. The napalm canisters sat on the weapons cart, fat and dull in the afternoon light. One of the armorers was checking the fusing on the first canister with the focused attention of a man who understood what he was handling.

The Colonel stood and watched them work. The heat came off the tarmac in slow waves. Somewhere on the far end of the field a transport was running up its engines, the sound rising and then leveling off.

He thought about what was on the ground out there. Two men moving through karst and jungle with enemy patrols working the same ground. One of them a PJ with a bad shoulder and a revolver. The other carrying something that could unravel every American asset between the Mekong and the DMZ if it fell into the wrong hands.

He didn't think about the second thing often. He thought about the first thing, which was that a man had gone down to get another man out and was still there, and the helicopter that was supposed to bring them both home had come back without either of them.

That was the thing that mattered. The other thing was the reason he'd gotten on the jeep and driven to the flight line himself instead of making phone calls, but it was not the thing that was going to keep him standing here until those napalm canisters were hung and the second helicopter was crewed and the whole package was ready to go back across the fence.

The armorer finished the fusing check and moved to the second canister.

The Colonel stayed where he was.
