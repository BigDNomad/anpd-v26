The Colonel stood at the operations table with the grease pencil still in his hand and looked at the map for a long time without marking anything on it.

Whitfield was behind him. The Colonel could hear him breathing.

"You understand what he's carrying," the Colonel said.

"I understand it."

"Say it back to me."

Whitfield was quiet a moment. "Eight KL-7 rotors. MACV-SOG network traffic, northern Laos. Air America cover identities, 404 personnel, road watch teams. Everything between the Mekong and the DMZ."

"And the other thing."

"The modifications to the Black Widow." Whitfield's voice had gone flat. "The fire control integration. The targeting system. He designed it. He field-tested it."

"He's the only man alive who knows both things," the Colonel said. "The crew chief is dead. The aircraft is down. That leaves Coyle."

He set the grease pencil on the table.

"If Tran gets him to Hanoi, we lose the rotor network inside of a week. Every asset we have running north of Vientiane gets rolled up. Road watch teams, the 404 people, all of it. And then they spend the next year reverse-engineering what we did to that gunship." He paused. "That's not a risk I can accept."

"He's still alive," Whitfield said. "Archer's with him. They're moving."

"I know they're moving."

"Then let the rescue package work the problem. Sandy's back up. The second Jolly Green is—"

"The second Jolly Green is a recovery option," the Colonel said. "Not a rescue."

The room was quiet. Outside on the flight line a wrench rang against metal and someone called out a number and the sound died.

Whitfield said, "You're talking about killing one of our own people."

"I'm talking about the calculus." The Colonel turned from the table. His face was the same as it had been when he was looking at the map. "Coyle knows what he signed. He knew it when he took the rotors off that aircraft. He knew it when he didn't destroy them."

"He was trying to get them out."

"He was. And that was the right call until it wasn't." The Colonel moved to the window. The second Skyraider sat on the ramp with the ordnance crew working the wing stations, the napalm canisters going up clean and even. "If he makes the LZ and we get him out, we're fine. The package goes in, Sandy runs the RESCAP, we pull both of them. That's the first option and it stays the first option until it's no longer viable."

"And when it's no longer viable."

"Then Sandy gets a different set of instructions."

Whitfield didn't say anything.

"The napalm isn't for the LZ," the Colonel said. "It's for the contingency. If Tran's people get to them before we do, if they're moving Coyle north, if there's a column and he's in it—" He stopped. "Sandy knows how to read a target."

"Sandy doesn't know what he'd be shooting at."

"Sandy will know what he's shooting at. He just won't know why." The Colonel came back to the table and picked up the grease pencil. "That's how this works. That's always how this works."

Whitfield crossed his arms and looked at the floor. He was a careful man and he had been in this business long enough to know that careful men sometimes arrived at the same place as reckless ones, just by a longer route.

"The PJ," he said. "Archer."

"Archer is doing his job. If he gets Coyle to that LZ, everybody goes home." The Colonel drew a line on the map, the drainage cut east to grid two-seven-four. "But Archer doesn't know about the rotors. He doesn't know what Coyle's carrying or what it means. He's running a rescue because that's what PJs do."

"And if the contingency goes active while Archer's still with him."

The Colonel looked at the line he'd drawn. He didn't answer right away, and in the space of that silence something settled in the room that had not been there before. Not conflict. Not doubt. Something more like the weight of a thing that has already been decided and is only now being spoken aloud.

"Then Archer is in the wrong place," the Colonel said. "And I'm sorry for it."

He capped the grease pencil.

"Get Sandy on the radio when he's airborne. Standard RESCAP brief. Don't deviate from it." He looked at Whitfield. "If I need to pass the contingency instructions, I'll do it myself. Direct. You won't be on that frequency."

Whitfield nodded once. It was not agreement. It was the nod of a man who understands that the conversation is over.

The Colonel turned back to the window. On the ramp the ordnance crew was finishing up, the last canister locked and pinned, and the crew chief was walking the wing with his checklist, touching each station in turn, doing the work the way the work was supposed to be done.
