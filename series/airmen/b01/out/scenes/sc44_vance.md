The replacement bird sat on the ramp with its port door open and the crew moving around it in the flat morning light. Someone had already run the preflight. Fuel lines were connected and disconnected. A crew chief was on his back under the tail rotor assembly with a flashlight.

Vance came across the tarmac with the ruck over his good shoulder.

He'd requisitioned it from the aid station without signing for it. The corpsman on duty had looked at the bandaging on Vance's arm and the way he was holding himself and had not said a word about the paperwork.

He climbed in through the port door and set the ruck on the deck and unzipped it and started over from the beginning.

The IV lines were there. He pulled them out anyway and checked the packaging on each one, looking for any seal that had been compromised, any bag that had taken a pinhole from rough handling. He set them aside in order. Plasma expanders next — two units of dextran, one of albumin, the albumin wrapped in a secondary bag because someone somewhere had learned that lesson the hard way. He checked the dates. He checked them again.

The crew gave him room. Nobody asked what he was doing. Nobody offered to help. Evans came to the door once, looked in, and walked away without speaking. That was the right call and Vance noted it without gratitude or resentment. It was simply the correct thing.

He worked through the surgical kit. Scalpel handles, blades still sealed, curved needles on the suture cards, hemostats, retractors he probably wouldn't need but packed anyway because the weight was nothing and the cost of not having them was everything. He'd done a chest in the field once with less than this. He didn't want to do it again but he packed for it.

The trauma dressings went in last, layered by size, the pressure bandages on top because those were the ones you needed first and you needed them fast and you did not want to be digging for them while a man was bleeding out on the jungle floor. He knew what Archer was dealing with. He knew the shoulder, knew the limited arm, knew the way a man compensates for that kind of injury and how the compensation creates its own problems over time. He knew the blood loss Coyle had already taken. He knew the terrain they were moving through because he'd been over it on the maps and he'd been over it in the air before the hoist and he'd seen the karst from above and understood what it meant to be inside it on foot.

He could not go back in. That was the fact of it. The arm wouldn't hold a hoist line and the Colonel had been clear and the Colonel was right, which made it worse rather than better. A man can argue with a wrong decision. There is nothing to argue with when the decision is correct.

So he packed the ruck.

He added a catheter kit because Archer had already used one and might need another. He added citrate solution. He added a second tourniquet because the one Archer was carrying was already deployed and a man in the field with one tourniquet is a man who has used his margin. He added morphine syrettes, three of them, and wrote the count on the inside flap of the ruck in grease pencil so whoever opened it would know what was there without having to inventory it under fire.

He thought about Archer's blood type and Coyle's blood type and the fact that the transfusion had already happened and had held and that Archer was O-negative and had given what he could give and was now running on less than he'd started with and still moving through limestone karst with a bad shoulder and a dead man's revolver and whatever was left of his legs.

Vance had trained Archer. Not all of it — nobody trains all of it — but enough. He knew how the man moved and how he thought and how he made decisions under pressure. He knew Archer would not leave the pilot. He'd known that before the hoist, before the fall, before any of this. It was not a flaw. It was the thing that made Archer what he was, and it was the thing that was going to get him killed if the package didn't get back in there.

He zipped the ruck.

He checked the zipper pull. He checked the shoulder straps. He lifted it once, felt the weight, set it back down on the deck in the position a man would find it when he climbed in and needed it fast.

Then he stepped back from the bird.

He didn't watch the crew. He didn't watch the ramp or the sky to the east or the operations building where the Colonel and Whitfield were working the problem. He turned and walked back across the tarmac toward the aid station because there was another ruck to build and the second Jolly Green was coming off hold and someone needed to pack for that one too.
