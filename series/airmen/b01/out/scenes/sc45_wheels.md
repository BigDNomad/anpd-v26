The replacement Skyraider sat on the ramp with its engine already turning, props cutting the morning air into something you could feel in your back teeth. Sandy climbed in without ceremony. The ground crew pulled the chocks and he taxied without waiting for the tower's full clearance, just the acknowledgment, and then he was rolling.

Jolly Green Two-Two lifted from the pad behind him, the big rotor thudding against the heat, Vance visible in the open door with his bandaged arm braced against the frame. The second Jolly, the one they'd pulled off hydraulic inspection hold, came up thirty seconds later, heavier, slower, its crew still running through abbreviated checks as it cleared the tree line.

Sandy didn't wait for either of them.

He pushed the throttle and the Skyraider answered the way it always did, that big radial engine pulling hard, and he came around east and climbed. The mountains were already there in the windscreen, blue-gray and stacked, the karst rising out of the jungle floor like something that had no interest in being navigated. He'd seen them a hundred times. They looked the same every time.

Behind him, Jolly Green Two-Two was a shape getting smaller.

That was the geometry of it. The Skyraider cruised at better than two-fifty. The HH-3E did a hundred and sixty on a good day with a clean airframe and favorable wind, and today it had neither. The gap between them would widen the whole way in, and that was the point. Sandy's job was to be on station before the helicopter arrived, to have the picture, to have already talked to Bird Dog, to know where the guns were and where they weren't. The helicopter arrived into a situation Sandy had already shaped. That was how it worked when it worked.

He crossed the Mekong at altitude, the river brown and wide below him, and then he was over Laos and the jungle closed in underneath and the karst started coming up through the green. He checked his ordnance. The napalm canisters hung under the wings where the external tanks usually rode. He'd noted that when he climbed in. The Colonel had made a choice about what this mission needed, and it wasn't extra range.

Sandy keyed his radio and raised Bird Dog.

"Sandy One-Five, this is Sandy. Say status."

The Bird Dog pilot's voice came back with some static in it, the karst doing what it always did to transmissions. "Sandy, Bird Dog. Still circling. No change on the ground. Two men moving east along the drainage, last visual. Gun positions on the ridge are cold but they're there. I've got movement on the north slope, looks like a patrol working back south."

"Copy all," Sandy said. "ETA approximately twenty minutes. Keep them in sight if you can."

"Doing my best. Canopy's thick in the drainage."

Sandy acknowledged and went back to flying. The mountains were filling the windscreen now, the limestone faces catching the morning light and throwing it back white and hard. He picked his entry point, the gap he'd used before, and started his descent.

Behind him, somewhere over the Mekong, Jolly Green Two-Two was still climbing to cruise altitude. The second Jolly was behind that. They would come in sequence, the helicopter slower and more vulnerable, needing the situation to be managed before it arrived. That was what Sandy was for.

He thought about the man on the ground. A PJ with a bad shoulder and a dead man's revolver and a pilot who was carrying something the Colonel wouldn't name but had armed him with napalm to protect. He'd flown RESCAP for PJs before. They were a specific kind of person. They went down the hoist into situations that had already chewed up the man they were going to recover, and they did it because that was what they did, and they didn't stop doing it because it got complicated.

This one had fallen sixty feet through jungle canopy and was still moving.

Sandy leveled off and the karst rose around him on both sides, the limestone walls close enough that he could see the texture of them, the cracks and ledges where the jungle found purchase and grew sideways out of the rock. The drainage was down there somewhere. The alternate LZ was down there. The two men were down there.

He checked his guns. He checked his ordnance again.

The Colonel had given him contingency orders that Whitfield didn't know about. Sandy had taken them without comment because that was the chain of command and because he understood what the rotors meant if they reached Hanoi. He understood it and he didn't like it and he filed it in the part of his mind where he kept things he would deal with when he had to deal with them.

Right now there were two men on the ground and a patrol working back south toward them and gun positions on the ridge that had already killed one aircraft.

He came around the last limestone face and the drainage opened up below him, the canopy broken where the rock had pushed the trees apart, and Bird Dog was there, circling tight, and Sandy keyed his radio.

"Bird Dog, Sandy is on station."
