The radio operator handed Tran the handset and stepped back.

Static, then Bounchanh's voice came through flat and clipped. "You are at the camp."

"I am," Tran said. "And you are in the field with men positioned to engage American aircraft."

"That is correct."

"Stand them down."

A pause. Not long. "The helicopters are in my airspace. My men have angles on the approach corridor. This is what we do."

Tran kept his voice level. "What you do is what Hanoi directs. I have the document. You have seen the document."

"I have seen a document," Bounchanh said. "Carried by a Vietnamese officer who arrived at my camp and occupied my command post."

"The pilot and whatever he is carrying must come to us intact. That is the directive. Not a suggestion from me to you. A directive from the General Reconnaissance Bureau with Hanoi's authority behind it."

"And my authority," Bounchanh said, "comes from the Pathet Lao provincial command. Which is not the General Reconnaissance Bureau."

There it was. Tran had expected it, had mapped the shape of it before he picked up the handset. Two chains. No clean junction between them. The NVA and the Pathet Lao had been fighting the same war for years and had never fully resolved who commanded what on Khammouane ground, and they were not going to resolve it on this frequency this morning.

"Major," Tran said. "If you engage those helicopters, the Americans will return in force. They will saturate this corridor with air assets. Every patrol you have in the field becomes a target. Every position on that ridge becomes a target. And the two men on the ground — the pilot and whoever is with him — will be extracted before your forces can reach them, or they will be killed by American ordnance before we can take them. Either outcome destroys what Hanoi sent me here to accomplish."

Silence on the frequency. The radio operator stood near the door, not looking at Tran.

"The cipher material," Tran continued, "is worth more than any aircraft you bring down today. More than any kill count you report to your command. If we take the pilot and what he is carrying, the Americans lose something they cannot replace. If you shoot down a helicopter, they build another one."

"You are asking me to hold my men in position while American helicopters operate freely over Khammouane."

"I am asking you to hold your men in position for the time it takes us to locate the pilot and close on him before the helicopters arrive. That is all."

Another pause. Longer this time. Tran could hear wind through the handset, or what might have been wind. Bounchanh was somewhere on a ridge or a slope with the jungle around him and American aircraft overhead, and he had men with weapons and angles, and he was being told to wait by a Vietnamese officer he had not invited and did not report to.

"Understood," Bounchanh said.

Two syllables. Nothing else.

Tran handed the handset back to the operator. He stood at the map table and looked at the grid lines, at the drainage cut marked in pencil, at the notation for the clearing at two-seven-four. The helicopters were coming. The two men on the ground were moving east toward that clearing. Bounchanh had men in the field and positions on the ridge and the operational habit of a commander who measured success in aircraft destroyed and bodies counted.

Understood was not agreement. It was acknowledgment of transmission. It was the minimum a subordinate could offer without open refusal, and Bounchanh had offered exactly the minimum.

Tran did not press it. Pressing it on this transmission would accomplish nothing. Bounchanh would comply or he would not, and the compliance would be nominal in either case — the kind of compliance that lasted until an opportunity presented itself and the calculus shifted. A helicopter in a hover over a clearing was an opportunity. Bounchanh had men who understood that.

What Tran had was time, and not much of it. The pilot was moving east. The helicopters were coming from the west. The window between those two facts was closing, and inside that window was everything Hanoi had sent him to accomplish.

He looked at the sergeant standing outside the door, visible through the gap in the canvas.

"Get me the patrol leader for the eastern element," he said. "The one working back south from the ridge."

The sergeant moved.

Tran turned back to the map. The drainage at two-seven-four. The limestone gap. The clearing visible from the air.

Bounchanh would work around the constraint, not through it. That was the nature of the fracture — not a break, not yet, but load-bearing. It would hold until it didn't. Until a helicopter presented itself at the right angle and Bounchanh made a decision that Tran's authority could not reach in time to stop.

He needed the pilot before that happened.

He needed the pilot before the helicopters arrived.

He put his finger on the grid and did not move it.
