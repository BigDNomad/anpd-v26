Bounchanh set the handset down and did not look at the radio operator. He looked at his officers.

There were three of them in the field position — Bounmy, Sergeant Phomma, and a younger man whose name Bounchanh had not bothered to learn yet but who had proven himself steady on the ridge. The limestone shelf gave them a natural parapet. The drainage below was quiet. The helicopters were coming from the west.

He spoke in Lao. Not the careful, measured Lao he used when the Vietnamese were present, but the flat provincial Lao of Khammouane, the language of this ground.

"The man from Hanoi wants the Americans taken alive," he said. "He has paper from the General Reconnaissance Bureau that says so."

Bounmy waited.

"He is concerned about drawing attention." Bounchanh looked out through the gap in the limestone toward the drainage below. The canopy was thick but the gap was there, the clearing visible even from here as a pale wound in the green. "He believes that if we are patient and quiet, the Americans will walk into his hands and he will carry them north and everyone will be satisfied."

He turned back to his officers.

"Tran sees two men and a bag," he said. "I see American helicopters in my airspace. I see men positioned to use that. These are not the same problem."

The younger man on the ridge shifted his weight. Not nervousness — attention. Bounchanh noted it.

"The Vietnamese captain has his mandate," Bounchanh said. "Hanoi's mandate runs to the border. This is Khammouane. The ground here is mine. The timing here is mine."

He did not say it as defiance. He said it the way a man states the distance between two points on a map — a fact requiring no argument, no permission.

Phomma said, "The helicopters will come in from the west. They will follow the drainage."

"Yes."

"The gun position on the north ridge has the angle."

"Yes."

Bounmy said nothing. He understood. He had understood before Bounchanh spoke, probably — Bounmy was not a man who needed things explained twice. What he was doing now was registering it, the same way you register a change in weather. You note it. You adjust.

None of them objected.

Bounchanh had not expected them to. These were not men who had survived this long by arguing with the officer who fed them and kept them alive in the karst. They were also not men who followed orders they believed were wrong without understanding why the orders were what they were. He had built that into them deliberately. An officer who needed blind obedience was an officer who did not trust his own judgment.

He trusted his.

Tran's caution was Hanoi's problem. Hanoi was far away. Hanoi had not watched American helicopters work the drainage for the past hour. Hanoi had not positioned men on the limestone with angles on the approach corridor. Hanoi had not lost scouts to whatever was moving through the jungle below — moving carefully, moving with purpose, moving east toward the clearing at grid two-seven-four.

Tran wanted the cipher material intact. Bounchanh understood the value of that. He was not a fool about intelligence. But Tran's method required patience and restraint and a narrow window of opportunity that depended on the Americans cooperating with their own capture, and Americans did not cooperate with their own capture. They called in helicopters. They called in air support. They put men on the ground with weapons and they fought their way out, and if they could not fight their way out they burned everything behind them so you got nothing.

Tran was going to get nothing.

Bounchanh was going to get the helicopters.

He did not announce this to Tran. There was no transmission to make, no order to countermand. He had acknowledged the Vietnamese captain's instructions with the appropriate words and the appropriate tone and he had set the handset down and turned to his officers and that was the end of it. The radio would stay quiet. His men would stay on the ridge. When the helicopters came in low over the drainage, following the gap in the canopy toward the clearing, his men would be there.

The fracture between what Tran believed was happening and what was actually happening had been structural from the moment the Vietnamese captain walked into the command post with his document from Hanoi. It was not structural anymore. It was a decision. Bounchanh had made it the way you make any decision in the field — quietly, without ceremony, in the full knowledge that you will live or die by it.

He looked at the drainage below. The clearing was pale in the morning light. The helicopters were coming.

"Hold your positions," he said. "Wait for my signal."

His officers turned back to the ridge. The younger man checked his weapon. Phomma moved to the parapet and looked west.

Bounmy said nothing. He already knew.
