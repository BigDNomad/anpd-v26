The maps were spread across the ground cloth, weighted at the corners with stones. Bounchanh sat cross-legged before them, the notebook open on his knee, the lantern turned low. No one else in the tent.

The blue ribbon lay on the map. He had not placed it there with any intention. He had reached into his breast pocket for the pencil and the ribbon had come out with it, and he had set it down, and there it was. It lay across the drainage line east of the limestone face, across the corridor where the Americans would come.

He looked at the notebook. The entries were in his hand, each one dated, each one precise. Fourteen aircraft. Eleven of them helicopters. The first entry was from the dry season before last, a single line, the date and the type and the grid. The most recent entry was not yet complete. He would finish it when the work was done.

He was not a man who kept trophies. The notebook was not a trophy. It was a ledger, the way a farmer keeps a ledger of the harvest, what the land has given and what it still owes. He had learned to read the American flight patterns the way he had learned to read the weather coming off the karst — by watching, by waiting, by understanding that the terrain does not change and that men flying over it will always choose the same corridors because the terrain gives them no other choice. The ridgelines funneled them. The drainage gaps opened the canopy and invited them down. He had walked every meter of this ground. He knew where the limestone pushed the trees apart and where the shadow fell at midday and where a man on a ridge could look straight down into a clearing and have the angle he needed.

The ribbon was blue. Faded now, the color of sky seen through smoke.

The notebook lay open beside it on the map. The ribbon and the tally in the same frame, the same square of ground cloth, the lantern light touching both equally.

He did not look at the ribbon the way a man looks at something he is thinking about. He looked at the map. The approach corridor from the west, the way the drainage bent north before the clearing, the gun positions he had placed on the north ridge where the limestone made a natural parapet. Khamla and Phomma and the younger one were up there now, waiting. The positions had been chosen for this. Not for any other day. For this corridor, this clearing, this morning.

The NVA captain had given his orders. Bounchanh had listened to them. He had acknowledged them in the way a man acknowledges weather — noting it, not obeying it. Tran wanted the Americans alive. Tran wanted the cargo intact. Tran had a document from Hanoi and a mandate and a certainty about how the morning would go.

Bounchanh had his own certainty.

He had lost scouts in the jungle below. The Americans moving east were not moving like men who expected to be taken. They were moving like men who expected to be rescued. The helicopters would come from the west, following the drainage, dropping into the clearing at grid two-seven-four. He had seen it before. He had seen it eleven times before, and each time the pattern was the same because the terrain was the same and the Americans trusted their machines and their machines needed open ground and open ground in this country was where the limestone pushed the trees apart and the drainage cut through.

He picked up the pencil and made a small notation on the map. Not a new position. A confirmation of what he already knew.

The ribbon lay where it had fallen.

He did not look at it. He did not need to. It had been in his breast pocket for long enough that he no longer thought about carrying it, the way a man does not think about his heartbeat. It was simply present, the way certain things are present — not displayed, not hidden, just there, in the same space as the work, occupying the same ground.

The notebook. The ribbon. The map beneath them both.

Fourteen aircraft. Eleven helicopters. The drainage running east. The clearing at grid two-seven-four with the limestone gap and the canopy open to the sky.

He reached down and picked up the ribbon. He did not look at it. He folded it once, the way he always folded it, and put it back in his breast pocket, and his hand came away and returned to the map.

The lantern burned low. Outside the tent the jungle was beginning to lighten, the darkness going gray at the edges, the birds starting up in the canopy.

He studied the approach corridor. He studied the angle from the ridge to the clearing. He studied the bend in the drainage where a helicopter slowing to land would be committed, nose up, rotor wash flattening the grass, the crew watching the ground and not the ridgeline.

He knew this ground.

He turned the page in the notebook to a clean line and waited.
