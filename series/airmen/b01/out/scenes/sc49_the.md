The Bird Dog came around on its third pass, low and slow over the drainage, and that was when Khamla's gun team opened up.

Bounchanh heard it from inside the tent — the RPD first, then two AKs joining in, the sound rolling off the limestone face and coming back at him doubled. He was on his feet before the echo died.

Outside, the gray light had come up enough to see the ridge line. He could see the muzzle flash from the north position, still going, the gunner walking tracers up toward the small plane banking hard to the east.

The Bird Dog jinked left, dropped its nose, and was gone below the tree line before the second burst reached it. The engine sound faded east. No smoke. No change in pitch. The plane was fine.

Bounchanh stood at the tent entrance and watched the north ridge position and said nothing.

The firing stopped.

The jungle went quiet the way it does after gunfire — not silent, but emptied of everything that had been moving through it a moment before. Then a bird called once from somewhere in the canopy and stopped.

He heard the Sandy before he saw it. The Skyraider came in from the southwest at low altitude, gear up, wings level, and it did not circle or climb or give any indication that it was doing anything other than what it was doing, which was rolling in on the gun position at the north ridge. The pilot had the smoke from the RPD and he had the muzzle flash and he had whatever the Bird Dog had radioed to him in the thirty seconds since the shooting started, and that was enough.

The 20mm cannons opened up and the strafing run walked straight through the north ridge position. The sound of it reached Bounchanh a half-second after the impacts — a tearing, percussive line of detonations moving left to right across the limestone face, and then the Skyraider was pulling up and climbing away to the south and the echo was still rolling back off the karst when the screaming started from the ridge.

Bounchanh walked to the north position.

Khamla was alive, crouched behind the limestone parapet with his hands over his ears. The RPD gunner was not alive. Two of the three men who had been feeding the belt were not alive. The younger officer who had been spotting was sitting against the rock with his rifle across his lap and a look on his face that Bounchanh had seen before — the look of a man who has just understood something about the world that he cannot ununderstand.

Phomma was checking the dead. There was not much to check.

Bounchanh looked at the position. The gun was still functional. The limestone had absorbed most of the cannon fire. The men had not.

He looked east along the drainage corridor. The clearing at grid two-seven-four was visible from here in the early light, the gap in the canopy where the limestone had pushed the trees apart. Empty. The Bird Dog was gone from sight but he could still hear it, circling somewhere to the east, well out of range now, and the Sandy was up high making a lazy orbit that was not lazy at all.

He opened his notebook. He looked at the last entry. He closed the notebook.

Nothing had been shot down. Nothing had been damaged. The Bird Dog had cleared the kill zone without a scratch and the Sandy had come in before the gun team could reposition and now three of his men were dead on the north ridge and the gun position that had taken him the better part of a dry season to site correctly was known to every American aircraft within radio range of the Bird Dog pilot.

He had spent men and position for nothing.

Khamla said something. Bounchanh did not respond.

The radio on Phomma's belt crackled and a voice came through it — Tran's radio operator, calling from the base camp command post, asking for a status report. The voice was careful and flat and Bounchanh recognized in it the particular flatness of a man delivering a message he does not want to deliver.

Bounchanh took the radio from Phomma. He looked at the dead men on the limestone. He looked at the clearing to the east where the helicopters would come in from the west following the drainage, the same approach corridor he had watched eleven times before, the same descent into the same clearing, the pattern that had never changed because the terrain did not give them another option.

He keyed the radio and told the operator the position had taken fire and sustained casualties and that the situation was under control.

He released the key and handed the radio back to Phomma.

The Sandy was still up there. The Bird Dog was still up there. And somewhere to the east, moving along the drainage toward the clearing, the two Americans were still moving.

The ambush position was burned. The gun team was reduced. Tran would know within the hour.

Bounchanh looked at the notebook in his hand and put it back in his pocket without opening it.
