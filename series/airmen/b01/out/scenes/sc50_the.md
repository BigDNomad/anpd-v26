Tran was at the radio when Phomma's transmission came through. He listened to the whole thing without speaking, then set the handset down on the table and stood there for a moment with his hands flat on the map.

The radio operator did not look at him.

Tran walked outside.

The sergeant was standing near the tree line, smoking. He straightened when Tran came out but Tran walked past him without stopping, moving toward the path that led up toward the ridge. He did not hurry. There was no point in hurrying now.

---

Bounchanh was still at the north position when Tran arrived. The major was standing at the parapet looking east toward the drainage, the same way he always stood, like a man surveying something he owned. Khamla was crouched behind the limestone with his hands pressed to the sides of his head. Phomma was moving among the bodies, checking them with the methodical attention of a man who had done it before.

Four dead. The RPD crew and the younger officer who had been spotting. The gun itself was intact.

Tran looked at the gun and then at Bounchanh.

"You broke the concealment order," Tran said.

Bounchanh turned. He did not appear surprised to see Tran there, which meant he had expected this conversation.

"A Bird Dog made three passes over this position," Bounchanh said. "My men engaged."

"Your men engaged a reconnaissance aircraft that was already leaving. It flew away clean. You accomplished nothing."

"We fired at an American aircraft over Khammouane province," Bounchanh said. "That is not nothing."

Tran looked at the bodies on the ground. "Four men dead. A prepared position burned. The rescue package now has a confirmed threat vector on this ridge. They know exactly where you are."

"They knew before. The Bird Dog was marking us."

"The Bird Dog was circling. There is a difference between a pilot who suspects a position and a pilot who has watched your guns fire." Tran kept his voice level. "You handed them confirmation. You handed them coordinates. The next aircraft that comes through this corridor will come with ordnance already armed."

Bounchanh said nothing for a moment. He reached into his breast pocket and took out his notebook, opened it, and made a small notation. Tran watched him do it.

"What are you writing."

"The aircraft that strafed this position. I am recording it." Bounchanh closed the notebook. "Fourteen now. Eleven helicopters."

Tran looked at him. "I have orders from the General Reconnaissance Bureau. Hanoi's authority. The pilot and the cargo are to be taken intact. I explained this to you."

"You explained Hanoi's priorities," Bounchanh said. "I understand them. They are not my priorities."

"They are your orders."

Bounchanh turned back toward the drainage. The clearing at grid two-seven-four was visible from here, a pale gap in the canopy where the limestone had pushed the trees apart. He studied it the way a man studies a problem he has already solved.

"The helicopters will come through that corridor," Bounchanh said. "They always come through that corridor. I have watched them do it eleven times. They descend from the west following the drainage and they slow when they reach the clearing." He paused. "This position covers that approach. The gun is still functional."

"I ordered you to stand down."

"You ordered me to allow the Americans to extract their pilot before reinforcements arrived." Bounchanh looked at him. "I did not agree to that."

Tran said, "The cipher material—"

"Is already gone or already in the pilot's hands. Your eastern patrol has found nothing along the route. The crash site had nothing. The men on the ground are moving east and they are not carrying hardware." Bounchanh's voice was flat, not contemptuous, simply stating what he believed to be true. "Whatever your Bureau wants from this operation, it is not going to be recovered by standing down and watching American helicopters fly out of my province."

Tran looked at Khamla, still crouched against the limestone. The lieutenant's hands had come down from his ears but his face was wrong, the expression of a man whose hearing had been replaced by a sustained tone.

"You cannot trust this position anymore," Tran said. "The Americans know it."

"They know where the gun fired from. They do not know where I am standing." Bounchanh made a small gesture toward the ridge. "I have other positions."

Tran did not answer that. There was nothing to answer. He understood now what he was dealing with, had understood it since the radio transmission, but standing here in front of the man made it concrete in a way that the transmission had not.

Bounchanh would not hold fire. Not for Hanoi's authority, not for the Bureau's mandate, not for the cipher material or the pilot or any calculation that originated outside this province. The next time an American aircraft entered this corridor, Bounchanh would engage it. That was simply what was going to happen.

Tran looked east toward the clearing.

The drainage was quiet. The Bird Dog was still circling somewhere beyond the ridge, its engine a faint and patient sound above the canopy.

The helicopters were coming.
