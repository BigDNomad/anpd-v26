The column moved slow. Too slow.

Archer watched it through a gap in the limestone where the drainage bent north before cutting back east. Eight men, maybe ten. Four guards spread wide on the flanks, not tight to the prisoners. The prisoners themselves — two Lao men he didn't recognize and a third who moved like he'd been hit somewhere in the torso — walked in the open where the canopy thinned above the drainage cut. The guards kept them there. Kept them in the light.

Coyle was beside him, breathing steady now, the color back in his face. He'd been watching it too.

"They're not watching the prisoners," Archer said.

"No."

"Guards are facing out. All four of them."

Coyle didn't say anything. He was working through it the same way Archer was, just arriving at the same place from a different direction.

The column crossed a clearing where the limestone had pushed the trees apart and the sky opened up overhead — a gap maybe forty meters wide. They didn't hurry through it. The pace didn't change. The guards kept their spacing, kept their eyes on the tree line, and the prisoners walked through the open ground like men on a Sunday road.

Archer said, "They're not hiding them."

"No," Coyle said. "They're showing them."

That was it. That was the whole thing, right there.

The guards weren't positioned to stop the prisoners from running. They were positioned to make the column look like a column — to make it look like exactly what it was, men moving east with prisoners, visible from above, visible to anything circling at altitude. The route through clearings wasn't carelessness. It was selection. Someone had walked this ground and chosen every open stretch.

Archer felt the weight of it settle in his chest.

"They're moving where a Bird Dog can see them," he said. "Where Sandy can see them."

"And they know we'll call it in," Coyle said. "They know what happens when we call it in."

A Jolly Green comes. Sandy comes. The package comes in from the west following the drainage, descending into the clearing, slowing for the hoist. The same way it always comes. The same way it had come eleven times before, according to whatever Bounchanh had written in that ledger of his.

The column moved out of the clearing and back under canopy. Unhurried. Patient.

Coyle said, "The gun positions aren't on the ridge to stop us from moving. They're on the ridge to cover the approach corridor."

"Yeah."

"The whole thing — the column, the route, the clearings — it's a signal. They want us to transmit. They want command to see this and send the package in."

Archer didn't answer. He was watching the last guard disappear into the tree line, the column gone now, the clearing empty and bright and open to the sky.

He thought about Vance. About Dalton up in the Skyraider. About the second Jolly Green they'd put on the ramp. He thought about the creed the way he'd said it so many times it had become something close to breathing — that others may live — and he understood now what the man on the ridge had understood. That a creed runs one direction. That if you know which direction it runs, you know where the man who holds it will go. You know it the way you know water runs downhill. You build your kill zone accordingly.

The enemy had taken the thing that made a PJ a PJ and turned it into a targeting solution.

Coyle was quiet for a long time. When he spoke his voice was flat, without inflection, the way a man speaks when he's already past the feeling and into the fact of it.

"I'm the bait," he said.

Archer didn't correct him. There wasn't anything to correct.

"Every man who comes in for me flies into that corridor," Coyle said. "Every Jolly Green that slows over that clearing. They're not coming for me. They're coming for what I'm carrying. And the men on that ridge know they'll come."

He stopped. The jungle held its noise around them — insects, a bird somewhere high in the canopy, the distant sound of water moving over limestone.

"Vance," Archer said. It wasn't a question.

"Vance," Coyle said.

Neither of them said anything after that. There was nothing to say that the silence didn't already hold. They knew what was moving toward them from the west. They knew what was waiting on the ridge. They knew the shape of the thing that had been built around them, patient and deliberate, built by a man who had walked every meter of this ground and written down the names of eleven helicopters.

Archer looked at the empty clearing. The light was going amber in the gap above the drainage, the afternoon burning down toward evening.

He didn't know what to do with what he knew. That was the truth of it. He knew the shape of the trap and he didn't know what to do with that knowledge, and neither did Coyle, and neither of them said so.

They stayed where they were and watched the clearing and didn't move.
