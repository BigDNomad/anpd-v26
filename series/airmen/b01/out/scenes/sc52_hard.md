The column came out of the tree line below them without warning — no advance sound, no scout element they could see, just men appearing in the gap where the drainage opened into the lower trail, one after another, moving at a steady walk.

Archer went flat. Coyle was already down.

Neither man breathed loud enough to matter.

The trail ran maybe forty meters below their position, screened by secondary growth and a limestone shelf that jutted out between them and the valley floor. Good cover. Not perfect. Archer kept his face turned slightly away from the open ground, the way you learned not to look directly at what you were trying not to be seen by.

He counted.

The lead element was eight men, AK-47s slung or carried at the low ready, moving with the loose confidence of men on familiar ground. Behind them a gap, then a heavier section — two RPD gunners with their weapons broken down across their backs, the bipods folded, the drums riding in canvas bags on the men beside them. Then more rifles. Then a pair carrying an RPG tube between them, the launcher broken at the coupling, the warheads in a padded sling one man wore across his chest like a bandolier.

Archer kept counting.

The column did not stop. It did not slow. It moved the way water moves through a channel it has used before — without hesitation, without looking for the way, because the way was already known.

He reached thirty-two and the column was still coming.

Coyle lay beside him with his chin on his forearm, watching. His color was better than it had been. His breathing was controlled. He was counting too, Archer could tell by the slight movement of his lips, the way his eyes tracked each element as it passed.

Forty-one. Forty-two.

A section with two more RPG tubes, these ones assembled and carried at the shoulder. Behind them, men with no visible weapons — support personnel, or men whose weapons were packed — and then a rear element of six rifles walking with the same unhurried pace as the lead.

The last man cleared the gap in the tree line and the column was gone, absorbed into the jungle on the far side of the drainage.

Neither of them moved.

The jungle settled back into its sounds. Birds first, then insects. The drainage ran its thin noise over the rocks below. Archer watched the gap in the tree line where the last man had disappeared and he waited, because the last man you see is not always the last man.

He waited longer than that.

When he finally shifted his weight off his bad shoulder, the relief was sharp enough that he had to hold still a moment before he could think clearly.

Coyle turned his head. He held up two fingers, then four, then one. Forty-one. Close enough.

Archer had forty-seven. The difference didn't matter. What mattered was the shape of it — the RPDs, the RPG tubes, the rear security element walking with the kind of spacing that came from training rather than habit. This was not a patrol. Patrols moved differently. They checked their flanks, they varied their pace, they sent men ahead and waited. This column moved like it had somewhere to be and knew the road was clear because someone had already cleared it.

This was a unit being repositioned.

He thought about the valley below them, the way the trail ran east through the drainage gap, the way the limestone walls on either side funneled everything into that single corridor. You could move a hundred men through here and the jungle would swallow the sound inside a kilometer. He had been thinking of this terrain as difficult. The men who used it thought of it as a highway.

Coyle was looking at him. His expression was flat, but his eyes were working.

Archer understood what he was thinking because he was thinking it himself. The prisoner column they had watched earlier — the deliberate spacing, the guards positioned for visibility rather than security, the route through the clearings where the canopy broke. That was bait. And bait needed a hook behind it. What had just passed below them was the hook. More guns, heavier weapons, organized and moving into position along the same corridor the helicopters would have to follow to reach the clearing at grid two-seven-four.

The kill zone wasn't being maintained. It was being thickened.

Neither of them said anything. There was nothing to say that the other man hadn't already worked out.

Archer looked back at the gap in the tree line one more time. Empty trail, limestone walls, the drainage running its thin line through the bottom of the valley. Afternoon light coming down through the canopy at a low angle, the shadows already longer than they had been.

Whatever came next — the radio, the helicopters, the clearing four hundred meters east — it happened inside a net that was larger now than it had been an hour ago. Larger, and tighter, and set by men who had walked this ground before and knew exactly where the Americans would have to go.
