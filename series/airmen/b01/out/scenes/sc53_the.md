The reinforcements came out of the tree line in two files, moving fast, rifles slung forward. Bounchanh counted them as they crossed the open ground between the last limestone outcrop and his position — twenty-two men, maybe twenty-three, with a sergeant at the head of each file. They had come from the base camp on foot and they were breathing hard but they were not winded. He watched them come and felt something settle in him, the way a calculation resolves when the last number falls into place.

The sergeant leading the first file stopped three meters out and reported. Bounchanh listened without looking at him, his eyes still on the tree line, on the drainage corridor below, on the clearing at grid two-seven-four sitting empty in the afternoon light.

He said: Send the first element north along the ridge. Past the gun position, then down the east slope to the drainage. He pointed. They go there and they wait. They do not fire unless I transmit.

The sergeant said yes.

Bounchanh said: Second element splits. Half south along the slope, half into the canopy above the drainage. I want coverage on both sides of the cut. Nothing moves through that corridor without someone seeing it.

He turned then and looked at the men directly. They were young, most of them, and they had come quickly when called, which was what mattered. He did not speak to them about what had happened at the gun position. The dead were dead and the position was known to the Americans now, which changed the geometry but not the outcome. He had other positions. He had always had other positions.

Lieutenant Khamla was standing behind the limestone parapet where he had been since the strafing run. His face was composed but his hands were not entirely still. Bounchanh said to him: You move the RPD to the secondary. You know where.

Khamla said he knew.

Bounchanh said: Do it now, before the light changes.

He watched the reinforcements disperse into their assigned sectors, the files breaking apart and filtering into the jungle and along the ridge with the quiet efficiency of men who had done this before. The terrain absorbed them. In two minutes there was no visible sign they had arrived at all.

Sergeant Phomma came up beside him and said nothing. He was good at that, Phomma — knowing when silence was the appropriate contribution.

Bounchanh looked at the drainage below. The shadows were lengthening now, the light going amber and flat across the limestone faces, and the clearing at grid two-seven-four was still empty, still waiting. The Americans were moving east along that drainage. He knew it the way he knew the terrain — not from reports alone but from the logic of the ground itself, from eleven previous times he had watched men make the same choice because the ground gave them no other.

The helicopters would come from the west. They always came from the west, following the drainage down, slowing as the clearing opened up beneath them. That was the moment. That had always been the moment.

He thought about the NVA captain at the base camp, the man with the mandate from Hanoi and the careful voice and the orders that Bounchanh had acknowledged without agreeing to. Tran wanted the Americans alive. Tran wanted the cargo intact. Tran had authority from the General Reconnaissance Bureau and a document to prove it, and none of that changed what Bounchanh knew about helicopters and clearings and the particular vulnerability of a machine that must slow and descend and hold position while a man is lowered on a cable into the jungle.

He had fourteen aircraft in his ledger. Eleven of them helicopters.

He put his hand briefly to his breast pocket, felt the small weight there, and took his hand away.

The reinforcements were in position now, or close enough. He could not see them but he knew where they were, could map their locations against the terrain in his mind with the precision of a man who had walked every meter of this ground. The cordon that had existed before — loose, with gaps a careful man could thread — was tighter now. The gaps were closing. The open ground between search elements was shrinking to nothing.

Below, somewhere in the drainage, the two Americans were moving east. One of them was hurt. One of them was carrying something Hanoi wanted badly enough to send a captain with a mandate to retrieve it. Bounchanh did not particularly care about what they were carrying. He cared about the helicopters that would come for them.

The clearing at grid two-seven-four sat in the fading light, limestone on three sides, canopy broken open above it, the drainage running through it like a line drawn on a map.

He said to Phomma: Make sure the eastern element understands. They hold until the aircraft is committed to the descent. Not before.

Phomma said he would make sure.

Bounchanh looked at the clearing one more time. Then he turned and walked back along the ridge to check the secondary position himself.
