Archer saw it before he understood it. The patrol had been moving west, quartering the ground in the methodical way of men who knew the terrain and had time to use it. Then the lead man stopped, crouched, and the whole line compressed behind him. The man was looking at the ground.

Not sweeping. Reading.

Coyle's blood had been dropping since the firefight — not a pour, but a steady dark punctuation on the leaf litter, one mark every few steps where the field dressing had soaked through. Archer had known it and kept moving anyway, trading speed for the trail he was leaving. Now the trade had come due.

He put his mouth against Coyle's ear. "Stream. Now. Don't talk."

The stream was thirty meters south, audible through the undergrowth. Archer got Coyle's arm across his shoulders and moved, keeping his weight under the man's, absorbing what he could of the broken leg's drag. Coyle made no sound. Whatever it cost him he kept it inside.

They went into the water at the bank's edge, not wading in but sliding, controlled, Archer going first and taking Coyle's weight as the man came down. The water was cold and shallow, knee-deep at the center, running clear over flat stone. Archer moved them upstream, against the current, each step placed with care to avoid the loose rock that would clatter. Forty meters. Fifty. He found what he was looking for where an old tree had come down across the bank, its root mass pulled free of the earth and hanging exposed over the water — a dark tangle of root and clay and dead fiber that overhung the stream by two feet.

He worked Coyle in against the bank beneath the roots, pressing him flat into the clay, the water at his chin. Then Archer settled in beside him, his back to the bank, facing downstream, the root mass above them both.

He could hear the patrol before he saw them. Boots in water, the particular hollow knock of a rifle stock against stone, voices in Lao — low, not whispering, the tone of men who were confident and not yet alarmed. They came around the downstream bend and into the stretch of stream where Archer and Coyle had entered. The lead man stopped again. Looked at the bank where they'd gone in. Looked at the water.

Archer felt Coyle's body against his arm. The man was shaking — not fear, the involuntary tremor of a body in cold water with a broken femur and burn tissue that had no insulation left. Archer pressed his arm against Coyle's and held it there. Coyle stopped shaking. Or held it down to something that didn't move the water.

The patrol worked the bank. One man waded to the center of the stream and stood there, turning slowly, looking both directions. He was close enough that Archer could see the mud on his boot laces, the way his trousers darkened at the knee where the water had soaked through. The man said something to the others. One of them answered. They moved downstream, away from the root mass, working the far bank now, checking the places where a man might have come out of the water.

Archer did not move.

The voices receded. The sound of boots in water faded and then was gone, replaced by the stream's own noise and the birds that had gone quiet and were starting again.

He waited. Counted his own breathing. Waited longer.

When he was certain — not satisfied, certain — he moved.

He got Coyle out of the water and onto the bank and went to work on the leg. The field dressing was saturated and useless. He stripped it off and looked at the wound in the fading light. The entry point was still seeping. He packed it with what the stream bank offered — moss pulled from the root mass, bark fiber stripped from a fallen branch, pressed in tight and held. He used the dressing as an outer wrap, tying it firm. The bleeding slowed. He watched it for a full minute. It stopped.

No fresh drops on the ground. No smear. Nothing to follow.

Coyle lay back against the bank and said nothing. His face was the color of the clay behind him.

The radio crackled once, twice, then the Bird Dog's voice came through in fragments — the karst eating the signal, giving it back in pieces. Archer pressed the receiver to his ear and listened. He caught enough. The base commander's message, relayed through the Bird Dog, arrived in broken syllables that nonetheless assembled into a complete and unambiguous instruction.

If extraction becomes impossible. If capture is imminent.

Terminate.

Archer held the radio against his thigh and looked at the stream. The water moved over the flat stones the same as it had before. The birds were back in the canopy. Coyle was two feet away, watching the sky through the root tangle above them, breathing.

Archer put the radio away. He kept his face still and his hands still and said nothing at all.
