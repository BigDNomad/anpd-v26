The patrol sounds faded northwest, swallowed by the jungle. Archer counted to sixty before he moved.

He came out from under the root mass low and fast, staying below the bank line, and went straight to Coyle's leg. The field dressing was saturated, dark and stiff at the edges. He peeled it back and looked at the wound in the failing light. The entry point was still seeping, not pumping, which meant the femoral was intact. Small mercies.

He worked the stream bank with both hands, pulling moss from the exposed root faces where it grew thick and wet. He stripped bark fiber from a fallen branch half-submerged in the current, working it between his fingers until it came apart in long strands. His right shoulder protested the motion and he ignored it. He packed the moss in tight against the wound, pressing it flat with his palm, then layered the bark fiber over it and held pressure.

Coyle watched him work. He didn't say anything.

Archer held the pressure and counted. The moss compressed against the entry point and the seeping slowed. He could feel it through his palm, the pulse of it diminishing. He kept counting. At ninety he lifted his hand slightly and looked. The moss held its color. Nothing fresh coming through.

He pressed it down again and held it another thirty seconds.

When he lifted his hand the second time the wound had stopped.

He looked at the ground around them. The blood trail from where they'd come in was already going dark, the drops losing their wet sheen, the smear along the root where Coyle had dragged himself turning brown at the edges. He looked downstream. Nothing fresh. No bright red on the stones, no smear on the bank. Whatever the patrol had been tracking, they wouldn't find a continuation of it. The trail ended here and went cold.

Archer sat back on his heels and let himself register that.

They could move now. That was the thing. Before, movement meant leaving a path any man with eyes could follow. Now the ground behind them would tell nothing. He looked at Coyle.

The man's color was better than it had been. The blood transfusion had done something, and the fish the woman had given them had done something else, and now Coyle was lying against the root mass with his eyes open and his breathing steady. Not good. But not the edge of it either.

The broken femur was the problem. It would always be the problem. Archer had the leg stabilized as well as he could manage with what the jungle had offered, but stabilized was not the same as functional. Coyle could not walk. He could not bear weight on that side. Every meter between here and the clearing at grid two-seven-four would have to be covered with Coyle's arm across Archer's shoulders and Archer's good arm around Coyle's back, the two of them moving like something wounded and slow through terrain that wanted to kill them on its own terms.

The burn tissue was the other thing. No insulation. Every degree the temperature dropped after dark would cost Coyle something he didn't have to spare.

Archer looked east along the drainage. The light was going. The canopy filtered what remained of it down to something gray and directionless. He could hear the water moving over the stones and nothing else. The patrol was gone. The ridge was quiet. Whatever Bounchanh had repositioned up there after Sandy's run, it wasn't making noise.

Four hundred meters. In open terrain with two good legs that was nothing. In this, moving the way they'd have to move, it was an hour. Maybe more. And the patrol that had gone northwest was still out there, working its way back.

He looked at Coyle again.

"Packing held," Archer said.

Coyle looked at the moss and bark fiber pressed against his thigh. "What is that."

"Moss."

"You packed my leg wound with moss."

"It stopped the bleeding."

Coyle considered this. "Okay," he said.

Archer checked the revolver. Four rounds. He'd fired twice at the point element in the drainage and he had not recovered anything from the men he'd killed with the knife. He turned the cylinder and closed it.

The drainage east was the only route that made sense. The ridge was covered. The slope north of them had patrols working it. The clearing at two-seven-four was the only place a helicopter could get down, and Bird Dog was still up there somewhere, circling, waiting for something to happen.

He looked at the sky through the canopy. The light was almost gone.

They needed to move before full dark, while he could still read the drainage and keep them out of the water. Once the temperature dropped Coyle would lose ground fast, and Archer couldn't carry him. Not with the shoulder. Not alone.

He got his good arm under Coyle's back.

"This is going to be slow," Archer said.

"I know it," Coyle said.

"Tell me when you need to stop."

"I'll tell you."

Archer got him up.
