The radio came through in pieces.

Archer held the handset close and listened to the Bird Dog pilot work through it, the transmission breaking apart over the karst the way they always did, syllables dropping out and coming back wrong. He caught enough. He caught the shape of it. The base commander's voice wasn't on the frequency — it was the Bird Dog pilot reading from something, his voice flat and careful the way a man's voice gets when he is reading words he did not write and does not want to say.

*If extraction becomes impossible. If capture is imminent.*

Static.

*Terminate.*

Static.

*Acknowledge.*

Archer pressed the transmit key twice. Released it. Set the handset down on the root beside him without looking at it.

Coyle was four feet away, propped against the limestone with his bad leg straight out in front of him and his hands loose in his lap. He'd eaten the fish the woman gave them and some of the rice from the village, and the color that had come back into his face after the transfusion had held. His breathing was steady. He was watching the canopy above the drainage, where the last of the light was going gray-green and thin, and he had the look of a man doing the arithmetic on his own situation — not panicking, just counting.

He hadn't heard the transmission. The handset volume was low and the limestone between them had taken the rest.

Archer did not move.

He sat with his back against the root mass and his right arm across his knee and he thought about what the order required. Not the logic of it — the logic was plain enough, he could follow it without effort: one man on the ground with eight cipher wheels that could unravel every American asset north of Vientiane, a kill zone thickening along the only approach corridor, helicopters that would have to slow and descend into it. The Colonel had done the arithmetic too, and the Colonel's arithmetic came out the same way every time. You did not let the rotors reach Hanoi. You did not let the man who knew the network reach Hanoi. You did what the mission required and you lived with it afterward, or you didn't live with it, but either way the mission was what mattered.

That was the logic. Archer understood it.

What he was sitting with now was not the logic.

He had been ninety hours getting to this man. He had fallen sixty feet through jungle canopy and walked on a shoulder that ground wrong every time he raised his arm and crossed a ridge with a security element twenty meters above him and put a knife into two men in the dark and given a pint of his own blood through a catheter line and carried Coyle's weight on that same bad shoulder for four hundred meters of karst. He had done all of it because the creed said you went in and you brought them out. That others may live. That was not a slogan. That was the thing he had organized his life around, the thing that made the rest of it make sense.

And now the base commander, who was sitting in a chair in Thailand, was telling him to put four rounds into the man he had spent ninety hours keeping alive.

He looked at his hands. They were still. He made sure of that.

Coyle turned his head. "Bird Dog check in?"

"Affirmative," Archer said.

"We good?"

"We're moving when you're ready."

Coyle studied him for a moment. Whatever he saw in Archer's face, he let it go. He'd been in the work long enough to know when a man had received something he wasn't going to share, and he had enough of his own weight to carry without borrowing someone else's.

"Give me a minute," Coyle said.

"Take it."

The light was going. Through the gap in the canopy above the drainage Archer could see the sky shifting from gray to something darker, the color draining out of it the way it did fast in the jungle, faster than you expected every time. They had maybe forty minutes of usable light. The clearing at grid two-seven-four was three hundred meters east along the drainage. At their pace, with Coyle's leg, it would take most of that.

The order sat between them in the space where Archer hadn't spoken it.

He thought about the revolver. Four rounds. He thought about what four rounds meant and what they didn't mean and what he would have to decide before the helicopters came in, if the helicopters came in, if the corridor wasn't already too thick with gun positions to thread.

He thought about the Colonel's arithmetic and his own arithmetic and whether they were the same.

They were not the same.

He didn't know yet what that meant for what happened next. He knew what it meant for right now. Right now it meant he picked up his end of the carry rig and he looked at Coyle and he said, "All right. Let's go."
