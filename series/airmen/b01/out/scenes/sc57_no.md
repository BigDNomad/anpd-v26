The sound came from three directions at once — brush moving, a boot on wet stone, the click of a selector switch somewhere in the green dark to the left. Archer didn't need to see them. The sound told him everything. They were inside fifty meters on the north side, closer on the east, and the ravine at their backs was the only thing keeping the south open, and it wouldn't stay open long.

He looked at Coyle. Coyle was already looking at him.

"How many," Coyle said.

"Enough."

The brush on the north slope moved again. Closer. Archer counted the angles and the math came out the same way it always came out when the math was bad. They were going to be taken alive. That was what the encirclement meant — no firing, no grenade, just the slow tightening of the net. Vorasak wanted them breathing. Wanted whatever Coyle was carrying in his head, whatever the CIA man knew about the network, the routes, the names. Archer had seen what that looked like on the other end and he was not going to let it happen.

He pulled the survival pistol and turned to Coyle.

The weapon came up clean. His hand was steady. That was the thing — his hand was perfectly steady and his mind was clear and he had the shot and he stood there with the muzzle six inches from Tom Coyle's face and he could not do it.

It wasn't fear. It wasn't hesitation in the way hesitation usually worked, the body balking before the mind caught up. It was something else, something that sat behind his sternum like a stone, and the stone said no, and the stone did not explain itself, and Archer stood there with the pistol up and the decision simply was not there.

Coyle watched his face. Coyle had been watching his face the whole time.

"Give me mine," Coyle said.

Archer looked at him.

"The .38," Coyle said. "Give it back."

Archer had been carrying it since the second day, when Coyle's leg had made the holster useless. He reached back and pulled it from his waistband and held it out and Coyle took it without looking at the gun, just looking at Archer, and broke the cylinder and checked the load and closed it again with the flat of his palm.

"Go," Coyle said.

"I'm not leaving you."

"That's not what I mean." Coyle turned the revolver and looked at it. "I mean go ahead and move. I'll be right behind you. I just need a second."

The brush on the north slope stopped moving. That was worse than the sound.

Archer looked at Coyle and Coyle looked back at him and neither of them moved and the jungle held its breath around them and somewhere to the east a man said something in Lao, low and flat, and another man answered, and the net was maybe thirty seconds from closing.

Then Sandy came in.

He came in from the southwest, low enough that Archer felt the airframe before he heard it, a pressure change in the air, and then the Skyraider was over the ridge and the guns were going and the sound was enormous and continuous and the rockets came off the wings in pairs and hit the north slope and the east slope and the jungle lit up in orange and the concussion rolled through the ravine like a physical thing and the Pathet Lao went to ground.

Archer grabbed Coyle's arm.

"Move."

Coyle moved. The leg was bad and it had been bad for two days and it didn't matter now. They went down the ravine slope fast, Archer half-carrying him over the loose rock, and Sandy came around for a second pass and the guns opened up again on the east side and the rockets walked up the slope and the secondary explosions told Archer someone had been carrying something they shouldn't have been carrying.

They crossed the ravine at the bottom, water to the knee, and Archer pulled Coyle up the far bank and they went into the tree line on the other side and kept moving. Behind them the firing continued, Sandy working the perimeter in long patient passes, and the Pathet Lao were not regrouping, not yet, because there was nothing to regroup into except more fire.

Archer didn't stop until the sound of the guns was behind a ridge and the jungle was thick enough to slow a man down. He stopped and listened. Nothing following. Not yet.

Coyle leaned against a tree and breathed. He still had the .38 in his hand. He looked at it for a moment and then holstered it.

Neither of them said anything about what had happened in the ravine. There was nothing to say about it that the silence didn't already cover. Archer checked the compass and found the bearing and looked at Coyle.

"Can you walk."

"Yes," Coyle said.

They walked.
