The fog came in off the karst as the light failed, rolling down the ridgelines in slow gray sheets until the valley floor disappeared beneath it. Archer watched it come. He had seen weather move fast in the mountains but this was different — this was the jungle breathing out, the whole basin exhaling at once, and inside of twenty minutes the tree line across the ravine was gone and the ravine itself was gone and there was nothing beyond the nearest trunks but white.

Coyle was propped against a root mass, his bad leg straight out in front of him. He hadn't complained in an hour, which was not a good sign.

"How's the leg," Archer said.

"Still there."

"That's not what I asked."

Coyle looked at him. "It's worse. You know it's worse."

Archer did know. The swelling had moved up past the knee and the skin around the original wound had gone the color of old meat. He'd done what he could with the kit and what he could was not enough and they both understood that.

He keyed the radio. "Sandy, Two-Two Alpha. You copy."

Static. Then: "Two-Two Alpha, Sandy. Go ahead."

"We've got zero visibility on the ground. Fog bank, valley-wide. What's your situation."

A pause. "We see it from up here. Jolly's holding at the edge. Stand by."

Archer stood by. He could hear Coyle's breathing, slow and deliberate, the kind of breathing a man does when he is managing pain rather than ignoring it.

The radio came back. "Two-Two Alpha, Sandy. Jolly's going to try an approach. Keep this channel clear."

---

Inside the Jolly Green, Dalton took her down.

He came in from the east where the fog thinned at the ridgeline, following the terrain as long as he could follow it, the rotors pulling them forward into the gray. The karst was invisible below. He knew it was there. He knew the ridges ran north-south and that the valley between them was maybe a mile wide and that a mile was nothing in this soup.

Vance was on the hoist mount, watching out the door. There was nothing to watch.

"Two hundred feet," the co-pilot said.

Dalton held the approach. The altimeter was the only truth he had. The karst didn't care about altimeters.

"Ridge coming up," the co-pilot said. "Estimated."

Dalton pulled back and climbed. He held the climb until they broke out above the fog into clear air and the stars were there and the valley was gone beneath white.

He keyed Sandy. "No penetration. I can't see the terrain. I go in there I'm flying blind into the rock."

"Understood," Sandy said. "Hold altitude."

Vance unclipped from the hoist mount and came forward. "Put me down," he said. "Rope me down through the fog, I'll find the deck, I'll find Archer."

"And then what," Dalton said.

"Stabilize the survivor. Wait for the weather."

"I'm not authorized to insert you with no extraction window," Sandy said over the radio. He had heard it. "Vance, I hear you. The answer is no."

"He's been down there two days."

"I know how long he's been down there. The answer is no. You go in, you're on the ground with no way out until this lifts. That's two men I can't get to instead of one."

Vance stood in the door of the Jolly Green and looked down at the white. Somewhere under that white was Archer and a man who needed a hospital. He knew Sandy was right. He stayed in the door anyway for another moment, the way a man does when he knows the argument is over but hasn't finished having it.

"Jolly Green Two-Two, hold at altitude," Sandy said. "We wait for dawn."

---

Archer heard the rotors. He heard them come in from the east, heard them drop toward the valley, heard them change pitch and climb back out. He listened until the sound faded north.

He looked at the fog. It was complete. It sat on the jungle like a lid.

He thought about what the fog was doing. It was keeping Vorasak's men from moving fast through the terrain — they'd be cautious in this, feeling their way, not wanting to walk into each other or into him. That was good. It was also keeping the Jolly Green on the other side of the ridgeline until the sun came back up and burned this off. That was the situation.

He looked at Coyle.

Coyle had his eyes closed. His chest was moving. The .38 was in his hand, loose, the way a man holds something he's not ready to use but wants close.

Archer settled his back against the root mass and faced the direction the sounds had last come from. The fog pressed in around them, soft and absolute. Cover and cage, both at once, and nothing to be done about either until the light came back.

He would keep watch. Coyle would breathe. The fog would hold.

Dawn was the next viable window and dawn was a long way off.
