Dalton took the Jolly Green down into the valley mouth and the fog came up at them like a wall. Not gradual. One moment he had the ridgeline silhouettes on both sides and the next he had nothing — white-gray nothing pressing against the windscreen, the rotor wash churning it without opening it. He held the descent another few seconds, watching the altimeter, watching the radar altimeter, watching for anything that would tell him where the karst was.

The karst didn't announce itself.

He pulled back up.

"Two-Two, Sandy. What've you got."

"Nothing," Dalton said. "Zero vis at the valley floor. I can't see the ridges and I can't see the ground. I've got maybe two hundred feet of clear air above the fog layer and that's it."

Static. Then Sandy came back. "Confirm you cannot penetrate."

"Confirm. I go down in that, I'm flying blind into rock. I'm not doing it."

He leveled off at holding altitude and the Jolly Green hung there, the rotors beating against the cold air, and below them the fog filled the valley from wall to wall like something poured in. You couldn't see the jungle. You couldn't see the ravine. You couldn't see anything that was down there waiting.

Vance unstrapped from the jump seat and came forward. He stood behind Dalton with one hand on the bulkhead.

"Put me on the hoist," he said. "Lower me through the layer. I can work blind on the way down if I have a compass heading and a rate. I'll find the tree line."

Dalton didn't say anything. That wasn't his call.

Vance keyed the radio. "Sandy, Two-Two PJ. Request permission for hoist insertion. I go down, I find Archer, I stabilize the survivor, and we wait out the weather on the ground. You pull us at first light."

The pause on the other end was long enough that Vance thought the transmission hadn't gone through.

"Negative," Sandy said. "Negative on the insertion."

"Sandy, the survivor's leg is—"

"I heard the last report. Answer's still no."

Vance looked at the fog below them. The valley had disappeared into it completely. Somewhere down in that white nothing Archer was sitting with a man whose leg was going bad, and the Pathet Lao were somewhere in it too, moving or not moving, and there was nothing anyone in the air could do about any of it.

"Sandy." Vance kept his voice flat. "I'm not asking you to risk the aircraft. I'm asking you to put me on a rope."

"And when the weather doesn't break at dawn? When it holds another day? You're on the ground with a compromised survivor and no extraction window and I've got one PJ down instead of zero. That's not a rescue, that's two men I can't get out."

Vance didn't answer.

"On-scene commander's call," Sandy said. "We hold at NKP and return at first light. That's the decision."

Dalton was already turning the Jolly Green west. He didn't wait for Vance to sit back down. The aircraft banked and the fog-filled valley fell away behind them and the dark came up around them as they climbed toward cruising altitude for the run back to Thailand.

Vance stood there a moment longer, hand still on the bulkhead, looking at the place where the valley had been. Then he went back and strapped in.

The flight back was quiet. Dalton flew it and didn't talk. The crew chief sat against the fuselage wall with his arms across his knees. Vance sat with his head back and his eyes open, not looking at anything in the aircraft.

What Sandy had said was correct. Vance knew it was correct. A one-way insertion with no extraction window wasn't a rescue operation, it was a gesture, and gestures got people killed without getting anyone out. Sandy had run enough of these to know where the line was. The line was there for a reason.

Knowing that didn't change what was sitting in his chest.

The lights of NKP came up through the windscreen, the runway strobes and the perimeter lights and the dull orange glow of the base against the low clouds. Dalton brought the Jolly Green in clean and set it down on the ramp and the rotors wound down and the night came in around them.

Somewhere east of them, across the Mekong and across the border that officially didn't exist, the fog was still in the valley. It would be there until it wasn't. The jungle didn't care about their schedule. The karst didn't care. The fog least of all.

Archer had his radio. He had the tree line. He had whatever he'd decided to do with the night.

Dawn was the next window. That was what they had.

Dalton shut down the engines and the last of the rotor noise died away and the ramp was quiet around them, and nobody said anything, because there wasn't anything to say that the silence didn't already cover.
