The sound came apart in the fog the way sounds do when there is nothing left to carry them. The rotors faded, then the engine note, then even the suggestion of both, and what was left was the jungle and the dark coming down through the trees and the fog sitting on everything like something that had always been there and always would be.

Archer heard it go. He didn't move.

Coyle was three feet to his left, back against the same tree line, and Archer didn't need to look at him to know he'd heard it too. They both sat with it for a moment. The fog pressed in close and white and useless.

Neither of them said anything.

There was nothing to say that the silence hadn't already said better.

Archer ran the inventory in his head the way he'd been trained to run it, the way you did it when the situation changed and you needed to know exactly where you stood. Radio. Survival knife. The .38 was back on Coyle's hip where it belonged. His own sidearm. No food worth mentioning. Water from the last stream crossing, maybe enough. The fog wasn't going anywhere before dawn, and dawn was a long way off, and the Pathet Lao were still out there in the dark moving slow and careful through the same fog that had just sent the Jolly Green home.

He looked at Coyle.

The man had been holding himself together through the day on something that wasn't quite will and wasn't quite stubbornness but was some combination of the two that Archer had seen before in men who had no other options. Now, in the last gray light before full dark, Archer could see what the day had cost him. The leg was worse. You could tell without touching it, without asking. The way Coyle held it, the way he'd been shifting his weight for the last hour, the slight tightening around his eyes when he thought no one was watching. The swelling had moved up past the knee. The discoloration Archer had noted at the last check was spreading.

He didn't say any of this.

Coyle looked back at him. "Cold camp," Coyle said.

"Cold camp," Archer said.

That was the whole conversation.

They worked without light, by feel and by the small sounds of each other moving in the dark. Archer found a position where the tree roots gave them something at their backs and the canopy overhead was dense enough that the fog thinned slightly, not enough to matter but enough that it didn't feel like breathing through wet cloth. He got Coyle settled with the leg elevated on a root, which wasn't much but was something. He checked the perimeter by ear, standing still for three full minutes and listening to the jungle, sorting the sounds into categories — wind, water, animal, nothing that was men. Not yet.

He came back and sat down.

The dark was complete now. The fog held what little light the sky might have offered and turned it into nothing. You could not see your hand in front of your face and Archer did not try. He sat with his back against the root and his rifle across his knees and he listened.

The hope that had been running underneath everything — the sound of rotors, the voice on the radio, the idea that the Jolly Green was up there above the fog and working the problem — that was gone now. It didn't leave all at once. It left the way the engine sound had left, in pieces, until there was nothing left of it and you were just sitting in the dark with what was true.

What was true was this: they were in the jungle, in Laos, in the dark, with men hunting them, and no one was coming until morning at the earliest, and morning was contingent on weather that had already proven it had no interest in their situation.

Archer filed it. Not as despair, not as anything that required a response beyond the next hour and the hour after that. You managed what you could manage. You stayed quiet, stayed still, stayed alive. That was the job. That was all the job was tonight.

Beside him, Coyle's breathing was controlled but effortful. The man was working to keep it even. Archer could hear the effort in it.

He did not speak. There was nothing useful to say, and Coyle didn't need to be told what Archer already knew about the leg, and neither of them needed to discuss what the night was or what it meant. They both knew. They sat with it.

The jungle made its sounds around them. Somewhere in the fog, water moved over stone. Something moved in the canopy above and was still. The dark held everything equally — the two men against the roots, the fog in the valley, the Pathet Lao working their slow careful way through the dark — and did not distinguish between any of it.

It was the longest night Archer had known. He suspected it was the longest night Coyle had known too.

He sat with his rifle and he listened and he waited for morning.
