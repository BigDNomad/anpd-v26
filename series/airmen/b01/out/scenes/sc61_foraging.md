Coyle's breathing had steadied into something close to sleep. Archer listened to it for a moment, then checked the .38 in Coyle's hand — still there, still loose in his grip — and moved away from the tree line.

The fog was total. He'd known fog before, mountain fog, sea fog, the kind that sits on water at first light, but this was different. This was a living thing, dense and warm and without any gap in it, and it pressed against his face like a wet cloth. He could see his hand at arm's length if he held it up. Past that, nothing. The karst and the jungle and whatever was moving out there in it — all of it reduced to sound.

He moved low and slow, the way they'd trained him, weight forward on the balls of his feet, each step placed before he committed to it. The rifle he'd slung tight across his back. Both hands free. He needed them for this.

The jungle floor was wet. Everything was wet. He worked by feel, fingers reading the ground the way a blind man reads a page, sorting what he touched — root, rock, rot, leaf — and keeping what was worth keeping. He found a cluster of roots near a fallen trunk, the kind that ran shallow and pale, and worked them loose with the knife. Bitter, probably. Didn't matter. Calories were calories and Coyle needed them.

He thought about Coyle's leg. The swelling had moved past the knee and the discoloration was the kind that didn't reverse on its own. Archer had seen that progression before and he knew what it meant and he knew what it would mean by morning if Coyle didn't eat, didn't keep his strength up enough to move when they needed to move. The leg was a problem he couldn't solve tonight. The food he could.

He found a rotting log and split it open with the knife, working quietly, and took what was inside — grubs, pale and fat, the kind that lived in wet wood. He put them in the breast pocket of his flight suit. He didn't think about it. You thought about it and you didn't eat, and if you didn't eat you didn't function, and if you didn't function out here you died. It was that simple and he'd made his peace with it somewhere in training and never revisited the question.

A sound stopped him.

He went flat and still, face against the ground, and listened. The fog amplified everything — dripping water, the creak of bamboo somewhere upslope, his own pulse. He waited. Whatever it was didn't repeat. Could have been an animal. Could have been a man placing a foot wrong in the dark. He gave it two full minutes before he moved again, and when he moved he went slower.

Vorasak's people were out there. He knew that. The fog had slowed them the same as it had slowed everything else, but it hadn't stopped them, and they were motivated. They wanted Coyle alive and they wanted whatever Coyle knew, and that meant they were willing to move through this dark and this wet and this silence to get him. Archer respected that in a professional way, the way you respected any force that was serious about its work.

He found wild ginger near a stream cut, identified it by the shape of the leaves under his fingers, and took a handful. Not much nutrition but it would help Coyle's stomach hold whatever else he brought back. He found something else he couldn't identify and left it. Unknown plants in the dark were a risk he didn't need to take.

He worked his way back toward the tree line by feel and memory, counting his steps, keeping the slope on his left. The fog had no direction in it, no light, no gradient. You could walk in circles in this and never know it. He kept the slope on his left and counted.

Coyle was where he'd left him, back against the tree, the .38 still in his hand. His breathing had changed — shallower, faster. Archer crouched beside him and put a hand on his shoulder and Coyle came awake hard, the revolver coming up.

"It's me," Archer said.

Coyle lowered the gun. He didn't say anything for a moment.

"Brought you something," Archer said.

He laid out what he had. The roots. The grubs. The ginger. In the dark it was just shapes in his palm and he described them to Coyle without ceremony, the way you'd describe a meal to someone who needed to eat it regardless of what it was.

Coyle looked at the grubs. Archer could feel him looking even in the dark.

"Eat them first," Archer said. "Don't think about it."

Coyle ate. He didn't say anything about it and Archer didn't either. When he was done Archer gave him the roots and watched him work through those, and then the ginger, and then there was nothing left and the fog was still there and the dark was still there and dawn was still hours away.
