He came back through the fog with both hands full. Some kind of tuber he'd dug from the creek bank, a handful of berries he was reasonably sure about, and a section of bamboo he'd cut and capped with mud to hold water. He moved without sound. The fog sat on everything, thick and warm, and visibility was nothing past arm's length.

Coyle was where he'd left him. Back against the root mass of a fallen dipterocarp, legs out, the bad one elevated on a coil of exposed root. His breathing was audible from four feet away. Shallow and fast, like a man running in his sleep.

Archer set the food down and was turning to check the perimeter when he saw it.

The python had come in from the left, following the smell of blood from Coyle's leg. It was already inside the root mass, moving slow and deliberate the way they do, the way that looks like patience but isn't. The head was up. Maybe three feet from Coyle's extended leg. The body behind it was as thick as Archer's forearm and it went back into the fog and disappeared.

Archer did not reach for the rifle.

He pulled the Ka-Bar and moved. No wasted motion, no hesitation. He came around the root mass from the right side, low, and got his left hand behind the snake's head before it could turn and got the knife in just below the skull and drove it through and into the ground. The python went into a full-body convulsion, the tail end of it coming out of the fog and thrashing through the leaf litter, and Archer held the head pinned until the movement slowed and then stopped.

He sat back on his heels.

Coyle was awake. He had the .38 in his hand, pointed more or less at the snake, and his eyes were wide and his breathing was worse than before.

"Problem solved," Archer said.

Coyle looked at the snake. He looked at Archer. He lowered the revolver.

"How long was it."

"Long enough."

Coyle put his head back against the root. "I didn't hear a thing."

"They don't make noise."

"I meant you."

Archer wiped the Ka-Bar on the leaf litter and sheathed it. He picked up the python's head and walked the length of it back into the fog, counting. He came back.

"Eleven feet," he said. "Give or take."

"Jesus."

"Protein," Archer said. "We're going to eat it."

Coyle looked at him.

"You said protein."

"I did."

Archer went to work on the snake with the Ka-Bar. He'd done it before, not in Laos but in training, and the technique came back without effort. He skinned it back from the cut, worked the body free, sectioned it. The fog held everything close and quiet. Somewhere out in the dark a bird called once and went silent.

He gave Coyle the tuber and the berries first. Coyle ate without comment, which was a good sign. A man who's given up doesn't eat. He chewed the tuber slowly and drank from the bamboo section and didn't ask what any of it was.

Archer ate standing up, watching the perimeter. The snake meat was pale and dense and tasted like nothing much. He ate his share and then some. He'd been moving for hours and his body had opinions about that.

"You should sleep," Coyle said.

"I'm fine."

"You've been out there for—" Coyle stopped. "A while."

"I'll sleep when it's light." Archer crouched and checked Coyle's leg without asking. The swelling had not gone down. The discoloration had spread past the knee, a dark bruised color in the pale glow of the fog. He pressed two fingers against the skin and Coyle made a sound through his teeth.

"That bad," Coyle said. It wasn't a question.

"It's what it is."

"Meaning."

"Meaning we need to get you out of here before it gets worse." Archer sat back. "Sandy comes back at first light. Fog breaks, we're out."

"And if it doesn't break."

Archer didn't answer that. There was no answer that helped.

Coyle was quiet for a moment. The fog moved around them, slow and indifferent. The python's skin lay coiled in the leaf litter where Archer had dropped it, pale and geometric, already going still.

"You saved my life," Coyle said. "Twice now, I think."

"Don't count."

"I'm counting."

"Get some sleep," Archer said. "You need to be able to move when the time comes."

Coyle shifted against the root mass, adjusting his leg with both hands, and closed his eyes. His breathing was still too fast but it steadied some. Archer watched him until it did.

Then he turned back to the perimeter and the fog and the dark, the rifle across his knees, and he sat with it the way a man sits with something that has no resolution yet, waiting for the light.
