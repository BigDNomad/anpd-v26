The command post was a shallow depression in the hillside, roofed with cut bamboo and a single layer of canvas that had gone green with mold. Bounchanh stood at the center of it and looked at his field commanders and said nothing for a long moment. There were four of them. They stood in a row the way men stand when they expect to be struck.

Outside, the fog had thinned enough to see the nearest tree line. Patrols were moving through it. He could hear them, the soft sounds of men pushing through wet brush, the occasional low voice quickly silenced. All of that activity. All of those men.

He looked at the commanders.

"Tell me what you have found," he said.

The senior of them, a lieutenant named Phommasack, spoke first. He described the sectors covered, the cordons established, the trails watched through the night. He spoke carefully, the way a man speaks when he knows the words are not enough.

Bounchanh let him finish.

"You have found nothing," Bounchanh said.

"The fog—"

"I know about the fog. The fog was the same for them as for you. Two men. One of them cannot walk without help. You have three companies in this valley and you cannot find two men, one of whom cannot walk without help."

Phommasack said nothing.

Bounchanh moved to the edge of the canvas overhang and looked out at the tree line. The fog was still thick in the low ground, pooled in the ravines, hanging in the canopy. He had committed everything he had to this. He had pulled men off the trail security details, off the resupply routes. He had done this because the prize was worth it. A CIA pilot. American. The names he carried, the routes, the network — that was worth more than anything his men might find on the trail in a month of operations. Hanoi had made that clear to him through channels he did not ignore.

He turned back.

"The American pilot is injured," he said. "His companion is a rescue soldier, not a line infantryman. They have been in the jungle since yesterday. They have no food, no shelter, no support on the ground." He paused. "And yet here we are."

The second commander, a man called Bounmy, started to speak. Bounchanh raised one hand and Bounmy stopped.

"I am not interested in explanations," Bounchanh said. "I am interested in results. You will give me results or I will find commanders who can."

He said it without raising his voice. That was the thing about Bounchanh — he did not need to raise his voice. The men who had served under him long enough knew what the quiet meant. Phommasack had served under him long enough. The color had gone out of his face.

"The fog will lift," Bounchanh said. "When it lifts, their aircraft will come back. They will attempt extraction. That is the window. Once that aircraft arrives, the American pilot is gone and everything he carries is gone with him." He looked at each of them in turn. "I want them before that aircraft arrives. I want them alive. Both of them. The rescue soldier is secondary — he can be damaged — but the pilot comes to me breathing and able to speak. Is that understood."

It was not a question.

"If the next report I receive tells me you have found nothing," Bounchanh said, "I will relieve you. All of you. I will send you north and I will find men who understand what is being asked of them." He let that settle. "The north is not a good place to be sent. You know this."

They knew this. The northern assignments were the punishment postings, the ones men did not come back from whole, if they came back at all.

"Tighten the cordons," Bounchanh said. "Push the patrols into the ravines. They will be in low ground — the injured man cannot climb. Follow the water. Follow the trails of least resistance." He moved back to the center of the space. "They are cold and they are hungry and the pilot is sick. They are not ghosts. They leave sign. Find the sign."

Phommasack said, "Yes, Major."

The others said the same.

Bounchanh dismissed them with a motion of his hand and they filed out into the thinning fog. He watched them go. He could hear the patrols in the brush, the low sounds of the search continuing, all those men moving through the wet jungle looking for two Americans who should have been found by now.

He thought about the pilot. What the man knew. What Hanoi wanted from him.

He thought about the rescue soldier, the one who had kept the pilot alive through the night. That one was the problem. That one was the reason the pilot was still out there instead of here.

He would deal with the rescue soldier when the time came.

He turned back to his maps and waited for the fog to lift.
