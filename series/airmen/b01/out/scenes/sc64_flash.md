The rain came without warning. One moment the canopy was dripping fog-wet and still, and then the sky opened and the water came down in sheets, not drops, a solid weight of it hitting the slope all at once.

Archer grabbed Coyle's arm and they kept moving uphill, Coyle taking most of Archer's weight on the left side, both of them leaning into the grade. The patrol was below them somewhere in the trees, maybe forty meters, maybe less. They'd heard voices twice in the last ten minutes. The fog had thinned enough to hear but not enough to see.

The slope changed under their feet.

It happened fast. The ground that had been soft became liquid, the soil releasing its grip on the rock beneath it, and the water that had been running in thin channels between the roots suddenly found a single path and that path was the one they were standing on. Archer felt it go — not a slip but a surrender, the whole surface of the hillside deciding at once to move — and then they were moving with it, both of them down, no footing, the mud taking them.

He reached for something and found nothing. Coyle went past him and Archer caught his collar and that was wrong too, they were both going, the flood carrying them downslope in a rush of brown water and uprooted vegetation, no control of speed or direction, the jungle floor coming up at them in pieces and then gone again. The sound of it was enormous. Water and mud and the cracking of small branches and somewhere below them the voices of men.

Archer's hand found a root. He didn't think about it. His fingers closed and the root held and the jolt of stopping nearly pulled his shoulder out. He had Coyle's collar in his other hand and Coyle swung around and hit the slope face-first and stopped.

They lay there. The water kept moving past them and over them, finding the low path, carrying everything that wasn't anchored. Archer's grip on the root did not loosen.

He could not tell how far they'd come down. Fifty meters. Maybe more. The voices were close now, very close, and he could hear the patrol moving through the brush below them, still climbing, still looking.

He did not move. He pressed his face into the mud and breathed through his nose and did not move.

The mud was everywhere. It had gone into his collar and his cuffs and the creases of his elbows, had packed itself into his hair and across his face and hands. He could feel it drying already in the places the rain wasn't hitting directly, tightening against his skin. Coyle was the same. They were the color of the hillside. They were the hillside.

The patrol came up through the trees.

There were four of them, maybe five — Archer couldn't count without lifting his head and he wasn't going to lift his head. He could hear their boots in the wet ground, the soft sucking sound of each step, and the low exchange of words between them, and the sound of a man breathing hard from the climb. They were close enough that Archer could hear the rain hitting their gear.

One of them stopped.

Archer felt Coyle go absolutely still beside him. The man stood there for what felt like a long time. Archer could hear him breathing. He was looking at something, or he was resting, or he was listening to the rain, and there was no way to know which. The mud on Archer's face had dried enough to pull at the skin around his eyes. He did not blink.

The man said something to the others. A short sentence. The others answered and kept moving.

The patrol went past them and up the slope, following the line the flood had cut, looking for two men who had gone uphill. The voices faded. The sound of boots in mud faded. The rain kept coming but lighter now, the first violence of it spent.

Archer held the root and did not move.

He counted. He got to two hundred and started again. The jungle made its sounds — water running, something moving in the canopy, the creak of a tree taking the weight of the rain. None of it was boots. None of it was voices.

He let go of the root.

He turned his head and looked at Coyle. Coyle's face was a mask of dried mud with two eyes in it. Those eyes were open and steady and looking back at him.

Archer put one finger to his lips.

Coyle nodded once, very small.

They lay there in the mud on the hillside while the rain slowed to a drip and the patrol worked its way up through the trees above them, looking in the wrong direction, and the jungle settled back into itself around them the way it always did, indifferent to what it had just covered over and kept.
