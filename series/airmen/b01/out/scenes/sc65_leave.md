The rain had slackened to a drizzle. The fog held.

Coyle spoke first, his voice low and flat, the way a man talks when he's already done the math.

"You move faster without me."

Archer didn't answer. He was watching the slope above them, the mud still creeping in slow dark tongues between the roots.

"That's not an opinion," Coyle said. "That's a fact. Your shoulder's good enough to climb. My leg isn't. You know where the rotors are coming at first light. You know the frequency, you know the grid. I've got rounds left." He shifted his weight and kept his voice even. "I can slow them down. Buy you the time you need."

Archer looked at him then.

"I'm not asking you to leave me to die," Coyle said. "I'm asking you to do the math. You're the faster man. You're the one who can be saved. That's not sentiment, that's the mission."

The fog moved around them, thick and close, and somewhere upslope a branch cracked under the weight of water. Neither man moved.

"How many rounds," Archer said.

"Enough."

"How many."

Coyle was quiet a moment. "Four."

Archer looked back up the slope.

"Four rounds and a bad leg," Coyle said, "and I can still make noise in the right direction. Give them something to chase that isn't you. You get to the LZ, you get Sandy on the radio at first light, you get out." He paused. "That's how this works."

"No," Archer said.

Just the one word. No weight behind it, no anger. The way you say no to something that was never a real question.

Coyle watched him. He'd expected argument, or at least the performance of it — some back-and-forth where he could press his case, wear the man down with logic. There was none of that. Archer had already been to the place Coyle was trying to lead him and had come back from it, and the door was shut.

"You're not thinking clearly," Coyle said.

"I'm thinking fine."

"Your job is to get out. That's the whole job. That others may live — that's your people, that's your creed. You said it yourself."

"I know what I said."

"Then act like it."

Archer turned and looked at him fully. His face was mud and shadow, the whites of his eyes the only clean thing on him. He didn't look angry. He didn't look conflicted. He looked like a man who had already made his decision and was waiting for the other man to catch up to it.

"You want to talk about the creed," Archer said, "then you know it doesn't have a clause that says leave your man in the mud."

"I'm not your man. I'm a liability."

"You're the mission."

"The mission is getting you out."

"No," Archer said again. Same tone. Same finality. "The mission is getting you out. That's why I'm here. That's the only reason any of this happened."

Coyle opened his mouth and closed it. The rain ticked against the leaves above them. He could feel his leg, the deep throb of it, the heat that had spread past the knee now and was working its way up. He knew what that meant and he'd known it for a while and he hadn't said it because saying it wouldn't change anything.

"You're going to carry me up that hill," Coyle said.

"If I have to."

"That's insane."

"Probably."

"You'll kill us both."

Archer didn't answer that. He turned back to the slope, scanning the fog, reading whatever he could read in it. His shoulder was bad — Coyle had seen him favor it, seen the way he'd held the arm close after the root caught them — but he wasn't going to say so and Coyle wasn't going to get anywhere by pointing it out.

The standoff held. Neither man moved.

Coyle had made his case the only way he knew how to make it, stripped of everything that wasn't logic, and the logic was sound. He knew it was sound. The numbers worked. Four rounds, a compromised leg, one man who could move and one who couldn't — the math was simple and it pointed one direction.

But Archer wasn't doing math. Or he was doing a different kind, one where the variables weren't rounds and speed and distance but something else, something Coyle didn't have a name for and wasn't sure he'd earned the right to argue against.

He looked at the revolver in his hand. Four rounds. He'd been holding it since the patrol passed, his thumb resting on the hammer, not cocked, just resting.

He lowered it.

Above them the fog moved and the jungle dripped and somewhere in the dark the hillside was full of men looking for them, and Archer was still watching the slope, and neither of them spoke again for a long time.
