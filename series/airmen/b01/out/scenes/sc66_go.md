The rain had dropped to a drizzle, barely audible now against the leaves. The fog sat on them like something solid.

Coyle spoke first.

"We keep moving away from them, they keep moving toward us. That's how this ends."

Archer didn't answer right away. He was listening to the hillside above them, the dark and the wet and whatever was in it.

"I know how it ends," he said.

"Then you know we can't wait on the weather."

Archer looked at him. Coyle's face was pale where the mud had dried and cracked at the edges. His breathing was still too fast, too shallow. The leg was worse — Archer could see it in the way Coyle held himself, the careful stillness of a man managing pain rather than resting from it.

"You're telling me we should go at them," Archer said.

"I'm telling you that right now they know where we're going before we do. We're reacting to them. Every move we make is a response to pressure they're applying." Coyle paused, steadied his breathing. "You flip that, they have to start protecting themselves. They lose people chasing us, they start thinking about their own exposure. They can't run a cordon and watch their flanks at the same time."

"There's a battalion out there."

"There's a battalion looking for two men who've been running. They're not set up for contact. They're set up for capture."

Archer turned that over. It wasn't wrong. Three companies spread across a hillside in zero visibility, moving in file, watching for tracks and broken brush — that was a search formation, not a defensive one. Hit the right element at the right moment and the whole thing would have to stop and reorganize. Commanders would pull men back to anchor points. The cordon would compress, not expand.

"We've got four rounds and a Ka-Bar," Archer said.

"We've got four rounds, a Ka-Bar, and the fact that they don't know where we are right now." Coyle shifted his weight, jaw tightening against whatever the movement cost him. "That's the only window we get. Once the fog lifts and they reacquire us, it closes."

Archer looked up the slope. The fog was absolute up there, the tree line invisible, the patrol that had passed them an hour ago somewhere above and still moving in the wrong direction. Bounchanh's men believed they were following water, following low ground. They were wrong, and they didn't know they were wrong yet.

That was something.

The logic of it was clean enough. Stay reactive, stay prey. The enemy held the initiative as long as Archer and Coyle were the ones responding to contact rather than initiating it. Two men couldn't sustain a running defense against a battalion-strength search — not with one man's shoulder compromised and the other man's leg going bad. The math on that was simple and it only ran one direction.

But two men who decided where the next contact happened — that was a different equation. Not better odds, exactly. Different leverage.

Archer didn't like it. He liked the odds less than he liked anything he'd considered in the last several hours, and he'd considered some things he didn't want to think about again. Two men, one of them barely ambulatory, going offensive against a force that outnumbered them by a factor he didn't want to calculate. The margin for error was essentially nothing.

He looked at Coyle.

Coyle was watching him with the flat, patient expression of a man who had already done the math and was waiting for someone else to finish doing it.

"You pick the contact point," Coyle said. "You know this terrain better than they do. You've been reading it since we came down. Pick something that gives us the exit before we initiate, and we're not fighting them — we're just making them stop and look the wrong way long enough for us to move."

That was the difference. Not a firefight. A disruption. One sharp contact, enough noise and confusion to make Bounchanh pull his search elements back and establish a perimeter, and then Archer and Coyle were already somewhere else. The hunters spending the next hour watching their own shadows instead of following tracks.

It wasn't a plan that required winning. It only required making the other side feel like they might lose something.

Archer thought about the patrol that had passed them. Four men, maybe five, moving upslope in a loose file. They'd be turning back eventually, working back down. If they came back down the same line —

"All right," he said.

No elaboration. Coyle nodded once, the decision made and closed between them.

They were no longer the thing being driven. That was the shift — not dramatic, not clean, just a change in the direction of pressure. Archer would pick the ground. Coyle would be where Archer put him. The next contact would happen because they chose it.

Archer checked the Ka-Bar and started moving upslope, slow and quiet, reading the fog.
