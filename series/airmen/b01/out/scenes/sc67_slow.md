Coyle's breathing had changed. Archer heard it first — the shallow pull of air, the pause before the exhale, the way a man's body starts rationing what it knows is running short. He'd heard it before, in training cadavers and in the field, and he knew what it meant. The body was doing the math.

They were on the third ridge. Archer had kept them moving since the patrol passed, trading the lower ground for elevation, staying off the obvious lines. Every time he found a position that offered cover he gave it twenty minutes and moved them again. No fixed point. Nothing for a search pattern to triangulate.

"Stop," Coyle said.

Archer stopped.

Coyle leaned against a tree and breathed. His leg was out in front of him, the boot unlaced to accommodate the swelling. The discoloration had moved past the knee. In the fog and dark Archer couldn't see the color but he could feel the heat radiating off the skin when he checked the dressing, and heat like that meant the infection was winning.

"How far to the LZ," Coyle said.

"Far enough."

"That's not a number."

"No," Archer said. "It's not."

Coyle understood that. He didn't ask again.

The fog sat on them like something with weight. Visibility was arm's length, maybe less on the upper slope where the cloud was thicker. It was the only thing working for them. Bounchanh's men couldn't see any better than they could, and a battalion moving through zero-visibility karst jungle made noise. Archer had been listening to that noise for the last hour — the occasional clatter of equipment, a voice cut short, the sound of men moving in a line. They were close. They were also everywhere.

That was the arithmetic Archer had been running since the Jolly Green turned back. Two men. One of them dying by degrees. Eight rounds between them. A battalion plus whatever Tran had added to the net, and Tran's people were NVA recon, which meant they were quiet and they were patient and they knew how to read ground. The LZs were covered. Every flat piece of terrain within three ridges had eyes on it or guns near enough to make no difference. Sandy wasn't coming until first light at the earliest, and first light was a long way off, and the weather might not cooperate even then.

He didn't let himself sit with that too long.

"We move in five," he said.

Coyle nodded. He was conserving words now, which was its own kind of signal.

Archer worked the terrain in his head. The next ridge offered a saddle that might give them a dead angle from the valley — a place where two search lines would overlap on paper but leave a gap in practice, because men got tired and gaps happened. He'd seen it work. He'd also seen it not work, and he knew which was more common.

He was checking the slope above them when he saw it. Faint, in the fog, but there. A length of wire running low between two trees, maybe shin height, tied off to something he couldn't make out in the dark. He held up a fist and Coyle went still.

Archer moved toward it slowly, staying low, and crouched to look without touching. The wire ran to a grenade pinned to the tree on the far side, the pin pulled and held only by the tension of the line. Standard tripwire. Simple. Effective. He traced the line with his eyes and found a second one running parallel, offset by about four feet. They'd bracketed the approach.

He moved back to Coyle.

"They're wiring the routes," he said, low. "Escape corridors. They're not just searching, they're funneling."

Coyle looked at him.

"They know where we're going," Archer said. "Or they know where we have to go."

That was the difference between a search and a hunt. A search covered ground. A hunt covered options.

He thought about the saddle. If Bounchanh's people had thought of the saddle — and Tran's recon would have thought of the saddle — there would be wire there too. Maybe a position. Maybe both.

Coyle shifted his weight and his breath caught. He held it until the pain passed, then let it out slow.

"Tell me something useful," Coyle said.

Archer looked at him. The fog moved between them.

"We go around," he said. "Longer. Harder ground. But we go around."

Coyle looked down at his leg. He didn't say anything about the leg. He said, "Okay."

That was the thing about Coyle. He didn't argue the math. He just asked what came next.

Archer helped him up, took the weight on his good shoulder, and they moved off the line and into the trees, angling away from the wire and the saddle and the obvious ground, into the harder dark where the karst broke the slope into something no search pattern could cover cleanly.

The fog held. For now, the fog held.
