Archer found the first one by the way the undergrowth didn't move right.

The fog sat heavy in the trees, chest-high, and he'd been reading the jungle floor by feel as much as sight — the way a root crossed the path, the way a branch had been bent and not sprung back. He stopped and put his hand out behind him without looking. Coyle stopped.

Archer crouched. The tripwire was monofilament, nearly invisible, strung shin-high between two stakes driven into the soft earth. The grenade was a ChiCom stick type, the wooden handle wedged into a fork of root, the wire looped through the pull ring. Tension set for a walking man. Set for an American moving fast and not watching his feet.

He looked at Coyle and held up one finger. Then he pointed at the wire.

Coyle nodded. He didn't say anything. He hadn't said much since the mudslide.

Archer worked the pin back in first, pressing the safety lever flat with his thumb while he reseated the cotter pin with the other hand. When it was safe he pulled the stake on the near side, coiled the wire, and handed the grenade back to Coyle. Then the stake. Then the wire coil. Coyle held each item without being told to, tucking them against his chest.

There were four more in the next hundred meters.

Two were the same ChiCom stick type. One was a Soviet-pattern fragmentation grenade, smaller, the wire looped through a ring on the base. The last one was rigged differently — the wire ran across the path at ankle height and then doubled back, a secondary line Archer almost missed, set to catch someone who'd stepped over the first and thought they were clear. Whoever had set that one knew what they were doing.

He took them all apart the same way. Pins reseated, wires coiled, stakes pulled. He handed each component back to Coyle and Coyle held them.

When he had all five devices disassembled he found a place where the undergrowth thickened on both sides of the path and the ground was soft enough to take a stake without noise. He worked from memory of the terrain behind them — the approach line, the natural funnel where the ridgeline pinched down and forced movement through a narrow corridor. Anyone following would come through there. They wouldn't have a choice.

He drove the first stake with the heel of his hand, slow pressure rather than impact, and ran the wire across the path at knee height. The grenade went into the fork of a root on the uphill side, handle wedged tight, wire through the ring with just enough tension that a man's leg would pull it clean. He reset the pin last, leaving just enough cotter to hold until a body weight hit the wire.

Coyle watched. He was leaning against a tree with his bad leg extended, not putting weight on it. His breathing was audible in the fog.

Archer built the second trap ten meters back, offset from the path, in the undergrowth where a man might step if he'd jumped the first wire or gone around it. The third he placed at the edge of a small depression where the ground dropped away — a man moving fast in the dark would step down into it without seeing the wire until it was across his shin.

He staggered them so no two were in line of sight of each other. A single blast wouldn't tell you where the next one was. You'd have to stop and look, and stopping and looking took time, and time was what he was buying.

The last device he placed at the far end of the corridor, low in the brush, the wire running between two stakes at a height that would catch a man who'd gone to a crouch after the first detonation. That one was for whoever was leading the element. The man in front always went to a crouch.

He pulled Coyle off the tree and got him moving.

They put fifty meters between themselves and the trap field before Archer slowed the pace. The fog was still thick enough to muffle sound, which meant it would muffle the blasts too — not silence them, but compress them, make them harder to locate. The pursuing element would hear the first one and stop. They'd call it in. They'd wait for the second. By the time they understood what they'd walked into, Archer and Coyle would be another ridge over.

No radio call. No signal. There was nothing to report and no one to report it to until first light.

Archer kept his hand on Coyle's arm and moved them through the trees, reading the ground by feel, by the way the fog shifted when the terrain changed. Behind them the trap field sat in the dark, five independent devices, each one waiting for the weight of a moving body.

The jungle was quiet. The fog held.
