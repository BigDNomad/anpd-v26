The blast came from behind them and below, a hard flat crack that the fog swallowed before it finished, and then the secondary went — a half-second gap, then another, the staggered field working exactly as Archer had set it, each device triggering the next in sequence rather than all at once. Not a single contained event. A progression. Confusion by design.

Archer had Coyle moving before the echo died.

"Go," he said, and Coyle went, one hand on Archer's shoulder, the bad leg dragging but holding weight when it had to. They moved through the fog at the fastest pace Coyle could manage, which wasn't fast, but it didn't need to be. Behind them the squad had stopped. You could hear it in the absence of sound — no more brush movement, no more careful footfalls on wet stone. Just the fog and the ringing aftermath of the blast and whatever was happening back there in the dark.

Archer counted. He gave them thirty seconds to regroup and they wouldn't have it. The second detonation would have scattered the point element and the men behind them would be trying to figure out what they'd walked into, whether there were more, where the line of them ended. That was the point. Not to kill, though it would kill. To make the ground itself unreliable. To make every step a question.

He pushed Coyle through a gap between two karst formations, the rock slick with condensation, and they came out into a narrow corridor that ran roughly northeast. Archer had marked it on the way through. He'd marked everything on the way through.

Coyle's breathing was loud in the fog. It had been loud for a while now, that shallow rapid quality that meant the leg was pulling resources from everywhere else. Archer kept his hand at the man's back, not supporting him exactly, just present, a point of reference in the zero-visibility dark.

Behind them, nothing. Then voices, low and urgent, and then nothing again.

The squad was stopped. Trying to figure out the geometry of what had just happened to them. Trying to decide if the man in front of them was still alive and whether the ground ahead was safe to cross. That decision would take time. Time was the only currency that mattered right now and Archer had just bought a quantity of it.

They moved another forty meters before Coyle's leg gave and Archer caught him, took the weight on his good shoulder, kept them moving. The fog pressed in from all sides. Visibility was nothing. You navigated by feel and memory and the faint downslope pull of the terrain.

"How many," Coyle said. Not a question exactly.

"Two went," Archer said. "Maybe three."

Coyle didn't say anything to that. He'd stopped asking about the math of it.

Archer was thinking about something else now. He'd been thinking about it since the first detonation, that clean hard sound cutting through the fog, and what it meant. The trap field had worked. Not just worked — performed. Staggered, sequenced, placed in the natural funnel where the squad had to come through. He'd built it in the dark with a bad shoulder and a man who could barely stand, and it had done exactly what he'd designed it to do.

Which meant he could do it again.

That was the thing sitting at the edge of his thinking. They'd been running since the mudslide, reactive, taking what the terrain gave them. The fog, the karst, the narrow corridors — all of it used defensively, buying distance, buying time. And that was right. That was the correct play when you had an injured man and no extraction window and three companies between you and the ridgeline.

But the trap field changed the arithmetic.

The squad behind them wasn't pursuing anymore. They were stopped, regrouping, trying to reestablish contact. And the other elements — the ones Bounchanh had spread across the hillside — they'd heard the blasts too, muffled by fog but audible, and they'd be adjusting, pulling toward the sound, trying to figure out what it meant. Which meant the corridors those elements had been covering were momentarily thinner.

Archer filed that away.

They had components left. Not many, but enough. And he knew the terrain ahead better than the men behind him did, because he'd walked it once already and he'd been paying attention. There were two more natural funnels between here and the ridgeline. Places where the karst forced movement into predictable lines.

He wasn't thinking about escape anymore. Not only about escape.

He was thinking about where to put the next one.

Coyle stumbled and caught himself, and Archer steadied him without breaking stride. The fog was total. The jungle was quiet except for the drip of condensation off the canopy and the sound of Coyle's breathing and somewhere behind them, still distant, the sound of men trying to decide whether to move forward.

They kept moving northeast.

Archer kept thinking.
