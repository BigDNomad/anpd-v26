The trap field had worked. That was the problem.

Archer knew it the moment the last grenade cooked off and the jungle went quiet except for the screaming. Two men down, maybe three, and the squad behind them had stopped cold. He'd felt it — the shift in the pursuit, the hesitation, the way a unit sounds when it's counting its dead. And something in him had moved toward that feeling instead of away from it.

He found the bend in the trail where the karst pushed the path into a tight arc, limestone on the left rising sheer, dense secondary growth on the right, the whole corridor narrowing to maybe four meters across. A natural choke. Anything coming through would have to come through single file or close to it.

He got Coyle positioned twelve meters back, behind a fallen dipterocarp that had come down in the slide and wedged itself between two boulders. Low. Still. The man's leg was bad enough now that still was about all he had left.

"Don't move," Archer said.

"I know."

"I mean it. Whatever you hear."

Coyle looked at him. His face was gray under the mud. "I know what I mean."

Archer took his position at the bend, prone behind a root mass, the survival rifle up. He had the angle. He had the choke point. He had maybe forty seconds before the lead element came around the curve.

He waited.

They came in pairs, which told him something, but not enough. The first two men moved carefully, rifles up, reading the ground. Behind them a third, then a fourth. He let them come. Let them fill the kill zone. When he fired it was controlled, two shots, and both men in front went down, and then the jungle erupted.

The volume of return fire was not from a squad.

It came from both sides of the trail and from behind the men he'd shot and from somewhere upslope to his left that he hadn't accounted for, and it was sustained, overlapping, the kind of fire that doesn't pause to assess because there are enough rifles that pausing is optional. He was already moving, rolling left into the root mass, pressing flat while rounds cut through the growth above him at chest height, and he understood then what he'd done.

He'd read the patrol as a lead element and stopped thinking past it.

The firing continued. He counted rates, counted pauses, counted the distinct sound signatures of weapons cycling, and the number was wrong. Badly wrong. Not a squad. Not two squads. A company or close to it, spread across the hillside in an arc that had been tightening since before he'd picked his position, and he'd walked into the closing end of it.

He got low and moved back toward Coyle, staying below the fire, using the root mass and then a shallow depression in the karst that ran parallel to the trail. The rounds were tracking him — not accurately, but the volume made accuracy secondary. They were walking fire across the vegetation and something would find him eventually if he stayed.

He reached Coyle.

"How many," Coyle said. It wasn't a question.

"More than I thought."

He got his arm under Coyle's and pulled him upright and they moved, Coyle's leg dragging, his breath coming in short pulls that Archer could feel through the man's ribcage. Behind them the firing was reorganizing, the random volume settling into something more deliberate, more directed. They were getting their bearings.

Then he heard it. Off to the right, upslope, the sound of men moving through brush. Not the main element. A separate movement, slower, deliberate, angling across the hillside in a wide arc. He stopped and listened and the arc was wrong for a flanking attack on his position. It was wrong for that.

It was right for Coyle's position. Where Coyle had been. Where they were going.

The flanking element was cutting across the terrain ahead of them, not behind, and the angle of movement was closing toward the fallen dipterocarp and the boulders and the ground beyond it. They were not reacting to where Archer had been. They were moving to where Archer would have to go.

He stood there with Coyle's weight against his shoulder and worked it through. The main element behind them. The flanking element ahead and to the right. The karst wall to the left. The trail below, which was covered.

The arc was closing.

He had his remaining components and a rifle and a man who couldn't run and four rounds in a .38 that belonged to someone else, and the fog that had been their cover all night was thinning now at the edges, the gray beginning to separate from the gray, and somewhere above the canopy the sky was doing something that looked like morning.

The flanking element kept moving. Unhurried. Patient. Like men who knew where the ground ended.
