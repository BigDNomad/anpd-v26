The flanking element was close enough that Archer could hear them moving through the brush to his left, not thirty meters out, and the main element behind was already pushing forward again. He fired twice into the dark—controlled, aimed at sound and movement—and grabbed Coyle by the collar.

"Up," he said.

Coyle got one leg under him and Archer took the rest, hauling him bodily off the ground and driving laterally into the undergrowth, away from the closing flank. Not back, not forward. Sideways, into the gap that wasn't a gap yet but would be for the next thirty seconds if they moved.

They moved.

The flanking element opened up and the rounds came through the canopy high and wide, cutting leaves, and then something caught Coyle across the left side—a ricochet or a fragment, Archer couldn't tell—and Coyle made a sound that wasn't a word and went heavy in Archer's grip. Archer didn't stop. He kept moving, pulling Coyle's arm across his shoulders, taking the weight, pushing through a stand of bamboo that raked them both and came out the other side into a shallow draw that ran perpendicular to the enemy's line of advance.

He went down the draw at a crouch, fast as the ground allowed.

Behind them the firing intensified for a moment, then slackened. The two elements were talking to each other now, sorting out where their targets had gone. Archer used the time. He fired the survival rifle twice more into the brush behind them, not at anything he could see, just noise and delay, and kept moving.

The draw bent left and he followed it, and after another fifty meters the sound of pursuit dropped back to something manageable. He found a boulder the size of a truck and put it between them and the direction they'd come from and stopped.

Coyle was breathing in short pulls. His left hand was pressed against his side, just below the ribs, and when Archer pulled the hand away the shirt was wet.

"Through and through," Coyle said. "I think."

Archer checked it in the gray pre-dawn light. Entry wound, no exit he could find immediately, which meant it wasn't through anything. He packed it with the last of the field dressing and pressed Coyle's hand back over it.

"Hold that."

"I know how to hold a wound."

"Then hold it."

He checked the survival rifle. Three rounds. The .38 had four. Seven rounds total between them, and the enemy had three companies spread across the hillside and was now actively converging on their last known position.

He had done this. That was the fact he couldn't put down.

After the trap field had worked—after the blasts had rolled through the fog and the pursuing squad had stopped and gone quiet—there had been a window. Not a wide one, but real. The terrain had opened briefly to the northeast, and the enemy's attention had been on the smoke and the dead, and Archer had known it and had chosen to fight instead of run. He'd told himself it was tactical. That he was buying time, creating distance, shaping the pursuit. And some of that was true.

But some of it was the satisfaction of the trap field working. Some of it was the clean feeling of having outthought them, and wanting to press it.

Hubris was the word his first instructor had used, a retired Marine who'd spent two years in Korea and had no patience for men who confused competence with invincibility. *The jungle will kill you for being good*, the man had said. *It kills you faster for knowing you're good.*

Coyle hadn't had a vote. That was the part that sat in Archer's chest like a stone. Coyle was a man with a ruined leg and an infected wound and four rounds in a revolver, and he had trusted Archer's judgment because he had no other option, and Archer had used that trust to indulge something that wasn't necessity.

Now Coyle had a hole in his side to go with everything else.

"How bad is the side," Archer said.

"Hurts."

"That's not what I asked."

Coyle thought about it. "I can move."

"I know you can move. I'm asking how bad."

"Bad enough that you should stop asking and start moving."

Archer looked at him for a moment. Coyle's face was gray in the early light, jaw set, eyes steady. Whatever he was feeling he was keeping it behind his teeth.

"LZ is northeast," Archer said. "Sandy comes up at first light. We need to be there."

"Then let's go."

Archer got him up again, took the weight across his shoulders, and started moving northeast through the thinning fog. Seven rounds. A wounded man bleeding through a field dressing. Dawn coming up through the canopy in pale strips.

He'd gotten them into this. He'd get them out of it or he wouldn't, but the debt was his and he knew it, and he carried it the same way he carried Coyle—without putting it down, without complaint, one step at a time through the dark.
