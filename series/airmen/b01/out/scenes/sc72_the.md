The fog was absolute. Archer could not see his own hand at arm's length.

He called out.

"Coyle. Talk to me."

His voice carried flat and strange in the wet air, no echo, just absorption. He waited. Heard movement to his left, the soft drag of a boot.

"Here," Coyle said.

Loud enough. Good.

They were in the shallow draw, the valley walls rising on both sides, and the fog had filled it like water fills a bowl. The Pathet Lao were somewhere upslope, moving by sound now, the same as everyone else. Archer had been listening to them for the better part of an hour. The way they moved — careful, stopping, listening — told him they were close but not certain. They had a direction. They did not have a position.

He needed them to have a position.

"Coyle," he said again. Conversational. "You still with me?"

"I'm with you." Coyle's voice came from the same place, a little stronger. He understood what they were doing.

Archer heard it then — the change in the movement upslope. The careful stepping had stopped. They were orienting.

He shifted behind the boulder, got the survival rifle up, and waited.

The fog brought them down the draw the way water finds a channel. He heard them before he saw them — the brush of equipment against vegetation, a boot finding loose stone, the small sounds of men moving in a compressed column because the valley walls gave them nowhere else to go. Then shapes in the gray. Then men.

He fired.

The survival rifle first, twice, and the shapes at the front of the column went down. He transitioned to the .38 and fired the remaining four rounds into the mass of them, working left to right, not rushing it. Coyle opened up from his position, four rounds, and the column broke apart. Men went sideways into the brush, some of them shouting, and the fog swallowed the sound and gave it back distorted so that it seemed to come from everywhere.

Archer reloaded nothing. There was nothing to reload.

He listened.

The shouting had moved upslope. The movement sounds had reversed. He could hear men crashing back through the brush the way they had come, and then that sound faded too, and there was only the fog and the valley and the distant drip of water off the karst.

He stayed behind the boulder for a full count of sixty.

Then he went to Coyle.

Coyle was sitting with his back against the draw wall, the .38 in his lap, the cylinder open. His face was the color of old concrete. He had used all four rounds.

"They're gone," Archer said.

"For now."

"For now is enough."

He got Coyle up. It took longer than it should have. Coyle's leg had stiffened in the cold and the wet, and the first few steps were bad, and Archer took his weight without being asked. They moved out of the draw and into the valley floor, heading northeast.

The fog was changing.

It was subtle at first — a thinning at the edges, a slight brightening in the gray overhead. Archer had been watching for it. The fog had been their cover and their weapon both, and now it was becoming something else. In twenty minutes, maybe less, the valley floor would be visible from the ridgelines. Anyone up there with a rifle and a clear angle would have them.

He pushed the pace.

Coyle kept up. Whatever was left in him, he was spending it now, and he did not complain and he did not ask Archer to slow down. He breathed through his teeth and he moved.

The valley bent northeast and the terrain opened slightly, the walls pulling back, and Archer could see farther now than he had been able to see all night. That was the fog lifting. That was the clock running.

He had the alternate LZ fixed in his head — a cleared shelf on the northeast slope, tight but workable, the kind of place a Jolly Green could hover if the pilot was good and the air was calm. Sandy would be inbound at first light. Sandy would be looking.

They had to be there.

The fog was knee-high now in the low places, pooling in the hollows, and above it the air was clear and pale with the coming dawn. Archer could see the ridgeline to the east beginning to separate from the sky. He could see the karst above them, the limestone faces going gray and then orange at the very top where the light was catching.

He did not look at Coyle's leg.

He knew what was there. The heat, the swelling past the knee, the color that meant the infection had been working through the night while they moved and hid and fought and moved again. He knew what it meant and he knew what it would mean if they did not get Coyle out in the next hour.

He kept moving.

The LZ was ahead. Sandy was coming.

That was all there was.
