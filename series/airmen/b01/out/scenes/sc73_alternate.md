The fog was breaking apart in long gray strips, pulling back from the ridgelines as the light came. Archer could see fifty meters now, then a hundred, the jungle resolving out of nothing into shapes and then into trees. He didn't like it.

"Move," he said.

Coyle moved. That was the thing about him — he moved when you told him to move. His leg had gone past the point where it registered on his face anymore. He just dragged it and kept going, one hand on the slope for balance, the other swinging free. His breathing was the thing that told you. Short and shallow, like a man trying not to use too much of something he was running low on.

Archer kept them off the ridgeline and below the crest, angling northeast through the secondary growth where the canopy thinned and the ground was open rock and root. The alternate LZ was a cleared shelf up there, maybe forty minutes at Coyle's pace. Maybe less if the man found something extra.

He didn't push him. He set a pace Coyle could hold and held it himself, watching the slope above and the slope below and the gray light spreading east over the karst.

At a rock outcrop he stopped and keyed the radio.

"Sandy, Two-Two Bravo. Radio check."

Static. Then the Skyraider pilot's voice, thin and compressed, coming through clean enough.

"Two-Two Bravo, Sandy. Read you Lima Charlie. Go ahead."

"Inbound to alternate. Confirm Jolly Green status."

"Jolly Green is airborne. Your window is inside the hour. Confirm you're mobile."

"Mobile," Archer said.

He didn't say anything else. Not the leg, not the ammunition, not the fact that they'd burned everything they had in the draw and were coming out of this with a Ka-Bar and whatever Coyle had left in his .38. Sandy didn't need that information. Sandy needed to know they were moving and where they were going.

"Alternate LZ," Archer said. "Northeast shelf. Confirm."

"Confirmed. Northeast shelf. Sandy will be on station. What's your ETA?"

Archer looked at Coyle. Coyle was leaning against the rock, his chest working. His eyes were open and he was watching Archer with the particular attention of a man who has decided to let someone else do the thinking.

"Inside the hour," Archer said.

"Copy. Sandy out."

He put the radio away. Coyle straightened off the rock without being told.

They went up.

The slope was steep enough that Archer had to use his hands on the rock faces, and Coyle did the same, pulling himself up on his arms where his leg wouldn't give him purchase. He didn't complain. He didn't ask how far. He just climbed, and Archer stayed close enough to catch him if he came off a hold, which he almost did twice.

The fog was gone from the upper slope now. Below them, in the valley, it still lay in long white pools between the trees, but up here the air was clear and the light was coming hard from the east, throwing shadows off every rock and fold in the ground. Archer didn't like that either. He kept them in the shadow side of the ridge, moving through the dark where the sun hadn't reached.

He thought about the men in the draw. He thought about the column breaking and going upslope and whether they'd reoriented yet, whether Bounchanh had figured out the northeast shelf. He thought about the flanking element that had been moving ahead of them and where it was now.

He stopped thinking about it. There was nothing to do with any of it except move.

Coyle went down once, his bad leg folding on a loose rock, and Archer caught him by the arm and held him until he got his weight sorted out. Coyle's arm was hot through the sleeve. Not just warm. Hot.

"I'm good," Coyle said.

"I know."

They kept going.

The shelf came into view through the trees — a long pale scar on the hillside where the rock had sheared away and left a flat open space maybe thirty meters across. Not much. Enough. The Jolly Green could hover over it and drop the penetrator and that was all it needed to do.

Archer brought them to the tree line and stopped. He scanned the shelf, the approaches, the ridgeline above. Nothing moved. The light was full now and the shadows were sharp and he could see everything there was to see.

He keyed the radio again.

"Sandy, Two-Two Bravo. We're at the alternate. In the tree line, north side."

"Copy, Two-Two Bravo. Jolly Green is inbound. Hold position. Sandy will be overhead in figures."

"Copy," Archer said.

He looked at Coyle. Coyle had his back against a tree and his eyes were half-closed and his chest was still doing that shallow work. But he was upright. He'd made it up the slope.

"Hear that?" Archer said.

"I heard it," Coyle said.
