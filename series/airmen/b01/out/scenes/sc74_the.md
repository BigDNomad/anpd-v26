Coyle said it flat, no preamble. "The rotors."

Archer looked at him.

"The cipher rotors. I left them in the aircraft." Coyle's voice had gone hoarse overnight, the fever working on him. "They cannot be there when Bounchanh's people reach the wreck."

"We have an extraction window."

"I know what we have."

Archer shifted his weight, looked back through the tree line toward the LZ shelf. The fog was pulling off the valley in long gray sheets, the light coming up pale and thin through the canopy. Maybe forty minutes before the ridgeline had eyes on the whole northeast slope.

"How far back," Archer said.

"Fifteen minutes. Maybe twenty the way we came."

"And the rotors are where."

"Cockpit. Behind the seat, in a canvas bag. You'll know it."

Archer didn't say anything for a moment. He was doing the math and the math was not good but Coyle was right and they both knew it. Names on those rotors. Routes. The whole network encoded in the cipher settings, and the Pathet Lao had people who could read what they found if they had the right help, and they had the right help. Tran's people would know exactly what to do with them.

"You can't go back," Archer said. It wasn't an argument. It was a statement of fact about Coyle's leg, about the fever, about the forty meters of hillside they'd just spent the last hour crossing.

"No," Coyle said. "I can't."

That was the end of it.

Archer pulled the .45 from his holster. He dropped the magazine into his palm, checked it, seated a fresh one from his vest, and racked the slide. He held the pistol out to Coyle grip-first.

"Seven rounds," Archer said. "Don't use them on anything you're not sure about."

Coyle took it. His hand was steadier than Archer expected.

Archer took the .38 from Coyle's belt. He turned the cylinder, checked the loads. Four rounds. He'd fired the other two in the draw. He closed the cylinder and put it in his pocket.

"Sandy's going to be on station," Coyle said.

"I know."

"If you're not back—"

"I'll be back." Archer looked at him. "Stay in the tree line. Don't move toward the LZ until you hear me or hear Sandy call it clear. You understand?"

Coyle nodded.

There was nothing else to say. Archer turned and went back into the jungle.

Without Coyle he moved differently. The pace opened up immediately, his body finding a rhythm it hadn't been able to use since the crash. No weight to manage, no injured man to read, no pace to calibrate against someone else's pain threshold. He went through the undergrowth at a controlled run, staying low, using the terrain the way he'd been trained to use it. The fog was still thick enough in the draws to give him cover and he used that too, dropping into the low ground where the visibility closed back down to arm's length and the sound of his movement was swallowed by the wet air.

He moved through the place where they'd lain in the mud while the patrol passed overhead. The root he'd grabbed was still there, torn from the bank, trailing into the slide scar. He didn't slow down.

The wreck was where they'd left it. The fuselage had settled further into the hillside overnight, the tail section canted at a steeper angle. He went in through the cockpit door, which had been sprung in the crash and hung open at a wrong angle. The smell of avgas and burned insulation was still there but he wasn't thinking about that.

Behind the seat. Canvas bag.

He found it in thirty seconds. The bag was olive drab, military-issue, about the size of a hardback book. He pulled it out and unzipped it enough to see the rotors inside, three of them, machined aluminum, the kind of thing that looked like nothing if you didn't know what it was. He zipped it closed and put it inside his vest.

He was back out of the cockpit in under a minute.

He heard movement upslope. Not close, but not far. The fog was thinner here than it had been and the light was coming up fast. He went still for three seconds, listening. Two men, maybe three, moving parallel to his position, not toward him. Searching the wrong contour line.

He went back the way he'd come.

The return was faster than the approach. He knew the ground now, knew where the roots were and where the slope broke and where the mud from the slide had filled in the natural paths. He ran where he could run and moved where he had to move and he didn't stop.

Coyle was where he'd left him, back against a tree, the .45 across his knee.

Archer came out of the undergrowth and Coyle didn't raise the pistol, which meant he'd heard him coming, which was good.

Archer held up the canvas bag.

Coyle looked at it for a moment. Then he looked away, back toward the LZ shelf, where the fog was almost gone now and the sky above the karst was going from gray to pale yellow.

"Sandy should be close," Coyle said.

"Yeah," Archer said. "He should."
