The Jolly Green came over the ridgeline low and fast, rotors beating the cold air, and Sandy tucked in alongside on the port quarter like a shepherd working a flock of one. Below them the valley was still gray with the last of the fog, thinning now, pulling apart in long streamers where the morning air moved through the karst. The northeast slope was visible. The shelf was there.

Dalton held the Jolly Green in a wide orbit, getting the picture. The LZ was tight. Thirty meters of cleared ground on a hillside that wanted to be vertical. He'd worked worse.

Sandy's voice came over the radio. "Two-Two, I've got the shelf. Wind's light, northeast. You want to go in from the low side."

"Copy that," Dalton said.

They held their orbit and waited.

---

Coyle heard the Bird Dog before he saw it. The small engine, that flat buzzing sound, circling somewhere above the tree line. He'd been listening to it for what felt like a long time. It was the only thing in the world that mattered.

He was on his back in the hide, the .45 on his chest with both hands around it, the safety off. His leg was a separate country now, something that sent dispatches of pain up through his hip and into his spine but no longer felt like part of him. The fever had settled into a steady pressure behind his eyes.

The Bird Dog came around again. Low. The pilot was staying close.

The radio clicked. "Coyle. You with me."

He keyed it once. Yes.

"Help's close. You stay put. Don't move, don't transmit. I've got you."

He keyed it once more and let go of the transmit button.

The engine sound faded around the far side of the orbit and then came back. He tracked it with his ears the way a man in a dark room tracks a candle. As long as he could hear it, the world had not closed entirely.

He did not think about the leg. He did not think about Archer. He held the pistol and he listened to the engine and he waited, because there was nothing else to do and because the alternative to waiting was something he had already decided against.

---

Archer found the stream by sound. He went to his knees at the bank and put his face in the water and drank until his stomach pushed back, then sat up and breathed. His hands were shaking. He hadn't noticed that until now.

The canvas bag was across his shoulder. He could feel the weight of it, the rotors inside, solid and specific. He'd checked them twice at the wreck. They were there.

He looked at the water moving over the rocks. The light was coming. Not much, but coming. The fog was off the high ground now and the valley below was starting to show definition, the ridgelines separating themselves from the sky.

He stood up.

His legs held. His shoulder was a problem he was managing by not thinking about it. The return route was longer than the outbound leg because the outbound leg had been direct and the return had to account for where the patrol was, where the flanking element had been, where the fog no longer covered him.

He started moving.

The jungle was quiet in the way it gets just before full light, that held-breath moment when the night animals have gone and the day animals haven't started. He moved through it carefully, not fast, watching his footing. A turned ankle now would be the end of the math.

He thought about Coyle in the hide. The leg. The fever. The way Coyle had looked when Archer left him, that particular stillness of a man conserving everything he had left.

He did not let himself think about what happened if he was slow.

The rotors were in the bag. The bag was on his shoulder. He was moving. That was the whole of it.

The tree line on the north side of the shelf was maybe twenty minutes ahead. Maybe less if he pushed. He pushed.

Behind him, somewhere on the hillside, something moved in the brush. He stopped and listened. Nothing followed. A bird, maybe. Or the jungle settling into morning. He waited ten seconds and moved again.

The light was growing. He could see his hands now without effort. The fog was gone from the slope above him and the karst was showing its teeth against the sky, gray limestone going pale in the early light.

He had the rotors.

Coyle was in the hide.

Sandy and the Jolly Green were inbound.

Everything that came next would happen in the next thirty minutes or it would not happen at all. He understood that clearly, the way you understand things when the body has burned through everything that isn't essential and what's left is just the core of it, just the part that keeps moving.

He kept moving.
