The canvas bag was heavier than it had any right to be.

Archer stood at the stream, the rotors in his hands, and let the water run over his wrists. He'd found the cache where he'd left it, tucked under the root shelf above the waterline, the bag still sealed, still dry. He hadn't let himself think about what he'd do if it wasn't there. He thought about it now, briefly, and then stopped.

He knelt and drank from the stream with his good hand cupped. The water was cold and tasted of clay and he drank until his stomach said enough. His shoulder had stopped sending clear signals. It was just noise now, background, the kind of thing you learned to file away. He'd been filing it for hours.

He stood. Settled the bag across his back, the strap over the good shoulder. Started moving.

The return route was longer. He'd known it would be. The patrol he'd spotted on the outbound leg had shifted position, which meant the direct line was closed, which meant he was adding distance he didn't have time to add. He moved anyway. Not fast. Fast wasn't available to him anymore. But he moved, and that was the thing. That was the only thing.

The jungle was beginning to show light in the canopy. Not much. Just the first gray suggestion of it, filtering down through the upper story in thin columns where the fog had thinned enough to let it through. On the ground the fog still sat heavy in the low places, pooling in the draws, but it was lifting. He could see ten meters now where he'd been able to see three. That was good for him and bad for him in equal measure.

He picked his footing carefully. The hillside was still wet from the night's rain, the soil loose where the water had moved through it, and his boots found the soft spots and sank and he pulled them out and kept going. The bag shifted with each step. He adjusted and kept going.

He did not think about stopping. The thought didn't form. There was the route and there was the next step on the route and there was the bag on his back and there was Coyle in the hide with his ruined leg and his fever and his four rounds of .45, and all of that was one thing, not several things, and the one thing required him to keep moving. So he kept moving.

The body had opinions. He noted them and did not act on them.

He crossed a secondary drainage, stepping stone to stone, and came up the far bank into a stand of bamboo that rattled softly in a breath of air he couldn't feel on his skin. He stopped and listened. Nothing that wasn't jungle. He moved through the bamboo and out the other side.

The rotors were in his hands. That was the mission. That was what Coyle couldn't do and what Archer had gone back for and what was now secured. The names and the routes and the network, all of it encoded in the cipher settings, all of it riding in a canvas bag on a man who was running on water from a stream and whatever it was that kept a person moving when the body had already submitted its resignation. He didn't think about the weight of what the rotors meant. He thought about the next step.

He was close now. He could feel the terrain changing under his feet, the slope angle shifting, the vegetation thinning at the edges where the shelf began to assert itself. The hide was northeast. Coyle was northeast. Sandy was inbound and the window was closing and the fog was lifting and the ridgeline would have eyes on the northeast slope inside thirty minutes if Bounchanh's people were worth anything, and they were worth something, he'd established that.

He moved faster. The shoulder registered its objection. He filed it.

The two halves of the operation had been running parallel since he'd left the hide — Coyle holding the ground, Archer recovering the objective — and now they were about to converge. He had the rotors. Coyle had the position. What came next was the part where everything either worked or it didn't, and the margin for it not working was Coyle's life and the network and whatever Bounchanh's people would do with a CIA pilot who knew too much and couldn't run.

He didn't let himself think past the next step.

The bamboo thinned. The shelf was ahead, the cleared ground pale in the early light, the fog sitting low at the edges like something that hadn't decided yet whether to leave. He could see the tree line. He could see the hide position.

He could see that something was wrong.

He stopped.

Stood very still in the last of the tree cover and looked at the hide and looked at the ground around it and looked at the tree line on the far side of the shelf, and the jungle was quiet in the way that jungle is quiet when something has already happened in it.

He unslung the bag and held it in his good hand and moved forward.
