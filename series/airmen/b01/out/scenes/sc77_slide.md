The fog had thinned to gauze. Coyle could see ten feet in any direction now, which meant they could see him.

He heard them before he saw them. Not footsteps — the fog swallowed footsteps — but the small sounds men make when they move through brush and believe they are quiet. A branch released. Cloth against bark. The particular silence that follows when a man stops and listens.

He counted four positions by sound. Maybe five. The hide was a shallow depression under a root shelf, good cover from above, useless from the sides. He'd known that when Archer put him here. It had been a reasonable calculation at the time.

The radio crackled.

"Coyle, Sandy. You read?"

He keyed it once. Yes.

"I've got eyes on your position. You've got company. Three sides, maybe four. I count eight, ten men. They're inside fifty meters and closing."

He keyed it once.

"Can you move?"

He looked at his leg. The swelling had gone past the knee and the skin had taken on a color that had no business being on a living man. He'd stopped feeling it an hour ago, which was either good or very bad.

He keyed it twice. No.

"Understand. Sandy out."

That was the whole conversation. Sandy was a professional and professionals didn't waste words on what couldn't be changed.

Coyle had four rounds in the .45. Archer had left it with him before going for the rotors, and Coyle had thought at the time that it was a gesture — something to hold, something that felt like agency. He understood now it was the only thing Archer had to give.

He thought about what four rounds could do. Not escape. Not suppression. What four rounds could do was make noise and draw fire, and if the fire was accurate enough and fast enough, that was a way out that didn't require him to walk anywhere.

He raised the pistol and fired twice into the brush to his left, where the sounds were thickest.

The return fire came immediately, three or four weapons, and the rounds cut through the root shelf above him and threw dirt into his face. He pressed himself flat and waited. Nothing hit him. He fired once more, into the brush to his right this time, and heard a man shout something in Lao and heard the answering fire and felt the rounds strike the earth six inches from his hand.

Still nothing hit him.

He fired the last round straight up into the canopy.

The slide locked back. The pistol was empty and he was still alive, which was not the outcome he'd been working toward. He set the pistol down. It was no longer a weapon. It was just a piece of metal.

He thought about this for a moment. The choice had been made for him, which was a different thing than making a choice. He wasn't sure it mattered. The result was the same.

He reached for the knife on his belt. It was a standard-issue blade, nothing special, and he held it for a moment and thought about the angle and whether his hands were steady enough. They were not shaking. That surprised him. He brought the blade up toward his throat and felt the edge against his skin and thought, this is the thing, then, and felt something almost like calm.

The first man came over the root shelf from above and landed on his arm.

The weight drove his elbow into the ground and the knife skittered sideways and then there were two more of them and they had his wrists and someone was kneeling on his chest and the knife was gone. He tried to move and could not. He tried to reach the knife and could not. He stopped trying.

They were shouting at each other in Lao. He didn't speak Lao but he understood the tone, which was the tone of men who have been frightened and are now angry and are deciding what to do with that anger. One of them hit him across the face with an open hand. Not hard. Almost perfunctory, like a man checking whether something is real.

He was real.

They bound his wrists with cord and two of them lifted him by the arms and he heard himself make a sound when his leg moved, a sound he hadn't intended to make, and one of the soldiers looked at the leg and said something to the others and after that they were more careful with it. They wanted him functional. He understood what that meant.

They moved him out of the hide and into the open and the fog was thin enough now that he could see the hillside above him and the tree line and the cleared shelf of the LZ thirty meters up the slope. He could see all of it. He could see exactly where he needed to be and exactly how far away it was.

He was facing the wrong direction when they started moving him.
