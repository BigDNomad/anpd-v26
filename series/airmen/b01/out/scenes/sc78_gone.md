The hide was empty.

Archer came through the tree line low and stopped. The ground told it plainly. Scuff marks in the soft earth, a heel drag where someone had been pulled upright, the flattened grass where two or three men had knelt. He read it in three seconds and did not need to read it again.

The .38 was on the ground where Coyle had dropped it or where it had been knocked from his hand. Archer picked it up. The cylinder was open, one round fired, the brass still seated. Coyle had tried. They hadn't let him finish.

The whittled bird was beside it. Half-done, the beak roughed in, the wings not yet separated from the body. Archer picked that up too.

He stood there with both objects in his hands and the jungle was very quiet around him. The fog had thinned to gauze and through it the shelf was visible, the cleared ground, the place where the Jolly Green would have come down. Would have.

The radio crackled.

"Two-Two Bravo, Bird Dog. You copy."

He keyed it. "Bird Dog, Two-Two Bravo. Send it."

"I had eyes on the shelf. Watched them close the ring." The Bird Dog pilot's voice was flat and careful, the way a man speaks when he has seen something he cannot unsee and is choosing his words because there are no good ones. "Eight, maybe ten of them. They had him surrounded before he could move. He went down under them. They pulled him up. He was conscious." A pause. "They moved him out northeast. Toward the karst. I tracked them as long as I could."

Archer did not say anything.

"Two-Two Bravo."

"I'm here."

"I'm sorry," the Bird Dog pilot said. "I couldn't get low enough to do anything. The karst—"

"I know," Archer said. "Northeast toward the karst. Moving on foot."

"Affirmative. Moving on foot. He was upright. They were carrying some of his weight but he was upright."

Archer looked at the drag marks in the earth. Upright. That was something. That was also nothing.

"Bird Dog, Two-Two Bravo. Out."

He clipped the radio to his harness and stood in the thinning fog on the northeast slope of a hill in a country that did not officially exist and held a half-carved bird in one hand and an empty revolver in the other.

Ninety hours. He had kept the man alive for ninety hours. He had given his own blood, lain in the dark with a needle in his arm and watched it run down the tube, had killed a snake in the dark with a knife, had carried the man when the man could not walk, had gone back to a burning wreck for a canvas bag of rotors because Coyle had asked him to and because the mission required it. He had considered, once, in the dark, putting a round through the man's skull to spare him what was coming, and he had not been able to do it. He had told himself that was weakness. He was not sure anymore what it was.

Not dead. That was the thing. Dead would have been an ending. Dead would have been a body on the ground and a report filed and a name on a wall somewhere and the particular grief that comes with finality. This was not that. This was Coyle conscious and upright and moving northeast toward the karst with his wrists bound and his ruined leg dragging and whatever was in his head — the names, the routes, the network, all of it — moving with him toward whatever room Vorasak had prepared.

Archer knew what those rooms looked like. He had seen the aftermath of them.

He stood there a moment longer than he needed to. The fog moved through the trees in slow gray sheets. Somewhere above it the Bird Dog was still orbiting, the engine a faint drone at altitude, and somewhere further the Jolly Green was inbound or holding or already turning back, and none of it mattered now in the way it had mattered an hour ago.

He put the .38 in his pocket. He looked at the whittled bird, the rough wood, the unfinished wings. Coyle had been working on it in the dark, in the cold, with a fever climbing through him and his leg going bad beneath him, and he had been making something. Archer did not know what to do with that. He put the bird in his breast pocket.

Then he looked northeast, toward the karst, toward the limestone ridges rising gray and absolute above the jungle, and he understood with complete clarity that the mission had failed in the one way that could not be walked back. Not the fall from the penetrator. Not the firefights. Not the wound in his shoulder that he had been not-thinking about for hours. This.

He keyed the radio.

"Bird Dog, Two-Two Bravo. What's your fuel state."
