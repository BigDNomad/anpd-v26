The Jolly Green came in low over the tree line and settled into the clearing with the rotors hammering the elephant grass flat in every direction. Vance was at the door before the skids touched, one hand on the frame, scanning the tree line. Archer came out of the jungle at a jog, head down against the rotor wash, and Vance reached out and grabbed his arm and pulled him toward the bird.

Archer stopped.

He reached into his shirt and came out with the cipher rotors wrapped in the oilcloth and held them out to Vance.

Vance took them. He looked at Archer. He looked at the rotors in his hand. He looked back at Archer.

"Get on the bird," Vance said.

"Coyle's still in there."

"Archer."

"He's alive. I had eyes on him." Archer's voice was flat, the way a man's voice gets when he's already made the decision and is just informing you of it. "I'm not leaving without him."

Vance stood there with the cipher rotors in his hand and the rotor wash beating at both of them and he did not say anything for two full beats. What moved across his face was not readable as anything in particular. It was not anger exactly, though it was close to that. It was something that lived in the same neighborhood as anger but was not quite anger.

"The mission is the rotors," Vance said.

"The mission is Coyle."

"The mission—"

"The mission is Coyle," Archer said again. Same tone. Same flat delivery. Not arguing. Just stating the thing that was true.

Vance looked at him. The rotors overhead were loud enough that they had to lean toward each other to be heard, and they were both leaning, and neither of them was moving.

Then Archer reached out and took the GAU-5/A off Vance's shoulder. Vance let him take it. Archer stripped the spare magazines from Vance's chest rig, two of them, and took the two fragmentation grenades clipped to his harness. He did all of this without asking and Vance did not stop him. Then Archer turned and walked back toward the tree line.

He did not look back.

Vance stood there in the rotor wash with the cipher rotors in his hand and watched him go. The thing that had moved across his face a moment ago moved across it again, and this time it settled into something that was not quite pride and not quite anything else he had a name for. He did not act on it. He did not show it. He turned and walked back to the bird and handed the oilcloth package up to the pilot and said something the pilot had to lean down to hear.

The pilot looked at the tree line. He looked at Vance.

Vance was already moving back into the cargo bay.

He went to the onboard stores and pulled another GAU-5/A from the rack and checked the action and slung it. He pulled four spare magazines and a bandolier of loose rounds and two M18A1 Claymores in their canvas carriers. He found the night-vision equipment in the forward locker, two sets, AN/PVS-2 starlight scopes, and he slung both of them over one shoulder. He was moving the way a man moves when he has already decided and is just executing the decision, no wasted motion, nothing theatrical about it.

He dropped out of the bird and crossed the clearing at a walk.

Behind him the pilot was on the radio. Vance did not look back to see what the pilot was doing or what he was saying into the handset. It did not matter what the pilot was saying. What mattered was the tree line ahead of him and Archer somewhere inside it and Coyle somewhere beyond that.

The elephant grass was still bent flat from the rotor wash, and then it wasn't, and then Vance was at the edge of the trees. The jungle closed around him the way jungle does, all at once, the light going from gray to green to something darker than either. He could hear the Jolly Green behind him, the sound of it changing as it lifted, the rotors beating the air in a different register as it climbed. He did not look back at that either.

Archer had left sign. Not much, but enough. A bent stem, a boot print in the soft earth at the base of a teak root, the direction of disturbed leaf litter. Vance followed it.

The sound of the helicopter faded. The jungle sounds came back in around the edges of the silence the helicopter left behind — insects, something moving in the canopy, the distant call of a bird that had no name Vance knew.

He moved deeper into the trees.

The cipher rotors were on the bird. The bird was gone. What was left was the work, and the work was finding Coyle, and Vance moved through the green dark toward wherever Archer had gone.
