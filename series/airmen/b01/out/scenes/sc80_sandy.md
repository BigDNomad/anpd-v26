The call came in broken, the way most calls did over the karst, and Sandy had to ask for a repeat twice before he understood what he was hearing.

He clicked off the radio.

Outside the canopy the jungle ran green and brown and endless below him, the karst ridges cutting through it like the spines of something buried. He flew a slow orbit and said nothing for a moment. The Skyraider's engine turned over with its big reliable sound, the kind of sound that didn't care what you thought about anything.

He clicked back on.

"Say again, Jolly Green."

The pilot said it again. Both PJs. Still on the ground. Rotors had departed with the package — the cipher material, the thing the whole mission had been built around — but the two men who'd gone down the hoist were still in the jungle. By choice. They'd sent the bird home and stayed.

Sandy said something that would not have been appropriate for broadcast on a clear channel. He said it anyway.

Then he said, "Copy that."

He thought about it for another orbit. What they'd done was either the stupidest thing he'd heard in this war or something else entirely, and he'd been in this war long enough to know those two categories were not always as far apart as they looked. A man still alive in the jungle. Two PJs who'd decided that mattered more than the extraction window. He didn't say any of that. He just flew his orbit and thought it.

"Jolly Green, Sandy. Set down. Find yourself a clear area and hold. You are not to loiter over the search grid."

The pilot came back. "Sandy, we can maintain altitude and—"

"Negative. You set down and you hold. You're a big green machine with a rotor signature that carries two miles in this terrain. You stay airborne over that grid, you paint a picture for every Pathet Lao unit in the valley. Find a clearing and sit."

A pause. "Understood. Setting down."

Sandy keyed the second frequency. "Bird Dog, Sandy."

The FAC came back immediately, his O-1 somewhere lower and slower, threading the ridgelines the way only the Bird Dogs could.

"Bird Dog, I need you to break off from tracking their position."

Another pause, longer this time. "Sandy, I've got a rough fix on—"

"I know what you've got. Break off. Every transmission you make over their location is a beacon. You understand what I'm saying. Break off and hold clear."

The FAC said he understood. He said it the way a man says something when he understands it and doesn't like it and knows there's nothing to be done about it.

"Maintain your orbit well clear of the grid," Sandy said. "No transmissions unless I initiate. Radio silence over their position."

"Copy."

Sandy clicked off and flew.

The package was gone. The helicopter was down in some clearing to the south, rotors stopped, crew sitting in the heat waiting for a call that might not come. The FAC was pulled back, circling wide, his radio quiet. And somewhere in the jungle below — in the green tangle of it, in the karst shadow where the light was already going amber and thin — two men were moving on foot with no air cover and no extraction window and whatever they'd carried down the hoist.

There was nothing left to do operationally. Sandy understood this the way you understand things that are simply true, without comfort or argument. The package was the mission and the mission was done. What was happening now in the jungle was something else. It didn't have a name in the operations order. It didn't have a frequency assigned to it or a contingency plan written up at NKP. It was two men making a decision that the operations order hadn't anticipated because the operations order had been written by people who assumed the men following it would behave like the operations order expected.

He flew another orbit. The sun was dropping toward the western ridgeline and the shadows in the valley were lengthening, the jungle going darker in the low places first, the way it always did.

He thought about the two of them down there. The one who'd gone first, who'd left a trail for the other to follow. The one who'd followed it. He didn't let himself think too much about what the jungle looked like at night from inside it, without air cover, with Pathet Lao units working the valley. He'd been in enough of those situations to know that thinking about it didn't help anything.

What helped was staying on station. What helped was being there when the call came.

Sandy held his orbit. The Skyraider turned slow circles over the valley, its engine steady, its pilot watching the jungle go dark below him and waiting for a radio that stayed quiet.

All they could do was wait.
