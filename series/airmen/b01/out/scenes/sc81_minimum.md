The medic worked without ceremony. He was a small man, compact, with the flat affect of someone who had learned to keep his face out of his work. Soviet-trained, Coyle guessed — the technique had that quality, efficient and cold, the minimum necessary and nothing more. He cleaned the wound on Coyle's side with something that burned, pressed a field dressing against it, taped it down. He did not look at Coyle's face while he worked.

No morphine. No sulfa. The dressing was clean but it was not thorough, and Coyle could feel the heat in the wound that meant it needed more attention than it was getting. The medic knew that too. He was not incompetent. He was following instructions.

Coyle lay on his back on a bamboo platform that was not quite a cot, his right wrist secured to a support post with parachute cord, his left wrist tied to a lower strut with what looked like comms wire — thinner, and the knot was a field knot, not a rigger's knot. Someone had done it in a hurry or done it carelessly or both. The right wrist he couldn't move. The left had maybe two inches of play.

The medic moved to Coyle's left side to check the secondary wound on his forearm, and when he leaned in, his body shifted the angle of the wire, and Coyle felt the slack increase. Not much. Enough to notice.

He let his left hand go loose. Watched the medic's hands. The man was probing the forearm wound with two fingers, his attention on the tissue, and his weight was still pressing the wire down at an angle that kept the slack open.

Coyle manufactured the spasm. He let his left arm jerk, not hard, just enough — a convincing muscle response, the kind of thing that happened when a man had been lying still too long and his body reminded him of it. He made a sound, not a cry, just a controlled exhale, and said, "Can you shift that — the wire's cutting into the bone."

The medic looked at him. Looked at the wire. He reached down and adjusted the loop, pulling it slightly higher on Coyle's wrist, and in the moment of adjustment the wire went slack entirely, and Coyle's hand was almost free.

Almost.

A voice from across the space said something in Vietnamese. Quiet. Two sentences.

The medic stopped. He looked across the room — it was not a room, it was a covered platform open on three sides, bamboo and palm thatch — and then he looked back at Coyle's wrist. He pulled the wire tight. Retied it. Not a field knot this time. A proper knot, snug against the bone.

Coyle did not look toward the voice. He already knew where Tran was. He had placed him when they brought him in — against the far post, sitting on an ammunition crate, a notebook open on his knee. Tran had not moved. He had not raised his voice. He had simply watched, and then he had spoken, and the medic had corrected his work.

Coyle looked at the thatch above him. A gecko moved along one of the bamboo poles, stopped, was still.

He had thought Tran was the kind of officer who managed from a distance — who set conditions and let subordinates execute, who would not be watching the details because the details were beneath him. That had been a reasonable read. It had also been wrong.

Tran had not reacted to the spasm. He had not reacted to Coyle's request. He had watched the medic's hands, and he had seen the slack in the wire, and he had understood what Coyle was doing before the medic finished doing it. He had not said anything to Coyle. There was nothing to say to Coyle. He had simply corrected the error and gone back to his notebook.

That was the thing that mattered. Not the failed attempt — Coyle had expected to fail, the attempt was reconnaissance, not escape. What mattered was the quality of attention Tran was paying. The man was not distracted by the larger situation, by the search that was presumably happening somewhere in the jungle outside, by whatever operational pressure he was under. He was here, in this space, watching.

The medic finished. He packed his kit without looking at Coyle again, stood, and left.

Coyle tested the new knot with the smallest possible movement. It did not give. He stopped testing it.

The wound in his side pulsed with a steady, deep heat. His forearm was stiff. He was thirsty in a way that had moved past discomfort into something more structural, the kind of thirst that started to affect how a man thought.

He looked at the gecko on the bamboo pole. It had not moved.

The window he had seen was closed. He filed that, and he filed Tran, and he lay still and let his body rest because rest was the only resource he had left to accumulate.
