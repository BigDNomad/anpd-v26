The shape came through the undergrowth and Archer had the weapon up before he knew he'd moved, finger already taking the slack, and then Vance's face came through the dark and Archer let the breath out slow and lowered the muzzle.

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

"You're fast," Vance said.

"Don't do that."

"I followed your trail. You left enough of one."

Archer looked back the way he'd come, the way he'd been going, which was the same problem he'd been working on for the last twenty minutes. The jungle had closed in with the dark and what had been a direction was now just trees. He'd had a line on the camp — or thought he had — and then the karst had bent the terrain in a way the map hadn't suggested and now he had nothing. No sound from the camp. No light. No bearing he trusted.

"I'm turned around," he said.

Vance didn't make anything of it. "How far off?"

"I don't know. That's the problem." Archer crouched and Vance came down beside him. "I had a fix on the platform structure before dark. Lost it when the light went. I've been working the grid but the ground doesn't match what I had in my head."

Vance was quiet a moment. He'd spent time with the recon photos before insertion, longer than Archer had, going over the imagery with the kind of patience Archer didn't always have for that kind of work. Vance had a mind for terrain. He could hold a photograph in his head and walk it.

"You're north of where you want to be," Vance said. "The platform is southeast. Maybe two hundred meters, maybe two-fifty. There's a dry streambed between here and there — you'll cross it before you reach the perimeter."

"How confident?"

"Confident enough."

Archer looked southeast into the dark. Two hundred meters in this jungle was a long way. It was also a short way, depending on what was between you and the end of it.

"They'll have sentries out," he said.

"They'll have sentries out," Vance agreed.

That was the whole of the tactical problem and they both knew it. The camp knew something had happened — the helicopter, the shooting, the cipher material gone. They would be alert. They would be listening. And Coyle was in there somewhere, wounded, and the longer this took the worse that math got.

Archer stood. Vance reached into his kit and came out with a piece of equipment that Archer recognized by shape even in the dark.

"Where did you get those?"

"Pilot had them. He wasn't going to need them where he was going." Vance held the night-vision goggles out. "Sandy's asset. He left them in the aircraft when he punched out."

Archer took them. They were heavier than he expected, the AN/PVS-2 model, the kind that turned the world a washed-out green and gave you back your depth perception in the dark. He'd trained on them. He'd never used them in the field.

"These should help even the odds," Vance said.

Archer turned them over in his hands. The rubber eyecups were cracked at one edge but the housing looked intact. He'd find out when he powered them up.

"You're not coming," Archer said. It wasn't a question.

"One man's quieter than two. You know the work."

That was true. The hoist work, the penetrator, the medical assessment under fire — that was PJ work and Archer had done it more times than he could count. Getting a man off the ground and into the aircraft. Keeping him alive long enough to matter. That was the job. The rest of it — the jungle, the sentries, the two hundred meters of dark — that was just the part before the job.

"If I'm not back by—" Archer started.

"I'll know," Vance said.

The jungle held its sounds around them. Something moved in the canopy above, a shift of weight on a branch, and both men went still until it settled. A bird, probably. The jungle was full of things that weren't threats and a few things that were and the trick was knowing which was which before it mattered.

Archer fitted the goggles and powered them on. The world came up green and grainy, the trees resolving out of the dark into shapes he could read. Vance's face was a pale smear beside him, the whites of his eyes catching the ambient light.

"Southeast," Archer said.

"Southeast. Dry streambed first. Don't cross it standing up."

Archer checked the weapon. He looked at Vance once more — not long, just long enough — and then he turned and moved into the trees.

Behind him Vance settled back against the trunk of a dipterocarp and listened to the jungle close over the sound of Archer's passage. It took less time than it should have. Archer was good at this. He was very good at this.

The dark held everything equally and gave nothing back.
