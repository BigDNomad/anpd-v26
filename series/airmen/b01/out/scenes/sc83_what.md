They hit him again across the shoulders and he went forward against the rope and stayed there, breathing through his teeth. The rope held. It always held.

The one doing it was not Bounchanh. Bounchanh would have been louder about it, more personal. This was the other one, the one who gave instructions in Vietnamese and watched from the ammunition crate with his arms folded. The man with the stick worked without expression, the way a man works when the work is simply work.

Tran said something. The man with the stick stepped back.

The interpreter crouched in front of Coyle. He was young, maybe twenty, and he had the careful English of someone who had learned it from a textbook rather than from people.

"Where are the rotors," he said.

"Gone," Coyle said. "I told you. They're gone."

"You told us this."

"Because it's true."

The interpreter looked back at Tran. Tran said something short. The interpreter turned back to Coyle.

"He asks you to explain gone."

Coyle pulled in a breath. His side was bad. The forearm was worse in a different way, a deep throbbing that had moved up past the elbow and was working toward the shoulder. He tried not to think about what that meant.

"Someone took them," he said. "Out of the jungle. Before your people got to me. They're not here anymore. They're not in this country anymore, probably."

The interpreter translated. Tran listened without moving. When the interpreter finished, Tran was quiet for a moment, then spoke again.

"He asks who took them."

"I don't know his name."

"An American."

"Yes."

"A soldier."

"I don't know what he was. He had the rotors and he left. That's all I know."

The interpreter translated this. Tran stood up from the ammunition crate. He walked to Coyle and looked at him the way a man looks at a problem he has seen before, a problem with a known solution that simply requires patience to arrive at. He spoke to the interpreter without looking away from Coyle.

"He says your answer is convenient."

"It's the answer I have."

"He says a man with something to protect gives an answer like this. He says the rotors are nearby. He says you know where."

"I don't."

Tran spoke again.

"He says we will continue until you remember."

The man with the stick came back. Coyle set his jaw and looked at the bamboo overhead and tried to find something to hold onto up there in the dark. There was nothing. Just the bamboo and the dark beyond it and the sound of the jungle doing what the jungle does, indifferent to everything happening beneath it.

He thought about Archer. He had seen Archer go into the trees with the rotors in his hands, moving fast, moving like a man who knew where he was going. That had been real. That had happened. The helicopter had come and the helicopter had gone and whatever Archer had done with the rotors, he had done it, and the rotors were somewhere that was not here.

That was what Coyle believed. He believed it the way a man believes something he cannot verify, which is to say he believed it because the alternative was that Archer had failed, and Coyle had decided not to believe that. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

The stick came down across his back and he went forward again and the rope held and he made a sound he hadn't intended to make.

They gave him a minute. That was the method — not continuous, not frantic. Intervals. Time to think about the next one. Time to decide whether the answer was worth it.

He understood now that the truth was not going to help him. He had given them the truth and the truth read as evasion because the truth was convenient and convenient answers from prisoners are not answers, they are obstacles. Tran had been doing this long enough to know the difference between a man who was hiding something and a man who had nothing left to hide, and either Tran could not tell the difference in this case or he had decided it didn't matter. Both possibilities arrived at the same place.

The interpreter crouched in front of him again.

"Where are the rotors."

"Gone," Coyle said. His voice was steadier than he expected. "Out of your reach. I'm sorry. That's all I have."

The interpreter looked at him for a moment. Something moved across his face that might have been something other than procedure, some small recognition, and then it was gone and he stood up and spoke to Tran.

Tran listened. He looked at Coyle. He gave an instruction and the man with the stick moved and Coyle turned his face away and waited.

Outside, the jungle was dark and the dark was complete and somewhere in it, two hundred meters away or two hundred miles, it made no difference now, the night was doing what the night does.
