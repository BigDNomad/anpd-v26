They came in from the north, moving slow through the dark, using the dry streambed as a guide until it bent east and the ground rose. Vance stopped first. Archer came up beside him.

The camp was below them, maybe forty meters. Firelight through the bamboo. Two guards on the near side, one walking a loose perimeter, one standing at the corner post with his rifle across his chest. A third somewhere in the dark to the west — they'd heard him cough twice, tracked the sound. The covered platform was center-mass of the camp, elevated on posts, open on the south side. Lantern light inside. Shadows moving.

Archer watched the guard rotation for ten minutes. Vance counted the intervals, tapped Archer's arm twice when the walking guard reached his far point and turned back. They had maybe twelve seconds of dead angle on the east approach. Maybe.

Coyle was in there. They couldn't see him but they knew the platform, knew the structure from the sound of it — the creak of the boards when someone moved, the low voice of the interpreter carrying in the still air.

They pulled back.

The streambed gave them a natural withdrawal corridor, and they worked it in sections, Archer going ahead while Vance covered, then switching. At the first choke point — where the streambed narrowed between two limestone outcroppings — Archer set the first Claymore. He pressed the stake into the soft earth at the base of the left outcropping, oriented the face down the corridor, ran the wire back along the bank and coiled it under a flat rock. Vance marked the position in his head. They moved on.

The second mine went in at the bend where the streambed turned south and the bamboo thickened on both sides. Anyone coming fast through the dark would funnel right into it. Archer set it low, angled slightly upward. He was careful with the wire. His hands were steady.

They came back to a position thirty meters short of the camp perimeter and crouched in the shadow of a dipterocarp. Vance had the goggles. He glassed the camp once more, then lowered them.

"One way in," Archer said. It wasn't a question.

"East approach. Twelve seconds of angle, maybe less if the third guard moves up."

"I go in on the guard's far point. You hold here until I'm at the platform."

"And if it goes loud before you reach him."

"Then it goes loud."

Vance looked at the camp. The lantern inside the platform threw a yellow rectangle across the ground below the open south side. "His condition," he said.

"Don't know."

"If he can't walk."

"I carry him."

Vance didn't say anything to that. He knew what carrying a man out through that streambed would mean if the camp came up behind them. He knew the math.

"There are a lot of them," he said.

"Yeah."

"More than we've handled before."

"Yeah."

That was the whole of it. No argument, no revision of the numbers. The guards they'd counted, the ones they hadn't, Tran in there running the show — a man who didn't miss things, who caught what other men let slide. The odds were what they were and saying so didn't change them.

Archer checked the detonator clacker for the first Claymore, confirmed the connection, passed the second clacker to Vance. "You hold both until I'm clear of the corridor with him. Then you blow whatever's behind us."

"Understood."

"If I don't come out with him in ten minutes—"

"I'll be right behind you," Vance said.

Archer looked at him.

"I'm not sitting here with two clackers while you're in there," Vance said. "That's not what this is."

Archer didn't argue it. He'd known before he said it. He'd said it anyway because it needed saying, and Vance had answered it the way Archer expected him to, and now they were done with that part.

The jungle was quiet. Somewhere above the canopy the stars were out, but none of that light reached the ground. The dark was complete and the camp was forty meters away and Coyle was in there with whatever they'd done to him and the night wasn't getting any younger.

Archer checked his knife. Checked the survival weapon. He thought about the streambed and the two Claymores and the twelve seconds of dead angle and the weight of a man who might not be able to hold his own legs under him. He thought about the creed the way he always thought about it — not as words but as a direction, a heading, the way a compass needle doesn't argue with north.

He looked at Vance once. Vance gave a short nod.

They moved.
