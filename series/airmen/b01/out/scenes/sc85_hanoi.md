The field report came in on a runner, handwritten on thin paper, folded twice. Tran read it standing at the edge of the platform, the single lamp throwing his shadow long across the dirt. He read it once, then again, then folded it along its original creases and put it in his breast pocket.

He stood there a moment.

The report was from the General Reconnaissance Bureau, relayed through the regional command at Tchepone. It named Coyle by his full designation — rank, unit, assignment. It named the AC-119K program. It named the modifications to the fire control system and the targeting integration that had made the gunship runs along the Trail so precise, so punishing. It named Coyle as the engineer of those modifications.

Hanoi's directive was three sentences. The third sentence was underlined.

He is not to be killed. He is not to be traded. He is to be delivered to Hanoi intact.

Tran folded his hands behind his back and looked at the man on the platform. Coyle was sitting against the post, wrists bound, head down. The infected wounds had made him slow. He was still thinking, Tran could see that — the eyes moved even when the head didn't — but the body was losing ground.

It changed things. Not the man himself, who had not changed. But what the man represented had shifted entirely, the way a piece on a board shifts when you learn what it actually is. Tran had been operating under the assumption that Coyle was a cipher prize — valuable for what he carried, for the rotors, for the technical material. The rotors were gone now. The helicopter had lifted them out and there was nothing to be done about that.

But this.

The man who designed the modifications that had been killing Trail traffic for eight months. Who understood the integration between the fire control and the targeting array well enough to have built it. Who could, if properly questioned by people who knew what questions to ask, provide the technical architecture of the entire system.

That was not a cipher prize. That was a primary intelligence asset. That was worth more than the rotors.

Tran turned to the interpreter. "Tell him we are moving."

The interpreter crossed to Coyle and crouched down. He spoke quietly. Coyle raised his head and looked at the interpreter, then past him at Tran.

"Moving where," Coyle said.

The interpreter looked back at Tran.

"North," Tran said, in Vietnamese.

The interpreter told him. Coyle said nothing. He looked at the ground between his feet and his jaw moved slightly, working something over, and then he stopped.

Tran called to the sergeant at the platform steps. He gave the order in three short sentences. The sergeant went down the steps at a jog.

The camp began to come apart in the way that camps do when the order is given — not chaos, but a kind of organized dissolution, each man knowing his task, the tasks overlapping and completing in sequence. The cooking fire was killed. The radio equipment came down from its mount and went into a canvas case. The two guards on the near perimeter pulled back toward the vehicles, which were parked under the tree line to the north, and Tran could hear the engines turning over, one and then the other, rough at first in the night air and then settling.

The Pathet Lao moved differently than his own men. They were capable soldiers but they loaded with less discipline, talking when they shouldn't, the truck beds clanging with equipment that should have been stowed quietly. Tran noted it and said nothing. They were not his to correct. Their commander, the major, had his own authority here and Tran had learned to work within that boundary.

Two of his NVA detail came up the platform steps and took Coyle by the arms. They were not rough about it but they were not gentle either. They got him to his feet and walked him down the steps and across the dirt toward the lead truck. Coyle moved slowly. The leg was stiffening. He didn't resist.

Tran watched him go.

The column was forming along the track that ran north through the tree line. Three trucks, the lead vehicle carrying Coyle under guard in the cab, the other two loaded with troops. The Pathet Lao filled the truck beds, weapons across their knees, talking in low voices. Someone laughed at something and was told to be quiet.

Tran came down from the platform last. He walked the length of the column once, checking, and then got into the lead truck on the passenger side. Through the windshield he could see the track disappearing into the dark between the trees.

The border was two days north if the track held. If the weather held. If the Americans didn't find them before they crossed.

He looked at Coyle, who was sitting between two guards, hands bound in his lap, eyes forward.

The truck lurched and began to move.
