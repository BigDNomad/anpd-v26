The Skyraider came in low and fast from the south, below the ridgeline, and the first thing anyone in the column heard was the engine — that big Wright radial unwinding at full throttle, a sound like the world tearing along a seam. Then the rockets.

They walked down the column in pairs, each impact throwing dirt and fire thirty feet in the air, the concussions arriving a half-second behind the light. The lead truck took the third pair. The fuel tank went and the cab peeled open like a tin can, and the fire that came out of it was immediate and total.

Coyle was already moving when the first rocket hit. His hands were bound in front of him and his leg had gone mostly stiff but he threw himself off the running board and hit the ground hard on his shoulder and rolled into the ditch on the east side of the track. Tran was not in the truck. Tran had been standing at the passenger door, one hand on the frame, and when the rockets started he was simply gone — into the dark, into the tree line, no hesitation, no wasted motion. A man who had been through this before.

The second truck caught fire from the first. The canvas over the bed burned fast and the ammunition in the bed started cooking off, small-arms rounds popping in the heat, and the men who had been riding in back were running, some of them still on fire, into the jungle on both sides of the track.

Sandy came around for the second pass.

The guard towers at the camp perimeter were still standing when the napalm canisters came off the wings. They tumbled end over end, slow-looking against the dark sky, and then the tree line lit up orange and white and the towers came apart in the fire, the bamboo and thatch going instantly, the posts collapsing inward. One of the guards made it off the platform. The other one didn't.

The third truck had stopped when the driver saw the first two burning. The men in the back were already over the sides and into the jungle before Sandy came around again. The truck sat in the track with its engine running and nobody in it.

The camp — what had been, twenty minutes ago, an organized departure, weapons staged, prisoners secured, radio equipment cased and loaded — was burning chaos now. Men were shouting in Vietnamese and Lao, the voices cutting across each other, nobody in command of anything. The radio equipment was in the second truck and the second truck was on fire. The weapons that hadn't been loaded were still in the camp, and the men who'd been guarding them were gone.

Sandy made a third pass, lower this time, the cannon going. The 20mm walked through the camp from south to north, stitching the ground, and the covered bamboo platform took two rounds through the roof and the posts on the south side splintered and the whole structure listed and held, barely, on the remaining posts.

Coyle lay in the ditch with his face in the dirt and his bound hands over the back of his head. The heat from the burning trucks was real and close. His leg was useless for running. He knew that. He'd known it since they'd loaded him into the truck and he'd felt the joint lock up on the step. Whatever infection was in the wound had gotten into the tissue around the knee and the knee was not going to bend the way he needed it to.

He turned his head and looked south along the track. Fire everywhere. The tree line on both sides lit orange. Men moving in the jungle, shadows against the light, but moving away from the track, not toward it.

He thought about Archer. He thought about whether Archer was still in the jungle or whether Archer was on the helicopter with the rotors, which was where Archer should be, which was where any sane man would be. He thought about the fact that nobody had come for him yet and what that probably meant.

Sandy came around a fourth time and this time didn't fire. Just flew the length of the column low and slow, the big radial engine loud enough to feel in the chest, and then pulled up and banked south and was gone.

The burning trucks popped and settled. Somewhere in the jungle a man was calling out in Lao, the same phrase repeated, and nobody answered him.

The camp was not a camp anymore. It was a perimeter of fire with a listing platform at the center and three burning vehicles on the track and no organized force left to move anything north. Whatever Tran had been building toward — the column, the border, Hanoi — it was broken apart now, scattered into the jungle and the dark.

Coyle got his back against the ditch wall and looked at the sky. The Skyraider was gone. The night was very quiet under the sound of the fires.

He was still here. That was something. He wasn't sure yet what it was something of.
