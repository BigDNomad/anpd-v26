The first truck was still cooking when they moved.

Archer went through the wire where the concussion had peeled it back, Vance two steps behind and then breaking left without a word. The smoke came off the burning column in thick rolling walls and the firelight threw everything orange and jumping. Archer pulled his balaclava up and moved.

A guard came out of the smoke at eight feet. He was looking back at the fire, his AK held loose at his side, and he never turned all the way around. Archer put him down and kept moving.

The NODs made the difference. Everything the fire didn't reach was black to the Pathet Lao, black to the NVA advisors running between the burning trucks trying to pull men back from the secondary explosions still cooking off in the column. To Archer it was green and gray and readable. He could see the shapes of men crouching behind cover that wasn't cover, men with their backs to him, men looking at the wrong problem.

He moved through them the way water moves through a broken dam. Not fast. Deliberate.

A second guard heard him and started to turn. Archer was already inside the man's reach. He used the Ka-Bar and the man went down without a sound that carried over the burning.

Vance had gone wide on the left flank. Archer caught a glimpse of him through the smoke — a shape moving low and steady along the tree line where the camp's vehicle track came in. Vance was working the perimeter, keeping anyone from coming back in behind them. They didn't need to talk about it. They'd talked about it before they ever hit the wire.

The camp was opening up in front of him now. Sandy's strike had broken the departure column before it could form, and what that meant was the camp's guts were still here, still laid out the way they'd been built. The trucks were supposed to be gone. The prisoners were supposed to be in those trucks. Instead the trucks were burning in the approach track and the camp was still a camp.

He could read it now. The command tent was the large canvas structure set back from the vehicle track, guy-wired to two trees, a field table visible through the open flap where someone had left a lantern burning. To the right of it, set apart, a smaller structure — not a tent but a frame of cut poles with a tarp stretched over it. A holding area. The kind of thing you build when you're not planning to stay long.

He angled toward it.

A burst of AK fire went through the air somewhere to his left — not aimed at him, aimed at the fire or at shadows or at nothing. Someone shouting in Lao. Another voice answering in Vietnamese, sharp and clipped. The two languages arguing with each other in the dark while the trucks burned.

Archer moved past a dead man he hadn't killed. One of Sandy's secondaries had caught him in the open. He stepped over the man and kept going.

The holding area was padlocked with a chain through the pole frame. He could see through the gap in the tarp — shapes inside, low to the ground. He put his shoulder against the frame and the poles weren't set deep and the whole thing shifted. He hit it again and the upright pulled free of the ground and the tarp sagged and he went through the gap.

Two men inside. One of them was trying to get up and couldn't. The other was already on his feet, hands bound in front of him, and he had something in those hands — a rock, a piece of wood — and he was ready to use it.

Archer pulled the balaclava down.

"PJ," he said. "Jolly Green."

The man with the rock didn't move for a moment. Then he lowered his hands.

"Coyle," the man said. His voice was wrecked. "Tom Coyle."

"Can you walk."

"Yes."

Archer looked at the man on the ground. He was moving but not getting up. Archer crouched and checked him — American, flight suit, one leg at an angle that said it wasn't going to hold weight.

"He goes on my back," Archer said. It wasn't a question.

He cut Coyle's wrists free and then the other man's. Outside the camp the fire was still going and the shouting was still going and somewhere in the dark Vance was working the perimeter and keeping it clear. They had maybe three minutes before someone organized enough to start a systematic search instead of reacting to the fire.

Archer got the injured man up and across his shoulders in a fireman's carry. The man made a sound but didn't cry out.

"Stay on my left," Archer told Coyle. "Don't stop for anything. Don't look back."

He moved back toward the wire the way he'd come in, through the smoke and the orange light and the chaos that Sandy had made for them, the burning trucks still giving off their secondaries into the Laotian dark.
