The strike was still working. Archer could hear it — the Skyraider's engine cycling through another pass, the crump of ordnance walking across the tree line north of the perimeter, secondary detonations going off in chains where the trucks had been. Fire lit the smoke from below and turned it orange. The whole north end of the camp was a problem that had nothing to do with him.

He moved south and east, staying low, using the smoke. The balaclava pulled heat against his face. Through the NODs the world was green and flat and full of moving shapes, none of them organized.

The column had never formed. He could see that now. Whatever order had existed before the first bomb hit was gone — men were scattered across the interior, some moving toward the fires, some moving away, some standing in the open looking at the sky with their weapons held wrong. A few were shouting. The Lao and Vietnamese ran together in the noise, neither language doing much good. Nobody was looking for two men in the smoke.

He passed between two tents, close enough to touch the canvas on his left. A man came around the far corner moving fast and Archer stepped into him and put the Ka-Bar in under the ribs and held him down until he stopped. No sound worth hearing. He left him there and kept moving.

The camp's layout was coming clear to him as he went. The command tent was set back from the center, larger than the others, a field lantern burning inside it that showed through the canvas as a dull yellow rectangle. There was a gap between it and the next structure east — a gap wide enough to move through without silhouette. The holding area was south of that, the tarp-covered structure he'd marked on the way in, set close to the tree line where the ground dropped off. Between him and it: maybe forty meters of open ground broken by a supply stack, a fire pit that wasn't lit, and whatever was moving in the dark near the east wire.

He stopped at the corner of a low structure — storage, by the look of it, corrugated metal sheeting over a timber frame — and watched the open ground for ten seconds. A man crossed it moving north, rifle slung, not looking. Another followed him. Both heading toward the fire. Archer waited until they were past the command tent and then moved.

He went straight across, not running, not slow. Running drew the eye. He moved like a man who belonged there, which was the only way to move in a camp that had lost its mind. The NODs showed him the ground ahead and he kept his head up and his hands loose and covered the distance without incident.

The supply stack gave him cover on the east side. He crouched behind it and looked at the holding area. Twelve meters. The tarp was still down on three sides. A guard had been posted at the near corner — he could see the shape of him, standing, not moving, facing the wrong direction. The man was watching the fires. Everyone was watching the fires.

Archer assessed the route. Straight line from the stack to the tarp's open side, using the guard's blind angle. The guard would have to turn almost completely around to see him coming. He wouldn't turn. The fires were too interesting.

He looked at the tarp and confirmed what he needed to confirm. Two shapes inside, one upright, one horizontal. The upright one was Coyle — he could tell by the way the man was sitting, knees drawn up, head forward, the posture of someone who had been in that position long enough to stop fighting it. The horizontal shape was the other man, the one with the leg. Neither of them was moving much.

Archer registered the distance, the angle, the guard's position, the gap in the tarp's near side. He registered the command tent behind him and the fire to the north and the fact that the Skyraider was still up there, still working, still keeping every armed man in this camp oriented toward the sky and the smoke and the burning trucks.

He did not stop moving. He pulled back along the supply stack and came around its south end and began working toward the tarp from a different angle, one that kept the stack between him and the command tent. The guard at the corner hadn't moved. The fires were still going. Somewhere to the north a secondary cooked off and sent a column of sparks up through the smoke and the men near the command tent shouted at each other in Vietnamese and one of them pointed at the sky.

Archer moved through the gap between the stack and the tree line, low and steady, and came up on the tarp's blind side.

Coyle was eight feet away. The other man was beside him.

The guard was still watching the fires.

Archer waited one breath, then two, and read the ground between himself and the tarp's open edge and found nothing in it that would make a sound.
