Tran had Coyle by the arm and shoulder, moving him fast across the open ground toward the truck at the camp's edge. Coyle was on his feet but barely — the broken femur made every step a controlled fall, and Tran was compensating, taking the weight, steering him through the smoke and the orange light thrown by the burning supply stack. They moved in a kind of lurching tandem, Tran's head down and turning, watching the perimeter.

Coyle didn't fight him. He couldn't. His leg was a column of white pain from hip to knee and the burns across his forearm had stiffened the skin so that even gripping Tran's jacket required concentration. He moved because Tran moved him. That was the sum of it.

The truck was a Soviet-built flatbed, canvas-sided, sitting at the northern edge of the camp where the track came out of the tree line. One of the few vehicles the air strike hadn't reached. Tran had the keys. He'd had them before the first bomb fell.

Archer came around the south side of the supply stack at a low run, the GAU-5/A up and tracking. Two Pathet Lao fighters were moving to cut the angle between the holding area and the truck — not organized, not coordinated, just men reacting to the chaos and moving toward the sound of something purposeful. Archer put a short burst into the near one and the man went down sideways. The second broke left and Archer tracked him and fired again and the man stopped running.

From the left flank, Vance opened up on a cluster of three soldiers who'd been trying to consolidate near the command tent. They scattered. One didn't make it to cover. Vance shifted his fire to the gap between the tent and the tree line, suppressing the ground, keeping anyone from forming up.

Tran heard the firing and pulled Coyle harder, and Coyle's bad leg buckled and Tran caught him and kept moving. The truck was forty meters. Thirty.

Archer saw them.

He saw Tran's silhouette through the NODs — the green-white shape of a man half-dragging another man, moving fast toward the vehicle. He angled left to cut the distance and a Pathet Lao soldier came out of the smoke directly in front of him and Archer drove the butt of the GAU-5/A into the man's face and stepped over him without breaking stride.

Twenty meters to the truck. Tran was pulling Coyle around the front of the flatbed toward the cab.

Archer raised the carbine and fired once.

The round caught Tran in the right shoulder, high and back, and the impact spun him a quarter turn and his grip on Coyle broke. Coyle went down hard on his good knee, both hands on the ground, and Tran staggered against the truck's front panel and caught himself on the headlight housing. He stood there for a moment, his right arm hanging, his breath coming in short hard pulls. He looked at Coyle on the ground. He looked at the tree line. He looked at the shape coming toward him through the smoke.

He let go of the headlight housing and walked into the dark at the camp's edge and was gone.

Archer reached Coyle and got a hand under his arm and pulled him upright. Coyle's weight came against him and Archer took it, shifted his grip, got his shoulder under Coyle's arm.

"I've got you," Archer said.

Coyle said something that wasn't words. His voice was still wrecked, the cords damaged, and what came out was low and broken. But he was moving. He put weight on his good leg and let Archer take the rest and they started toward the jungle perimeter.

Behind them the camp was burning in sections. Sandy made another pass to the north, the Skyraider's engine a hard snarl dropping low over the tree line, and the secondary detonations walked across the far end of the camp in a line of orange flashes. The light strobed across the ground and threw long shadows and then the darkness came back.

Vance was already at the tree line. He'd shifted position as soon as Archer reached Coyle, moving back through the brush to the edge of the jungle, and now he was down on one knee with the rifle up, watching the camp's open ground, covering the withdrawal. Nothing was pursuing. The camp had stopped being a military unit and become a collection of men trying to find each other in the smoke and the dark.

Archer moved Coyle into the brush and the jungle closed around them. The ground was uneven and the roots caught at Coyle's dragging foot and Archer adjusted his grip and kept moving. Coyle's breathing was ragged but steady. He was conscious. He was holding on.

Vance came in behind them and pulled the brush back into place and they were in the dark of the jungle with the camp's fires visible through the canopy in broken orange pieces, and the sound of the Skyraider circling, and the distant noise of men shouting in Lao.

Archer didn't stop moving.
