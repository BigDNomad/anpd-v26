The Skyraider came in low from the north, engine hammering, and Sandy saw them — two figures moving along the camp's southern edge, one upright and one not, working toward the tree line. He pulled the nose up and broke left, the ordnance already committed to the run but the vector wrong, and he let the rockets go wide into the open ground east of the holding area rather than walk them through where the men were moving.

He came around.

The camp below him was burning in three places now. The supply stack had gone up early and was still going, throwing orange light across the whole central ground. The command tent had caught from a secondary and the canvas was collapsing inward on itself, the field table and whatever maps and papers had been on it going with it. Sandy could see figures running — some toward the fires, some away from them, none of them moving with any coherence that looked like command.

He picked his next target.

There was a personnel concentration near the northern tree line, men who had come back from the truck column or never made it to it, milling in the light from the burning vehicles. Sandy rolled in and put the guns on them. The 20mm walked through the group and kept going and the men who had been standing were not standing anymore. He pulled off and climbed and came around again and this time he put rockets into the far side of the camp where the secondary structures were — the lean-tos and the covered storage that hadn't burned yet — and those went up clean, the secondary detonations telling him there had been ammunition or fuel cached there, something worth keeping, something worth losing.

The camp was not a camp anymore. It was a series of fires connected by darkness.

Below, at the tree line, Archer had Coyle moving.

Coyle's right leg was wrong and had been wrong since before Archer found him, the femur gone somewhere it wasn't supposed to be, and every step was a negotiation between what the body could do and what the situation required. Archer had him by the left arm across his own shoulders and they were moving in the way that men move when one of them cannot move correctly — lurching, compensating, the rhythm of it ugly and functional and nothing else.

The tree line was close. Twenty meters. Fifteen.

Behind them the camp burned. Sandy's last pass had taken something structural in the northern quadrant and the collapse of it sent a column of sparks up into the dark air and the light from it reached all the way to where they were, throwing their shadows long and thin across the ground ahead of them.

Archer did not look back.

Coyle made a sound that was not a word. His voice was what it was — the cords damaged, the sound that came out of him rough and low and not carrying far — but Archer heard it and understood it as acknowledgment, as the man still being present, still working the problem of moving forward.

They reached the trees.

The undergrowth took them and the firelight fell away behind the first line of vegetation and Archer kept moving, deeper, until the light was something seen through leaves rather than felt on skin. He found a depression in the ground, a fold in the terrain where a tree had come down and taken the earth with it, and he put Coyle down into it with his back against the root mass and his leg as straight as it was going to get.

Coyle's face was tight. He breathed through it.

Overhead, Sandy made another pass. The sound of the Skyraider's engine was different now — not the diving hammer of an attack run but the steadier note of a plane working a pattern, assessing, looking for what was left worth hitting. Then the guns again, brief, somewhere in the northern camp. Then quiet except for the fires.

From where Archer crouched he could see the camp through the gap in the canopy. The command tent was gone. The supply stack was a low red glow. The holding area — the tarp structure where Coyle had been, where the other man still was — was intact, untouched, the tarp still standing in the middle of everything that had burned around it. He noted that. He filed it.

The Pathet Lao fighters who were still alive were not organized. He could see movement at the camp's edges, individuals and pairs, no one giving orders that anyone was following. Whatever Vorasak had built here in terms of command structure, Sandy had taken it apart. The men who remained were soldiers without a formation, which made them dangerous in the way that frightened men are dangerous and less dangerous in every other way.

Archer checked Coyle. Checked the tree line behind them. Checked the camp again.

Vance was somewhere to the east, covering the withdrawal. Tran was somewhere in the dark, wounded and moving, and that was a problem that would not stay solved.

The fires burned. Sandy circled.
