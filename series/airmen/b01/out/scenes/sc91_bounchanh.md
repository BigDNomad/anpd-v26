Bounchanh came out of the burning supply stack at a run, low and angled, the AK-47 tight against his shoulder. Something detonated behind him — a secondary, ammunition cooking off in the heat — and the concussion pushed him forward two steps. He kept his feet.

The camp was gone. Not destroyed, not damaged. Gone. The command tent was a frame of burning poles. The trucks at the northern edge were twisted metal and fire. Men ran in every direction that was not toward anything, just away, and the Skyraider was still up there, still circling, the engine sound dropping and rising as it banked.

He had been at the supply stack when the first strike came in. He had been checking the manifest, which was a thing he did himself because his quartermaster was a thief and he had not yet decided what to do about that. The first rocket hit forty meters north and he was already moving before the sound reached him, already reading the angle of the strike, already calculating. He had been in the jungle since he was seventeen years old. He knew what incoming looked like.

What he had not calculated was the American on the ground.

He understood it now, moving through the burning camp, stepping over a man who was not going to get up. The air strike was cover. The air strike was noise and fire and the death of organization. Someone had come in behind it.

The holding area tarp was empty. He had gone there first, before anything else, and found the corner post knocked down and the interior vacant and a dead man at the entrance he did not recognize as one of his own until he turned the body with his boot. One of the guards. The other guard was not there at all.

He felt the fury come up clean and cold, the way it always came, without heat, without confusion. Just the fact of it.

He moved north through the camp, toward the men who were running.

The first soldier he reached was a boy from Savannakhet who had been with him eight months. The boy was moving toward the tree line at the eastern edge, rifle gone, hands empty. Bounchanh caught him by the collar and spun him around and put the muzzle of the AK-47 against his sternum.

He said, in Lao, that the boy had two choices. He could go into the jungle alone and unarmed and explain to whatever found him in the dark why he had run. Or he could stand here and be a soldier.

The boy stood.

He found four more that way, moving through the smoke and the burning debris, pulling men out of their panic by force when rank alone failed. One man he had to hit. Not with the rifle — with his fist, open-handed, hard across the face, the way you brought a man back from shock. The man blinked and looked at him and Bounchanh pointed south and the man went south.

He had seven men by the time he reached the southern perimeter. Seven out of what had been thirty-two. He did not count the NVA — Tran's people were Tran's problem, and Tran was gone, which was its own problem for another time.

Seven was enough.

He knew where they had gone. The holding area was at the southern end of the camp and the tree line was forty meters beyond it and there was only one depression in the ground between here and there, a natural fold in the terrain where a fallen tree had come down across a shallow wash. He had walked that ground himself. He knew it the way he knew his own hands.

The American had used the depression. Had to have. There was no other cover.

He pushed his men into a line at the southern edge of the camp, the burning structures at their backs throwing enough light to see by. He looked at their faces. Some of them were still not right, still somewhere between soldier and animal, the fear not fully out of them. He let them see his face. He let them see that he was not afraid, that he had never been afraid, that the burning camp behind them was a problem to be solved and not a catastrophe to be survived.

Then he turned and faced the tree line.

The Skyraider made another pass to the north, working the road, and the sound of it faded. The fires crackled. Somewhere in the darkness a man was moving through the jungle with a prisoner who could not walk, and Bounchanh had been on this ground for two years, and the American had been on it for one night.

He took the lead himself. Did not point a man forward, did not send scouts. This one he would take himself.

He pushed into the tree line at a walk, AK-47 forward, and the jungle closed around him and his seven men, and the burning camp fell away behind them, and he moved through the dark without a light because he did not need one.
