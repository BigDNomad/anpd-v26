They ran hard through the dark, Coyle between them, his arm across Archer's shoulders and his bad leg dragging. The jungle closed behind them. Somewhere back in the trees a man was shouting and another was answering and the voices were getting closer.

Vance said nothing. He was running on Coyle's left side and his breathing was controlled and even and he kept his eyes moving. The NODs were up on his helmet now, useless under the canopy where the ambient light from the burning camp didn't reach. They were running on memory and feel, on the corridor they'd walked in their heads a dozen times before the op.

Archer felt Coyle's weight shift and caught him. The man's leg was bad and getting worse and every root and depression in the ground cost him. He wasn't making a sound about it. That was something.

Vance touched Archer's arm once, a single tap, and peeled off to the right without a word.

Archer kept moving. He had Coyle's weight and he took it and he moved through the dark with the sounds of pursuit behind him, branches breaking, men calling to each other in Lao, the occasional muzzle flash strobing through the trees back toward the camp. They weren't shooting at anything yet. They were just shooting.

Bounchanh's men came through the tree line at a run, seven of them spread in a loose line, and they were moving fast and they knew this ground. That was the problem with a man who'd been somewhere two years. He knew where the depression was. He knew where the fallen tree was. He knew the ground the way you know a room you've slept in all your life, in the dark, without thinking.

Vance went down behind a rotting log and got his weapon up and started shooting.

The burst caught two of them and the line broke apart, men going flat or diving sideways, and the shouting changed character. Vance put two more bursts into the dark where the muzzle flashes were answering him and then he was up and running again, moving hard and low, cutting back toward the corridor.

Archer heard it all behind him. He didn't look back. He had Coyle and he had the corridor and he had the clacker in his left hand, the wire running back through the dark to where they'd planted the mine two nights before, the wire laid out along the ground under the leaf litter, running back forty meters to the Claymore they'd set in the fork of a banyan root at knee height, facing north, facing the direction any pursuit would come from.

He'd counted the paces. He knew where he was.

Vance came up on his left, breathing harder now, and said, "Go."

Archer stopped. He turned. He could hear them coming, the Pathet Lao moving fast through the brush, Bounchanh pushing them, and he could hear Bounchanh's voice above the others, steady and hard, not panicked, just a man who knew his business telling his men what to do.

He waited.

The sounds got louder. A muzzle flash, close, and the round went wide and high and clipped something in the canopy above them. Coyle had gone down to one knee and he was holding himself up with one hand on the ground and he wasn't looking at Archer. He was looking at the dark.

Vance was clear. Archer knew it without checking. Vance was clear because Vance had said go and Vance didn't say things he didn't mean.

He squeezed the clacker three times.

The Claymore went off with a flat, hard crack that was nothing like the movies, nothing like the air strikes, just a sharp mechanical sound and then the ball bearings going through the brush like a hand sweeping a table clean. Seven hundred steel balls at four thousand feet per second and the kill zone was sixty degrees wide and the Pathet Lao were in it.

The jungle went quiet.

Not entirely quiet. There was a man making a sound somewhere in the dark, a low sound, and there was the creak of something settling, a branch or a body, and there was the distant sound of the Skyraider still working the road to the north, Sandy's engine a steady drone above it all.

Six men. Archer didn't know the number yet but he would know it later, when the count came back through channels, and the number would be six.

Bounchanh was not among them.

Archer didn't know that either. Not yet.

He got Coyle up and they moved. The corridor was ahead of them, the extraction point beyond it, and the Jolly Green was out there somewhere in the dark over Thailand waiting for the call. Vance was already moving and Archer followed and Coyle came with him, one arm across Archer's shoulders, his bad leg dragging through the leaves, not making a sound about it.

Behind them the jungle held its dead and said nothing.
