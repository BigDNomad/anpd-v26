The jungle behind them was moving again.

Vance heard it before he saw anything — the brush-snap and shuffle of men trying to move fast and quiet at the same time, doing neither well. He touched Archer's arm and pointed back down the corridor. Archer nodded and kept moving with Coyle.

Vance dropped back.

He found a root mass off the left side of the trail and got behind it, rifle up, and waited. The NODs were with Archer. Vance was working on sound and the faint ambient light that filtered through the canopy from a sky that wasn't quite dark anymore. He could make out shapes. That was enough.

They came through in a loose cluster, four or five of them, moving fast and careless now, the way men move when they think they're close. Vance let them come. When the lead man was twenty meters out he opened up, three-round bursts, walking the fire across the trail. The lead man went down. The ones behind him scattered left and right into the brush and Vance heard them shouting to each other in Lao, the words sharp and urgent.

He fired twice more into the brush where the movement was, then pulled back.

Ahead of him Archer had the radio up. Vance could hear the static and the clipped exchange as he moved.

"Sandy, Two-Two. We've got pursuit element on our six, maybe thirty meters back. Request immediate strafing run, north to south along our egress corridor. We are clear to the south."

The response came back broken, then clear. "Two-Two, Sandy. Copy. Rolling in."

Vance caught up to Archer and they pushed Coyle between them, moving hard. Coyle's leg was bad and getting worse — he was dragging it now, putting weight on Archer's shoulder with every other step. They got another forty meters before the Skyraider came in.

The A-1 came low and fast, the engine sound building from a distant rumble to something that filled the whole jungle in about three seconds. The strafing run tore through the canopy behind them, the 20mm rounds walking a line through the undergrowth with a sound like a zipper being pulled through sheet metal. Then it was past, the engine fading north, and the jungle went quiet in the way it does after something very loud has happened.

Vance stopped.

"Hold here," he said.

Archer looked at him.

"The second one," Vance said.

Archer understood. He pulled Coyle to the side of the trail and got him down against a tree. Vance moved back up the corridor, counting his steps. The Claymore was pre-positioned sixty meters back from their current position, set in the fork of a banyan root at knee height, facing north. He'd placed it himself. He knew exactly where it was.

He found the wire and followed it to the clacker and crouched there in the dark and listened.

The strafing run had pushed them. He could hear it — the survivors of the pursuit element moving through the brush, pushed south by the Skyraider's pass, funneled back toward the trail by the density of the undergrowth on either side. They were moving fast and they were moving scared and they were moving right down the corridor.

He waited until the sound was close enough that he could hear individual footsteps, individual voices.

Then he squeezed the clacker.

The Claymore detonated with a flat hard crack and seven hundred steel balls went north at twenty-eight hundred feet per second across a sixty-degree arc at knee height. The screaming started immediately and then stopped, most of it. What was left was a sound that wasn't quite human and then that stopped too.

Vance set the clacker down and moved back to Archer and Coyle.

The jungle behind them went very quiet.

---

Bounchanh was on his stomach in the undergrowth thirty meters east of the trail when the second mine went off. He felt the concussion in his chest. He lay still and listened to what was left of his pursuit element and did not move for a long moment.

Then he got up.

He had three men still with him. He could see two of them from where he stood. The third was somewhere to his left, not moving. Bounchanh looked at the two men and they looked back at him with the faces of men who had decided something.

He raised his rifle and pointed it at the nearer one.

"Move," he said.

The man looked at the rifle and then at the darkness ahead of them and then back at the rifle. He moved. The second man followed without being told.

Bounchanh came behind them, pushing them forward with his presence and the rifle and the fact that he had survived two Claymore mines and a strafing run in the same night and was still moving. That counted for something. It counted for enough.

The jungle closed around them and they went south.
