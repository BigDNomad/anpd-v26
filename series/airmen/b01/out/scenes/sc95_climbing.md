The ground fire opened up the moment Dalton broke hover.

Rounds came up through the belly of the aircraft before the skids had cleared the canopy — a sound like gravel thrown against sheet metal, fast and irregular, and then a harder impact somewhere aft that rang through the airframe. Dalton felt it in his hands before he heard it. He pushed the collective and the Jolly Green climbed, slow and heavy, trading airspeed for altitude the way you had to when the bird was loaded and the air was thin and men on the ground wanted you dead.

"Taking hits," Sparky said from behind him. "Fuselage, aft of the cabin. Nothing critical."

Another burst. Two rounds, maybe three, punching through the skin low on the starboard side.

"Still nothing critical," Sparky said.

Dalton kept climbing.

Evans had his eyes on the instruments, calling altitude and airspeed in a flat voice, the way he always did when things were bad — not louder, not faster, just steady. The ridgeline was ahead of them, dark against the early sky, and Dalton aimed for the lowest point he could find and pushed the bird toward it. The fire from below was walking up toward them. He could see the tracers now, orange lines curving up from the tree line, and he adjusted heading two degrees and kept climbing.

In the cabin Archer had Coyle's leg braced against the deck with his knee, keeping the fracture from moving while the aircraft bucked through the fire. Coyle's face was gray and his jaw was set and he wasn't making any sound. Vance was sitting up against the bulkhead with his hand pressed flat over the pressure dressing on his thigh, watching the rounds come through the fuselage skin like he was watching weather.

"You good," Archer said. It wasn't a question.

"I'm good," Vance said.

Meat was at the hoist housing, braced against the frame, watching the cabin. There was nothing for him to do now except stay out of the way and not fall over when the aircraft moved. He watched Archer hold Coyle's leg and he watched Vance's hand on the dressing and he watched the two small holes that appeared in the fuselage skin near the deck, daylight showing through them, and he didn't say anything.

The tracers dropped off. The ridgeline passed under them and the terrain rose behind and the ground fire lost its angle and then it was gone.

Dalton let out a breath he hadn't known he was holding.

"Clear the ridge," Evans said.

"Clear," Dalton said.

The Jolly Green leveled and the noise in the cabin changed — the sharp irregular percussion of rounds hitting metal replaced by the steady mechanical roar of the rotors and the engines working, the sound of a damaged aircraft flying west. Dalton ran his eyes across the instruments. Nothing in the red. The bird was hurt but it was flying and it was going to keep flying.

Sandy came up on the radio.

"Jolly Green Two-Two, Sandy. I've got you visual. Package is clear. How are you reading me."

"Sandy, Two-Two. Reading you five by five. We're clear the ridge, heading two-seven-zero. Bird took some hits, fuselage only, systems nominal."

"Copy that. Bird Dog is with me. We'll take your flanks. Egress route is direct to the Mekong, stay low until you're past the karst. I'll call the handoff."

"Two-Two copies."

The Skyraider came up on the left side, close enough that Dalton could see the silhouette of it against the sky, the big radial engine and the bent wings and the ordnance stations empty now. The Bird Dog was somewhere above and behind, the little O-1 invisible in the gray light. Dalton didn't need to see it. He knew it was there.

They flew west.

The karst came up on either side of them, the limestone formations rising out of the jungle like broken teeth, and Dalton threaded the Jolly Green between them, staying low the way Sandy had said, keeping the terrain between the aircraft and anything that might still be looking for them. The light was coming up now, the sky going from black to gray to something that was almost blue, and the jungle below them was a dark mass without detail, without movement, without any sign that men had died in it.

Archer sat on the deck with his back against the bulkhead and Coyle's leg across his lap and he looked out through the open door at the jungle going past below them. He was very tired. The kind of tired that sits in the bones and doesn't move when you shift your weight. He looked at Coyle and Coyle looked back at him and neither of them said anything because there wasn't anything to say yet.

Sandy called the egress clear at the Mekong.

The river appeared below them, wide and brown and moving, and then they were across it and the ground beneath them was Thailand and the threat envelope was behind them and the rescue package held its formation and flew on toward NKP in the early morning light.
