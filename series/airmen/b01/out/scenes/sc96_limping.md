The Jolly Green ran west with holes in her skin.

Dalton held the heading and didn't talk about it. Evans watched the instruments the way a man watches something he can't fix, steady and without expression, calling out numbers when numbers needed calling. The gauges that mattered were in the green or close enough. The ones that weren't, weren't critical. The bird had taken what it had taken and it was still flying, and that was the conversation nobody was having.

Below, the karst gave way to lower ground, the jungle canopy thinning as the terrain flattened toward the river. The Mekong was out there somewhere ahead of them, and past it, Thailand, and past that, NKP and a runway and people who would take Coyle off their hands and do what needed doing. That was the shape of the next hour. Nobody said it out loud.

In the cabin the adrenaline was gone.

It left the way it always left — not gradually but all at once, like a switch thrown, and the men who had been moving and firing and hauling and cutting were now simply sitting, hands loose in their laps, backs against the bulkhead or the hoist housing or whatever was there to lean against. Sparky had his eyes open but wasn't looking at anything. Meat was braced in his position near the hoist housing, forearms on his knees, head down. The noise of the rotors was the same noise it had always been and it filled everything and nobody tried to talk over it.

Vance was working.

He was on his knees on the cabin deck beside Coyle, and he had been on his knees since they lifted off, and he did not stop. The leg was the problem — the fracture had worsened in the corridor, the foot turned at an angle that wasn't right, and Vance was assessing it with his hands the way a man reads something in a language he knows well, methodically, without hurry. He checked the pulse at the ankle. He checked the color. He adjusted the improvised splint and checked it again. Coyle lay on his back with his jaw set and his eyes on the ceiling of the cabin, not making noise, which was its own kind of discipline.

Vance's left thigh was wrapped in the pressure dressing. The dressing had a dark spot on it that was larger than it had been at the LZ. Not much larger. Enough to notice.

Archer noticed.

He was sitting on the deck a foot away, handing off what Vance needed — the roll of tape, the folded gauze, holding the flashlight at the angle Vance indicated with a look. He watched Vance work through the leg assessment without once reaching for his own thigh, without shifting his weight off it, without acknowledging it was there. The wound was there. Vance simply declined to let it be relevant.

"Pulse is okay," Vance said. He wasn't talking to Archer. He was talking to himself, or to Coyle, or to no one. "Circulation's okay."

Coyle moved his jaw. What came out wasn't words exactly, just the shape of a question.

"You're going to be fine," Vance said. "Leg's going to need work. You're going to be fine."

Coyle closed his eyes.

Archer handed Vance the tape without being asked. Vance took it and kept working.

The weight of the last ninety-plus hours was not a thing anyone was going to name. It was in the way Sparky sat. It was in the way Meat hadn't moved from his position in ten minutes. It was in the small holes punched through the fuselage skin where the light came through in thin lines, the jungle air leaking in at altitude, cold now. The camp was behind them. The Claymores were behind them. The corridor through the dark was behind them, and Vance getting hit was behind them, and the napalm lighting the tree line was behind them. All of it was behind them and none of it was gone.

Sandy was off the left flank, the Skyraider's silhouette visible when the light caught it right. The Bird Dog was somewhere above and behind. The package was intact. The mission was not finished — Coyle was alive but he was not stable, and NKP was still ahead of them — but the shape of the thing had changed. The worst of it was behind them. The crew knew this. Nobody said it.

Evans called out a heading correction. Dalton adjusted without comment.

The Mekong came up below them, wide and brown in the early light, the surface moving the way rivers move, indifferent to everything above it. They crossed it and the air didn't change and the instruments didn't change and nothing marked the moment except that they were in Thailand now, and the jungle below was Thai jungle, and the war below was a different war, or no war at all, depending on who you asked.

Vance finished the assessment and sat back on his heels.

He looked at Coyle for a moment. Then he looked at the dressing on his own thigh, pressed two fingers against it, and went back to work.
