The lights of NKP came up through the windscreen and Dalton brought the Jolly Green down over the threshold, flaring at the last moment, the wheels finding the tarmac with a single hard thump that ran through the whole airframe. He held it there, collective down, and the rotors began their slow wind-down, the pitch dropping, the vibration easing out of the deck plates.

Before the ramp was fully open the medics were already moving.

A stretcher team came across the tarmac at a jog, two men on each side, a flight surgeon out front with a trauma bag over one shoulder. They didn't wait for the rotors to stop. They came up the ramp into the noise and the rotor wash and the smell of hydraulic fluid and cordite that had been baked into the cabin for the last hour, and they moved directly to Coyle.

Vance was still on his knees beside him.

He had been on his knees since the LZ. He had kept his hands on Coyle through the egress, through the ridge crossing, through the long run west over the Mekong and into Thai airspace, keeping pressure where it needed to be, watching the leg, watching the color in Coyle's face, talking to him when he needed talking to and staying quiet when he didn't. He had done the work the whole way. He had not stopped doing it.

Now the flight surgeon was beside him and a medic was taking Coyle's wrist for a pulse and another was cutting the makeshift splinting away to get a look at the leg, and Vance found that there was nothing left for him to do.

He sat back on his heels.

It wasn't a collapse. Nothing gave way. He just stopped holding the thing he had been holding, the way you set down a weight you've carried long enough that you've forgotten you're carrying it, and only know it's gone when your hands come up empty. The tension went out of his shoulders and his back and somewhere behind his sternum, and he sat there on the cabin deck with his hands on his thighs and watched the medical team work.

Archer was against the forward bulkhead. He had his eyes on Coyle and the medics both, tracking the handoff the way he tracked everything, not because there was anything left to do but because that was how he was built. His left arm was held close to his body. He didn't say anything.

The flight surgeon looked up from Coyle's leg and said something to the stretcher team and they moved into position. Coyle had his jaw set and his eyes open and he didn't make a sound when they transferred him to the stretcher, though the leg moved in a way it shouldn't have and the color left his face for a moment. They had him strapped and moving before the rotors had fully stopped.

Sparky was sitting against the port side of the cabin with his knees up and his head back against the fuselage skin. He watched the medics take Coyle down the ramp and across the tarmac toward the flight line building and then he looked at the overhead and didn't look at anything else.

Meat hadn't moved since the Mekong.

Dalton came back from the cockpit. He stood at the top of the ramp and looked at what was left of his crew and didn't say anything for a moment. Evans was behind him.

"Coyle's going to make it," Dalton said.

Nobody answered him. It wasn't that kind of silence.

Vance looked at his hands. The pressure dressing on his thigh had bled through and the tape had come loose at one corner and he would need to deal with that soon. A medic would come back for him. They always did. He would let them do what they needed to do and then he would sleep, and when he woke up he would find out what came next.

He thought about Archer on the penetrator. The moment the cable went taut and the jungle swallowed him and there was nothing to do but wait and watch the cable and listen to the radio and keep Coyle alive until the hoist came back up. He had not let himself think about it until now. There had been no room for it. Now there was room and he thought about it briefly and then he put it away.

Archer was still watching the tarmac where the stretcher team had gone.

Outside, the flight surgeon's team was moving at a controlled run toward the building, the stretcher between them, Coyle's arm hanging off one side before a medic tucked it back. The tarmac lights were very bright after the dark they'd come out of. A ground crewman with orange wands stood well clear, watching them pass.

The rotors ticked down into silence.

Vance put one hand on the deck and pushed himself to his feet. His leg held. He stood there a moment in the open ramp of the Jolly Green, the Thai night air coming in warm and flat and still, and then he walked down the ramp and onto the tarmac.
