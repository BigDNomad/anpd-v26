Archer found Vance on the tarmac, twenty feet from the helicopter's nose, standing with his weight on his good leg and his hands at his sides. The flight surgeon had cut the pressure dressing away and was working the thigh with gauze and tape, and Vance was letting him do it the way a man lets someone change a tire on his car — present but not involved.

Archer walked over. His left arm was still held close to his body, the elbow bent at the angle that hurt least.

"Silas," he said.

Vance looked at him.

"Thank you." Archer said it once, flat, and meant the whole of it — the hoist, the wait in the dark, the return. All of it.

The flight surgeon kept working. The tarmac lights threw long shadows across the concrete and the Jolly Green sat behind them with its rotors winding down, the sound dropping through registers until it was just a tick and a sigh.

Vance said, "You ever disobey a direct command again, I will personally see you reassigned to a desk in Saigon."

It was not ritual. The reprimand had weight behind it, the kind that comes from a man who has spent time in the dark not knowing whether the person he sent in is alive or dead, and who has had to keep moving anyway.

"Yes, Master," Archer said.

One beat of silence. The flight surgeon tore a length of tape with his teeth and pressed it flat against the gauze.

"You're damn right," Vance said.

He looked back down at the surgeon's work. Archer stood there a moment longer, then turned and walked back toward the helicopter.

What he understood, walking away, was that the reprimand and the rescue were the same thing. Vance had gone back in because Archer had gone in without authorization, and the anger was real, and the return was real, and they were not separate events. They were the same event expressed two different ways by the same man. Archer filed this without comment, the way you file a thing that is true and does not need to be said aloud to remain true.

The tarmac was busy in the way that airbases are busy at night — purposeful, low-voiced, lit by floods that turned everything flat and colorless. A jeep had come out to meet the stretcher. Coyle was already gone, the flight line building doors closed behind him, and somewhere past those doors a surgeon was being woken up or was already awake, already scrubbing, already thinking about the femur and what it would take to put it right.

Sparky was sitting on the helicopter's step, his helmet off, his head down. He had his hands between his knees and he was not moving.

Meat was still inside.

Archer climbed back up into the cabin. The overhead light was on, the dim red of it making the interior look like something underwater. Meat was in the same position he'd been in since the Mekong — back against the bulkhead, legs out, hands in his lap. His eyes were open. He was breathing.

Archer sat down across from him and said nothing. There was nothing to say that would do any work. Whatever Meat was moving through, he was moving through it at his own speed, and the only thing Archer could offer was to not make it worse by talking.

Outside, he could hear Vance's voice — short, clipped, answering a question from someone on the ground. Then boots on the tarmac, moving away.

The rotors had stopped completely now. The helicopter was just a machine sitting on concrete, cooling. The bullet holes in the fuselage skin were visible from inside if you looked — small ragged circles where the light from outside came through in thin lines, like the helicopter had been punched by something that didn't quite have the force to finish the job. Archer looked at them for a moment and then looked away.

His arm ached from the shoulder to the wrist. He would let someone look at it when there was time. There would be time.

Dalton appeared at the cabin door, leaning in with one hand on the frame. He looked at Meat, then at Archer.

"Flight surgeon's going to want to see you," Dalton said.

"I know it."

"Vance too, once he's done arguing with the debrief officer."

"There's a debrief officer."

"There's always a debrief officer," Dalton said. He looked at Meat again. "How long has he been like that."

"Since the river."

Dalton nodded. He didn't say anything else about it. He dropped down off the step and his boots hit the tarmac and then he was gone.

Archer leaned his head back against the bulkhead. The metal was cool through his hair. Above him the cabin ceiling was scuffed aluminum and wiring bundles and the smell of hydraulic fluid and old sweat, and outside the floods were burning white against the Thai night, and somewhere past the perimeter the jungle started again and ran east without stopping for a very long time.
