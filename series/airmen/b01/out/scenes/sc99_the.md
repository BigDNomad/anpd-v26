The field hospital was a long room with cots in two rows and a ceiling fan that turned without cooling anything. Coyle was in the third cot on the left, his right arm bandaged from shoulder to wrist, his left leg immobilized in a half-cast and wrapped tight with an ace bandage from knee to ankle. The leg was elevated on a folded blanket. He was awake when Archer came in.

Neither of them said anything for a moment. Archer pulled a metal chair from against the wall and sat down beside the cot. His left arm was in a sling now, the elbow bent at the angle that hurt least. He'd been cleaned up, the jungle mud gone, wearing a fresh flight suit someone had found for him.

Coyle looked at the ceiling. His face was the color of old paper.

"They tell you," Archer said.

Coyle nodded once. Not a question, not an answer. Just acknowledgment that the fact existed and had been received.

He would never fly again. The leg was part of it, the nerve damage was more of it, and there were other things the surgeon had said that Archer hadn't been present for. It didn't need to be discussed. Coyle knew it and Archer knew it and the knowing sat between them without requiring any particular handling.

The fan turned. Somewhere down the row a man was breathing in a way that suggested sleep or something close to it.

Coyle moved his right hand — the bandaged one, slowly — toward the edge of the cot. He reached under the thin mattress and came back with something and held it out.

Archer took it.

It was a piece of basswood, maybe four inches long, worked down from what had been a rectangular block. The shape of a bird was in it — the body roughed out, the wings suggested but not finished, the head present but without detail. The wood was pale and smooth where the knife had been, still raw at the unworked edges. It had been in his pocket through the camp and through the corridor and through the hoist and the Mekong crossing and the landing at NKP. It had been under the mattress through surgery prep and whatever came after.

Archer turned it in his good hand. The grain ran lengthwise through the body. Whoever had started it knew what they were doing — the proportions were right, the weight distribution was right, the bird was in there and had been in there from the beginning, waiting.

Coyle didn't explain it. Archer didn't ask.

There was nothing to ask. Ninety hours of jungle and karst and darkness and the smell of cordite and the sound of men dying in the dark, and now a quiet room with a ceiling fan and a piece of unfinished wood passing from one man's hand to another. The accounting for it would come later, in forms and debriefs and the particular bureaucratic language the Agency used to describe things that had happened in a country the United States was not fighting in. None of that was this.

Archer looked at the bird a moment longer. Then he closed his hand around it.

Coyle turned his head toward the window. Outside, the flight line was quiet. The Jolly Green sat on the tarmac with its rotor blades tied down, the fuselage holes still unpatched, the aluminum skin dimpled and torn in a dozen places. In the morning someone would come with a clipboard and count the damage and write numbers in boxes. Tonight it just sat there.

"Get some sleep," Archer said.

Coyle didn't answer. His eyes were already closing, or nearly. The medication was doing what medication does.

Archer stood. He put the chair back against the wall, careful not to scrape it. He walked to the door and stopped there with his hand on the frame and looked back once at the man in the cot — the bandaged arm, the immobilized leg, the face gone slack now toward sleep — and then he went out.

The night air outside was warm and flat. He walked along the edge of the building with the bird in his right hand, the fingers loose around it. The basswood was dry and light. The unfinished edges were slightly rough against his palm where the knife hadn't reached yet.

He stopped at the corner of the building and stood there in the dark. The flight line stretched away to his left. The Jolly Green sat where they'd left it. Beyond the tarmac the jungle was a black mass against a sky with too many stars.

He opened his hand and looked at the bird in the ambient light from the flight line. The body. The roughed wings. The head without a face. The grain running clean through the center of it, the way it would have run through whatever tree the block had come from, before any of this.

He closed his hand around it again and put it in his pocket.
